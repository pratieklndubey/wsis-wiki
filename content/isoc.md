# Internet Society (ISOC)

## Overview
The Internet Society (ISOC) is an international nonprofit organisation founded in 1992 to support the open development, evolution, and use of the Internet for the benefit of all people throughout the world. It serves as the organisational home of the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF), the principal body responsible for developing internet technical standards, and maintains a global membership of over 70,000 individuals and hundreds of organisational members.

ISOC was co-founded by Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn — two of the principal architects of the TCP/IP protocol suite that underlies the Internet — and has served since its founding as a bridge between the internet's technical community and its broader policy and governance dimensions. Headquartered in Reston, Virginia and Geneva, Switzerland, ISOC acts as an organisational home for IETF processes, a standards promoter (DNSSEC, IPv6, MANRS), and an advocacy body in internet governance debates — including WSIS and the [[internet-governance-forum]].

In the WSIS stakeholder framework, ISOC occupies a dual role: it is part of the "technical and academic community" stakeholder group as IETF's organisational umbrella, and it also acts as civil society on governance policy questions. This reflects the broader tension in internet governance between technical governance (where engineers and technical organisations are primary actors) and public policy governance (where states, civil society, and private sector interact).

## Detail

### Founding and Mission

ISOC emerged from recognition that as the internet transitioned from a U.S. academic/military network to a global infrastructure, it needed an independent organisational home to support its open evolution. The founding vision was "a professional society to facilitate, support, and promote the evolution and growth of the Internet as a global research communications infrastructure."

Vint Cerf served as ISOC's first president (1992–1995), followed by Lawrence Landweber (1995–1998). The founding of ISOC was closely connected to the formal establishment of the IETF as an ISOC administrative body — previously the IETF had operated under informal academic arrangements.

### IETF and Standards Role

ISOC serves as the organisational and financial home for three key internet standards bodies:

**Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF):**
- Primary body for developing internet technical standards (RFCs — Requests for Comments)
- Manages protocols including TCP/IP, HTTP, SMTP, DNS, TLS, and hundreds of others
- Operates on a rough-consensus, open-participation model
- In August 2018, ISOC formalised IETF's administrative structure as the "IETF Administration LLC"

**Internet Architecture Board (IAB):**
- Provides architectural oversight of IETF activities
- Appoints the IETF Chair; provides liaison to other standards organisations

**Internet Research Task Force (IRTF):**
- Longer-horizon research complementing IETF's operational standards work

Staff and volunteer count: 100+ staff; 4,099 volunteers working in IETF/IESG/IAB/IRTF roles.

### Key Programs and Initiatives

**Public Interest Registry (PIR):**
ISOC won the bid to operate the .ORG registry in 2002, transferring management from Verisign. PIR operates .ORG (the registry for nonprofits and NGOs), .NGO, and .ONG domains. In 2019, ISOC announced an attempted sale of PIR to Ethos Capital (a private equity firm) for $1.135 billion — a decision that provoked significant community opposition. In April 2020, ICANN rejected the transaction, and PIR remained under ISOC's control.

**Internet Hall of Fame:**
Established in 2012 to recognise individuals who have made extraordinary contributions to the development and advancement of the internet.

**Deploy360:**
A programme promoting operational deployment of internet technologies: initially focused on IPv6 and DNSSEC, later extended to MANRS (Mutually Agreed Norms for Routing Security) and DANE (DNS-based Authentication of Named Entities).

**MANRS (Mutually Agreed Norms for Routing Security):**
A community-led initiative to improve internet routing security by addressing BGP (Border Gateway Protocol) security vulnerabilities — the key infrastructure of inter-domain internet routing.

**World IPv6 Day/Launch:**
ISOC coordinated global IPv6 adoption testing (June 2011) and permanent IPv6 enablement (June 2012) by major content providers.

**Indigenous Connectivity Summit:**
Annual initiative from 2017 addressing connectivity challenges for Indigenous communities, particularly in North America, Australia, and Latin America.

**Africa Peering and Interconnection Forum (AfPIF):**
Annual forum promoting internet exchange points (IXPs) and interconnection in Africa, linking to WSIS Action Line C2.

### Internet Governance Roles

ISOC is one of the core "I* organisations" — the group of bodies with significant roles in internet coordination that includes [[icann]], [[rirs]] (via NRO), IETF, IAB, W3C, and others. ISOC:

- Participates in [[internet-governance-forum]] (IGF) as a key civil society/technical stakeholder
- Engages in WSIS processes, representing technical community perspectives
- Published the "Internet Invariants" and other policy frameworks for internet governance
- Conducts internet governance training and capacity-building globally

**Three governance models discussion (October 2020):**
ISOC led multi-round stakeholder dialogues on internet governance reform, discussing three models:
- Digital Commons Architecture (DCA)
- Distributed Co-Governance Architecture (CoGov)
- Reformed Internet Governance Forum (IGF+)

### ISOC in WSIS Context

ISOC's relationship to WSIS is complex:

1. **Technical community representative:** WSIS recognised four stakeholder groups; ISOC, alongside IETF and ICANN, constitutes the "technical community" pillar — helping ensure WSIS declarations reflect technical realities
2. **Civil society actor:** On governance policy questions (e.g., whether to transfer ICANN oversight to a UN body), ISOC has generally opposed expanded intergovernmental control, aligning with civil society advocates for open, decentralised governance
3. **Montevideo Statement (October 7, 2013):** Following Snowden revelations, ISOC co-signed the Montevideo Statement with ICANN, IETF, IAB, IRTF, [[rirs]], and W3C, expressing "strong concern" about internet fragmentation and surveillance, and calling for "accelerating the globalization of ICANN and IANA functions"

## Connections
- ISOC is the organisational home of IETF — the principal internet technical standards body — linking it to [[icann]] (DNS policy) and [[rirs]] (IP address policy)
- The "I* organisations" (ISOC, ICANN, IETF, IAB, NRO/[[rirs]], W3C) issued the Montevideo Statement (2013) calling for globalisation of ICANN/IANA
- ISOC participates in [[internet-governance-forum]] (IGF) as a key stakeholder
- ISOC engages in WSIS processes representing technical community; WSIS [[multistakeholder-model]] explicitly includes technical/academic community
- ISOC's advocacy for open internet standards relates to WSIS Action Line C6 ([[wsis-action-lines]]) on enabling environment
- [[internet-governance]] — ISOC shapes the open standards and multistakeholder character of internet governance

# C4: Capacity Building

## Overview
Action Line C4 is the human capital pillar of the WSIS framework, addressing the skills, knowledge, and capabilities that individuals and institutions need to participate in, create, and benefit from the Information Society. Infrastructure ([[c2]]) without skills is insufficient; access to information ([[c3]]) requires literacy to be meaningful. C4 bridges the gap between connectivity and meaningful use.

C4 has the broadest facilitating coalition of any Action Line — [[undp]], [[unesco]], [[itu]], and [[unctad]] are all co-facilitators — reflecting the cross-cutting nature of capacity building across ICT infrastructure, education, development programming, and trade. This makes C4 one of the most collaborative but also most complex Action Lines to coordinate.

The Geneva Plan of Action's vision for C4 was ambitious: universal ICT literacy, with attention to basic literacy as a prerequisite; gender equity in ICT education; distance learning for underserved communities; professional ICT training for the workforce; and research and development capacity to build local innovation ecosystems. By 2025, digital skills had become the defining challenge of the digital economy era, with AI literacy emerging as the new frontier — themes not anticipated in 2003 but now central to C4 facilitation.

## Detail

### Scope and Purpose

C4 addresses six major dimensions of human capacity:

**1. Basic literacy:** Recognising that ICT capacity building requires foundational literacy first. The [[geneva-plan-of-action]] called for programmes to combat illiteracy as a precondition for digital inclusion — making C4 directly linked to SDG 4 (Quality Education) at its most basic level.

**2. ICT professional training:** Developing the technical workforce that builds, operates, and maintains digital infrastructure and services. This includes tertiary education in computer science, engineering, and information systems; vocational training; and continuing professional development.

**3. E-literacy (digital skills for all):** The broader skills needed to navigate the digital environment — media literacy, online safety, data literacy, and the ability to evaluate online information. By 2025, this had expanded to encompass AI literacy.

**4. Gender equity in ICT education:** Women and girls remain underrepresented in ICT careers and training in most regions. C4 calls for gender-responsive training programmes, encouragement of girls in STEM, and equal access to ICT scholarships and professional development.

**5. Teacher training:** Equipping educators to use ICTs effectively in the classroom and to teach ICT skills. This directly links to the broader educational transformation enabled by [[c7]] e-learning.

**6. Research and development:** Building local R&D capacity, particularly in developing countries, to move from technology consumers to technology producers. This includes university-industry partnerships, innovation hubs, and science and technology parks.

### Key Geneva Plan of Action Provisions

The C4 section called for:
- Each country to develop at least two connected ICT training institutions per country
- ICT training to be integrated into teacher education
- Distance learning programmes using ICTs for inaccessible or remote communities
- Gender-sensitive ICT training programmes
- E-literacy campaigns aligned with lifelong learning frameworks
- Public-private partnerships for ICT professional training
- Support for ICT research and development in developing countries

### The Skills Gap: 20-Year Assessment

The global digital skills gap has widened alongside the growth of the digital economy. Key findings from the WSIS+20 preparatory process:
- By 2025, 54% of countries had digital skill standards integrated into school curricula (up from near-zero in 2003)
- The AI skills gap: demand for AI-literate workers far exceeds supply in virtually all countries
- Gender gap persists: women constitute only 26% of AI professionals globally (UNESCO/ITU data)
- LDC skills deficit: digital skills training infrastructure is weakest precisely where the digital divide is widest

ITU's academy (launched in the WSIS era) provides online capacity building for ITU member states. UNESCO's ICT in Education programme directly implements C4 provisions.

### SDG Connections

C4's broad mandate spans many SDG areas:
- **SDG 1.b** (Pro-poor and gender-sensitive development strategies): Digital skills as poverty reduction
- **SDG 4.7** (Education for sustainable development): ICT literacy as 21st-century competency
- **SDG 5.5, 5.b** (Women's full participation; technology empowerment): Gender in ICT training
- **SDG 17.9** (Enhance international support for capacity building in developing countries): ODA for digital skills
- **SDG 17.18** (Enhance capacity-building for data, monitoring): Statistical capacity

### WSIS+20 Mandate (2025)

[[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025) addressed capacity building explicitly:
- **Para. 18:** Reaffirmed C4 and all Action Lines as "the main framework"
- **Para. 36–41** (Digital skills and capacity building): Called for AI capacity building programmes; recognised gender gaps in STEM/ICT; welcomed ITU Academy and UNESCO digital skills initiatives; called for enhanced south-south knowledge sharing
- **Para. 84** (AI governance): Called for an AI capacity-building fellowship — a new C4 commitment
- **Para. 112:** Human rights and gender equality to be core themes within C4
- **Para. 113:** C4 facilitators to develop implementation roadmap by CSTD 2027

### Facilitation Arrangements

C4 is the most multi-agency Action Line after C7. The four co-facilitators — [[undp]], [[unesco]], [[itu]], [[unctad]] — each contribute from their programmatic mandates:
- **UNDP:** Capacity development programming in 177 countries; ICT for development projects
- **UNESCO:** ICT in education; teacher training; higher education; literacy programmes
- **ITU:** Technical training; ITU Academy; digital skills standards; AI readiness
- **UNCTAD:** ICT for trade capacity; developing-country ICT policy training; Digital Economy Report

The current ITU lead (Carla Licciardello, Senior Coordinator for Digital Skills) coordinates cross-agency facilitation, including the annual meeting at [[wsis-forum]].

## Open Questions
- The original C4 targets (two connected training institutions per country by 2005) were modest — has facilitation kept pace with the exponential growth in skills demand?
- AI literacy is now a critical dimension of digital capacity — will the 2027 implementation roadmap specifically address AI skills, and with what targets?
- How can C4 address the skills gap in LDCs when those countries also face the greatest shortages of qualified ICT trainers?
- Is the four-agency co-facilitation model for C4 efficient, or does it create coordination costs that reduce effectiveness?

# Working Group on Internet Governance (WGIG)

## Overview
The Working Group on Internet Governance (WGIG) was a UN multistakeholder advisory body established in 2004 to resolve the political impasse over internet governance that had deadlocked [[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]]. The first WSIS phase in Geneva (December 2003) failed to reach agreement on who should oversee [[icann]] and the internet's technical infrastructure, creating an urgent mandate for structured deliberation.

The WGIG was convened by the UN Secretary-General following a mandate in the Geneva Declaration of Principles, tasked with: developing a working definition of internet governance; identifying the relevant public policy issues; and clarifying the roles of governments, the private sector, civil society, and international organisations. Chaired by Nitin Desai and comprising 40 members drawn from all stakeholder groups and all regions, WGIG held four meetings between November 2004 and June 2005 and produced a landmark report that shaped all subsequent internet governance discourse.

The WGIG Report's most lasting contribution was its working definition of internet governance — still cited today — and the four proposed oversight models that it put before the second WSIS phase (Tunis, November 2005). Although no agreement was reached on oversight, the Tunis negotiations that built on the WGIG report resulted in the creation of the [[internet-governance-forum]] (IGF), which has served as the principal multistakeholder internet governance dialogue ever since.

## Key Facts
- **Created:** September 21, 2004 (by the UN Secretary-General, pursuant to WSIS Geneva mandate)
- **Chair:** Nitin Desai (former UN Under-Secretary-General for Economic and Social Affairs)
- **Executive Coordinator:** Markus Kummer
- **Membership:** 40 members (drawn from governments, private sector, civil society, and technical/academic community; all regions represented)
- **Mandate:** (1) Develop working definition of internet governance; (2) identify public policy issues; (3) clarify roles of stakeholders
- **Meetings:** 4 meetings held (November 2004; February 2005; April 2005; June 2005)
- **Report released:** July 18, 2005
- **Key outcome:** Four proposed oversight models; creation of [[internet-governance-forum]] at Tunis 2005
- **WGIG internet governance definition:** Still the standard definition used in UN contexts

## Detail

### Background: The Geneva Impasse

At [[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]], the governance of [[icann]] and internet infrastructure became the single most contentious issue. Many developing countries and the European Union argued that U.S. unilateral oversight of ICANN was inappropriate for a global resource. The U.S. and private sector favored retaining the existing multistakeholder model. No agreement was reached.

Paragraph 48 of the Geneva Declaration of Principles acknowledged "the need to find solutions to the issues arising from the use of the Internet" and called for the UN Secretary-General to establish a working group to undertake deliberations and produce proposals for action by WSIS Phase II in 2005.

### Mandate and Scope

The WGIG was specifically tasked to:
1. Develop a **working definition of internet governance**
2. Identify the **public policy issues** that are relevant to internet governance
3. **Develop a common understanding** of the respective roles and responsibilities of governments, existing intergovernmental and international organisations, and other forums, as well as the private sector and civil society

### Four Domains of Internet Governance

The WGIG Report identified and categorized internet governance issues into four domains:
1. **Infrastructure:** Domain Name System (DNS), IP addresses, and internet protocols
2. **Security, safety, and privacy:** Cybersecurity, spam, cybercrime, data protection
3. **Intellectual property and international trade:** Copyright, trademarks, e-commerce
4. **Development issues:** Digital divide, access in developing nations, multilingualism

### Working Definition

The WGIG Report produced a definition that remains the standard reference in UN contexts:

> "Internet governance is the development and application by Governments, the private sector and civil society, in their respective roles, of shared principles, norms, rules, decision-making procedures, and programmes that shape the evolution and use of the Internet."

This definition was significant for explicitly recognising three stakeholder categories — governments, private sector, and civil society — as co-producers of internet governance, rather than treating it as exclusively a technical or governmental matter.

### Four Proposed Oversight Models (July 18, 2005)

The WGIG Report proposed four models for internet governance oversight:

| Model | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Global Internet Council (GIC)** | Governments and stakeholders collectively assume U.S. oversight of ICANN; intergovernmental body with multistakeholder input |
| **Strengthened ICANN GAC** | Retain ICANN structure but formalise the Governmental Advisory Committee as the primary oversight forum; no new institutions |
| **International Internet Council (IIC)** | Remove U.S. oversight entirely; restrict ICANN to technical functions; new intergovernmental council for policy |
| **Three-body structure** | Create three separate entities: Global Internet Policy Council (GIPC) for policy; World Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (WICANN) for technical functions; Global Internet Governance Forum (GIGF) for dialogue |

No consensus emerged from the four models. The Tunis negotiations that followed produced a compromise: ICANN retained its structure (no model implemented), but the [[internet-governance-forum]] (IGF) was created as a new multistakeholder dialogue forum — effectively a partial implementation of the "forum" element common to several models.

### Significance

The WGIG's lasting contributions to internet governance are threefold:

1. **The working definition:** First internationally agreed definition of "internet governance," establishing that governance includes non-technical policy actors (governments, civil society) — not just engineers
2. **The multistakeholder framework:** WGIG's membership model (40 members from all sectors) was itself a demonstration of inclusive deliberation; influenced subsequent multistakeholder forum designs
3. **The IGF:** The WGIG's proposed "dialogue forum" concept, combined with Tunis negotiating dynamics, produced the Internet Governance Forum — the principal multistakeholder internet governance forum now operating annually

## Open Questions
- Why was no agreement reached on an oversight model if WGIG represented broad stakeholder consensus?
- Does the IGF, as a "non-binding, non-decisional" forum, represent a genuine implementation of WGIG's vision or a diluted compromise?
- Given the 2016 IANA transition, do any of the original WGIG oversight models remain relevant?
- Should a new WGIG-style process be convened following WSIS+20 to address AI governance and internet fragmentation?

# Digital Solidarity Fund

## Overview
The Digital Solidarity Fund (DSF) was a voluntary international financing mechanism created to mobilise resources for bridging the global digital divide, particularly for developing countries, least developed countries (LDCs), and small island developing states (SIDS). It was proposed at the first phase of the World Summit on the Information Society in Geneva (December 2003), established formally in 2005, and represents the WSIS community's most direct attempt to create dedicated financing for ICT development beyond traditional official development assistance (ODA).

The Fund emerged from the broader Digital Solidarity Agenda embedded in Section D of the [[geneva-plan-of-action]]. It was an innovative concept: rather than relying solely on government ODA or private-sector investment, the DSF proposed a voluntary contribution model — notably, one version of the proposal ("Geneva Principle") suggested that cities and local authorities purchasing ICT equipment would voluntarily donate a small percentage of the procurement contract value to the Fund. This model was championed by the city of Geneva and by African governments as a South-driven financing innovation.

The Digital Solidarity Fund was formally welcomed and legitimised in the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] (2005), which noted it as one component of the financing landscape alongside traditional ODA and public-private partnerships. However, the voluntary nature of contributions and the absence of a binding commitment mechanism limited its financial impact. The Fund was eventually wound down, though the concept of dedicated ICT-for-development financing it embodied continues to influence discussions around the [[global-digital-compact]] and [[sdgs]].

## Key Facts
- **Proposed:** Geneva Plan of Action, Section D (12 December 2003)
- **Formally established:** 2005, following the Tunis phase
- **Legal basis:** Geneva Plan of Action para. D; Tunis Agenda para. 28
- **Headquarters:** Geneva, Switzerland
- **Founding concept:** Voluntary contributions, including the "Geneva Principle" — a percentage of ICT procurement contracts donated to the Fund
- **Champion:** City of Geneva; African governments; developing country caucus at WSIS
- **Type:** Voluntary, non-binding international financing mechanism
- **Status:** Wound down; no longer operational
- **Related concept:** Partner2Connect digital coalition (a successor-spirit initiative under the [[itu]])

## Detail

### Origins: The Digital Solidarity Agenda

The Geneva Plan of Action Section D (Digital Solidarity Agenda) stated that "the market alone cannot ensure the full participation of developing countries in the information society" — framing digital solidarity as a political and ethical imperative, not merely a development finance technicality.

Section D called for:
1. **Integration of ICT strategies** into national development plans and Poverty Reduction Strategy Papers (PRSPs)
2. **ODA mainstreaming** — donors to increase the share of ICT in their assistance programmes
3. **Task Force on financial mechanisms** — established under the UN Secretary-General to review existing mechanisms for adequacy and suggest improvements (reported to Tunis phase)
4. **Voluntary Digital Solidarity Fund** — proposed as an innovative complement to existing mechanisms for developing countries and LDCs

The Fund was particularly championed by the city of Geneva and African states at WSIS Phase I. The "1% for digital solidarity" concept — whereby ICT procurement contracts would carry a 1% voluntary contribution to the fund — was celebrated as a South-led financing innovation, though it was never mandated.

### Tunis Phase: Legitimation and Limitations

The UN Secretary-General's Task Force on Financial Mechanisms (TFFM) reported to the Tunis phase in 2005. Its assessment was mixed: existing mechanisms (ODA, multilateral development bank loans, private investment) were generally assessed as adequate in volume, but poorly distributed and not specifically targeting ICT-for-development goals.

The [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] (2005, para. 28) welcomed the establishment of the Digital Solidarity Fund, noting it as one among several financing mechanisms. Crucially, it did not:
- Make contributions mandatory
- Set a funding target
- Establish a governance structure with enforcement powers

The [[tunis-commitment]] (para. 12) reaffirmed the importance of adequate resources for digital development, including ODA and innovative financing, without specifically mandating the DSF's capitalisation.

### Governance and Operation

The Digital Solidarity Fund operated from Geneva under a Foundation structure, with Adama Samassékou (former President of WSIS Phase I) as a prominent early champion. Its governance brought together:
- Municipal governments (led by the City of Geneva)
- National governments (primarily from Africa and Asia)
- Private sector contributors
- NGO observers

The "Geneva Principle" — voluntarily donating 1% of ICT procurement contract values — was adopted by some cities and local authorities, particularly in France and Switzerland, but never gained widespread adherence. Major technology corporations, which would have been the principal contributors under this model, largely did not participate.

### Impact and Wind-Down

The DSF was the first dedicated international fund specifically for ICT-for-development, but its voluntary architecture made it financially modest. Total contributions over its operational lifetime remained far below the hundreds of millions envisaged by proponents.

Key lessons drawn by evaluators:
- **Voluntary mechanisms without G7/donor-country commitment** cannot generate transformative financing flows
- **Procurement-linked contributions** are politically attractive but administratively complex and easily declined
- **Competition with established funds** (World Bank, regional development banks) and bilateral ODA reduced the DSF's perceived added value

The Fund was eventually wound down, with its conceptual legacy absorbed into broader ICT-for-development financing discussions under the SDG framework (particularly SDG 17 — means of implementation) and the GDC.

### Successor Concepts and Legacy

The DSF's spirit lives on in several successor initiatives:
- **Partner2Connect (P2C)** — a voluntary pledging platform launched by [[itu]] at WSIS Forum 2021; attracted $30+ billion in pledges from governments, private sector, and civil society for connectivity; avoids the procurement-levy model in favour of direct pledges
- **ITU Connect the World** — broader connectivity financing campaign
- **GDC Financing commitments (2024)** — the [[global-digital-compact]] calls for enhanced financing for digital development in LDCs and SIDS, echoing DSF's original purpose
- **WSIS+20 outcome [[unga-res-80-173]] (paras. 56–65)** — dedicated section on financing for digital development in developing countries; calls for innovative financing mechanisms and enhanced ODA for ICT

The core argument of the DSF — that the market alone is insufficient to bridge the digital divide and that dedicated solidarity-based mechanisms are needed — remains active in global digital policy discussions.

# C3: Access to Information and Knowledge

## Overview
Action Line C3 is one of the most expansive in the WSIS framework, addressing the fundamental right to access information and knowledge through ICTs. It covers open-source software, digital public libraries, community access points, public domain information, scientific publishing, and multilingual content — everything that determines whether the infrastructure of [[c2]] becomes a genuine tool for human development or simply a conduit for commercial content.

C3 is jointly facilitated by [[itu]] and [[unesco]], reflecting the intersection of technical access (ITU's domain) and cultural-knowledge dimensions (UNESCO's mandate). It is the only Action Line mapped to all 17 SDGs in the [[wsis-sdg-matrix]] (launched WSIS Forum 2015), on the grounds that access to information is a cross-cutting enabler of progress across every development goal.

C3 addresses a fundamental tension in the information society: the technical infrastructure exists to share knowledge universally, but intellectual property regimes, paywalls, language barriers, and content gaps prevent this potential from being realised. WSIS mandated a balanced approach — respecting intellectual property while maximising access to the public domain, publicly-funded research, and community-generated knowledge.

## Key Facts
- **Full name:** Access to Information and Knowledge
- **Code:** C3 (Action Line 3)
- **Origin:** [[geneva-plan-of-action]], Section C, para. C3 (adopted 12 December 2003)
- **Facilitating organisations:** [[itu]] / [[unesco]]
- **Current lead contact:** Marius Lukosiunas, UNESCO
- **Related SDGs:** All 17 (C3 is the only Action Line linked to all SDGs)
- **WSIS-SDG Matrix:** "The most broadly linked Action Line — information access is cross-cutting across all Goals"
- **Facilitation meetings:** 14 (2006–2021), held annually at [[wsis-forum]]
- **WSIS+20 mandate:** Implementation roadmap linking C3 to SDG targets and [[global-digital-compact]] commitments, due to CSTD 30th session (2027)

## Detail

### Scope and Purpose

C3 addresses five interconnected dimensions of information and knowledge access:

**1. Public domain information:** Government-held information as a public good; open government data initiatives; freedom of information legislation; access to official government documents and statistics online.

**2. Open-source and free software:** Software diversity as a means of reducing barriers to access; promotion of free and open-source software (FOSS) for developing countries; interoperability standards.

**3. Digital public libraries and archives:** Libraries, archives, museums, and cultural institutions as access points; digitisation of cultural heritage; digital preservation standards; equitable access to digital library services.

**4. Access to scientific knowledge:** Open access to scientific publishing; developing-country research capacity; the Research4Life programme (PubMed Central for LDCs) as a model; data sharing for research.

**5. Community public access points:** Telecentres, community centres, cybercafes, and other shared-access models that provide internet access where individual home connections are unaffordable.

### Key Geneva Plan of Action Provisions

The C3 section of the [[geneva-plan-of-action]] called for:
- Public domain policy: information produced by governments with public funds to be accessible online
- Open-source software and free software to be promoted to increase availability and affordability
- Preservation of cultural heritage through digitisation
- Open access to scientific literature, particularly for developing countries
- Community access points (telecentres, cybercafes, libraries) in every village
- ICT-enabled multilingual access — all world languages to be accessible online
- Intellectual property balance — protecting creators while maximising access

### Open Source and Free Software

C3 was the first major intergovernmental framework to specifically promote open-source and free software as a development tool. The [[geneva-plan-of-action]] called for "the promotion of the use of software that enables the widest accessibility to information, taking into account that this should encompass both proprietary and free and open-source software." This careful formulation acknowledged both models, avoiding an outright endorsement of FOSS over proprietary software but giving official legitimacy to open-source approaches in developing-country ICT strategies.

This position has influenced national ICT policies, with many developing countries adopting FOSS policies for government systems to reduce licensing costs and foster local technical capacity.

### Scientific Open Access

C3's open access provisions preceded the global open access movement's main inflection point (the Budapest Open Access Initiative, 2002) but aligned with it. UNESCO's mandate on scientific cooperation and open science directly supports C3 implementation. By 2025, the majority of major research funders in Europe and North America had adopted open access mandates, substantially advancing C3's goals — though developing-country research institutions remain disadvantaged by article processing charges (APCs) in the Gold OA model.

### Multilingualism

C3 addresses multilingual access — ensuring all world languages are represented online. This is also shared with [[c8]] (Cultural Diversity and Linguistic Diversity). WSIS called for ICT tools to support all languages; by 2025, approximately 400 languages were fully accessible online, but 6,600+ of the world's 7,000 active languages remained inadequately represented.

### SDG Connections

C3 is uniquely mapped to all 17 SDGs in the WSIS-SDG Matrix, on the grounds that information access is a universal enabler. Key thematic connections:
- **SDG 4** (Quality Education): Access to educational resources, e-learning, open educational resources
- **SDG 9** (Industry, Innovation): Open data for innovation; scientific knowledge sharing
- **SDG 10** (Reduced Inequalities): Ensuring knowledge does not become a luxury good
- **SDG 16** (Peace, Justice): Transparency; access to public information; freedom of expression online
- **SDG 17** (Partnerships): International scientific cooperation; data sharing

### WSIS+20 Mandate (2025)

[[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025) addressed information and knowledge access:
- **Para. 18:** Reaffirmed C3 and all Action Lines as "the main framework"
- **Para. 50–55** (Information and knowledge section): Called for enhanced open access to publicly-funded research; recognised AI-generated content as a new dimension; called for multilingual AI applications
- **Para. 113:** C3 facilitators to develop implementation roadmap by CSTD 2027

### Facilitation Meeting History

14 facilitation meetings were held under C3 (2006–2021), co-organised by [[itu]] and [[unesco]]. Meeting themes evolved from basic community access and open-source (2006–2010), to open access and multilingualism (2011–2015), to AI-generated content, data sovereignty, and open educational resources (2016–2021).

## Connections
- Defined in [[geneva-plan-of-action]] Section C (2003); co-facilitators confirmed in [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] Annex (2005)
- Co-facilitated by [[itu]] and [[unesco]] — bridging technical access and cultural/educational dimensions
- Linked to [[c8]] (Cultural Diversity) — both address multilingualism and cultural heritage digitisation
- Linked to [[c9]] (Media) — freedom of information, access to media content
- Linked to [[c4]] (Capacity Building) — access to knowledge is only meaningful with skills to use it
- Underpins [[c2]] (Infrastructure) goal: infrastructure must carry content that is accessible and available
- SDG alignment: all 17 SDGs via [[wsis-sdg-matrix]] — unique among Action Lines
- UNESCO's Open Science recommendation (2021) is the most comprehensive contemporary C3 implementation framework
- Facilitation meetings held annually at [[wsis-forum]]; coordination through [[ungis]]

## Open Questions
- C3's link to "all 17 SDGs" in the WSIS-SDG Matrix is intellectually compelling but analytically weak — should the 2027 implementation roadmap provide more specific target-level mappings?
- Open access mandates have advanced in the global North; what is the actual impact of C3 facilitation on developing-country access to scientific knowledge?
- AI-generated content is now being distributed online at massive scale — does this help or hinder C3's goal of making meaningful information available to all?
- The community access point (telecentre) model has been partially superseded by mobile internet — how has C3 facilitation adapted?

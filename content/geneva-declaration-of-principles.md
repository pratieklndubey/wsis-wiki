# Geneva Declaration of Principles

## Overview
The Geneva Declaration of Principles is one of two outcome documents adopted at the first phase of the World Summit on the Information Society ([[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]]), held in Geneva, Switzerland, from 10–12 December 2003. It establishes a shared vision and foundational principles for building a people-centred, inclusive, and development-oriented Information Society. Over 175 countries endorsed the document, making it one of the broadest intergovernmental agreements on the role of information and communication technologies (ICTs) in human development.

The Declaration is structured in three main sections: a common vision of the Information Society, eleven key principles for an inclusive Information Society for all, and a forward-looking commitment to shared knowledge. It draws explicitly on the UN Charter, the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the Millennium Declaration, the Monterrey Consensus, and the Johannesburg Declaration, anchoring the Information Society agenda within the existing international human rights and development frameworks.

The Declaration is paired with the [[geneva-plan-of-action]], which translates its principles into concrete action lines and targets. Both were reaffirmed and built upon at the second phase of WSIS in Tunis 2005, through the [[tunis-commitment]] and [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]].

## Key Facts
- **Adopted:** 12 December 2003, Geneva, Switzerland
- **Forum:** WSIS Phase I (first phase of the World Summit on the Information Society)
- **Lead agency:** [[itu]] (International Telecommunication Union)
- **Structure:** 67 paragraphs across three sections (A, B, C)
- **Section A:** "Our Common Vision of the Information Society" (paragraphs 1–18)
- **Section B:** "An Information Society for All: Key Principles" (paragraphs 19–64), covering 11 principles
- **Section C:** "Towards an Information Society for All Based on Shared Knowledge" (paragraphs 65–67)
- **Signed at:** Geneva, 12 December 2003
- **Reaffirmed:** Tunis, 18 November 2005 via [[tunis-commitment]]

## Detail

### Section A: Our Common Vision

Paragraph 1 articulates the central commitment: to build a "people-centred, inclusive and development-oriented Information Society, where everyone can create, access, utilize and share information and knowledge." The vision is explicitly grounded in the UN Charter and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.

Paragraphs 2–18 situate the Information Society within broader UN development goals — the Millennium Declaration, Johannesburg Plan of Implementation, Monterrey Consensus — and identify cross-cutting obligations: human rights, gender equality, inclusion of marginalized groups, protection of children, indigenous peoples' rights, and digital solidarity.

Key paragraph 10 acknowledges unequal distribution of ICT benefits between developed and developing countries and commits to "turning this digital divide into a digital opportunity for all." Paragraph 17 calls for new forms of solidarity, partnership and cooperation among governments, private sector, civil society, and international organizations — an early articulation of the multistakeholder model.

### Section B: Key Principles

The Declaration enumerates eleven key principles:

1. **The role of governments and all stakeholders** (paras. 20) — building a people-centred Information Society is a joint effort requiring cooperation and partnership.
2. **ICT infrastructure** (paras. 21–23) — universal, ubiquitous, equitable, and affordable access to ICT infrastructure is a core objective.
3. **Access to information and knowledge** (paras. 24–28) — rich public domain, open access, libraries, and software diversity (including open-source and free software) are essential.
4. **Capacity building** (paras. 29–34) — ICT literacy, education, and institutional capacity are foundations of the Information Society.
5. **Confidence and security** (paras. 35–37) — cybersecurity, privacy, and anti-spam measures are prerequisites for trust.
6. **Enabling environment** (paras. 38–47) — transparent, pro-competitive, and technology-neutral regulatory frameworks; radio spectrum managed in the public interest; state sovereignty respected.
7. **ICT applications** (para. 51) — ICTs should deliver benefits across e-government, health, education, agriculture, environment, disaster prevention, and culture.
8. **Cultural and linguistic diversity** (paras. 52–54) — cultural heritage, multilingualism, and local content are essential to an inclusive Information Society.
9. **Media** (para. 55) — freedom of the press, independence, pluralism, and diversity of media are essential.
10. **Ethical dimensions** (paras. 56–59) — the Information Society must respect peace, human dignity, privacy, and freedom of conscience; abusive uses of ICTs must be prevented.
11. **International and regional cooperation** (paras. 60–64) — global cooperation is needed, including through a potential Digital Solidarity Fund.

### Internet Governance (paras. 48–50)

Paragraphs 48–50 address Internet governance specifically — a topic that proved highly contentious. The Declaration affirms that:
- Internet governance should be "multilateral, transparent and democratic, with the full involvement of governments, the private sector, civil society and international organizations."
- Policy authority for Internet-related public policy issues is the sovereign right of States.
- The private sector should continue its important role in the Internet's technical and economic development.
- Civil society plays a role, especially at community level.

Paragraph 50 mandates the UN Secretary-General to establish a **Working Group on Internet Governance (WGIG)** to produce proposals by 2005 — which it did, feeding into the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] and the creation of the [[internet-governance-forum]].

### ITU's Role (para. 64)

Paragraph 64 explicitly recognises [[itu]]'s core competences — bridging the digital divide, international cooperation, radio spectrum management, standards development, and information dissemination — as "of crucial importance for building the Information Society."

### Section C: Shared Knowledge

The Declaration closes (paras. 65–67) with a forward-looking commitment to follow-up and evaluation, to reduce the digital divide, and to build "a new Information Society based on shared knowledge."

## Connections
- Paired with [[geneva-plan-of-action]], which operationalises these principles as action lines
- Reaffirmed by [[tunis-commitment]] (2005), which built on Geneva's foundations
- Mandated creation of WGIG, which fed into [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] and the [[internet-governance-forum]]
- [[itu]] served as lead agency and is explicitly recognised in paragraph 64
- Principles directly informed all 11 WSIS Action Lines (C1–C11)
- Part of the broader [[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]] process
- Linked to [[millennium-development-goals]] (MDGs) as an overarching development framework
- Informed subsequent connections to [[sdgs]] and [[global-digital-compact]]

## Open Questions
- To what degree were civil society and private sector voices genuinely incorporated vs. governments dominating the final text?
- The Digital Solidarity Fund (mentioned in para. 61) was proposed but remained voluntary — what became of it after 2003?
- The Internet governance paragraphs (48–50) represented a compromise: how contested was the final text, and which parties were most dissatisfied?
- How has the Declaration's framing of ICTs as "tools not ends in themselves" held up as technology has become increasingly infrastructural?

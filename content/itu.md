# International Telecommunication Union (ITU)

## Overview
The International Telecommunication Union (ITU) is the oldest United Nations specialised agency, originally established in 1865 as the International Telegraph Union — predating the UN itself by 80 years. Headquartered in Geneva, Switzerland, at Place des Nations (the Palais des Nations complex), ITU coordinates global telecommunications standards, allocates international radio-frequency spectrum and satellite orbits, and facilitates international cooperation in ICT infrastructure, capacity building, and policy. As of its founding in modern form, it has 193 member states and some 900 sector members drawn from the private sector, academia, and civil society.

ITU's role in the World Summit on the Information Society (WSIS) was central and defining. It served as the **lead management agency** for both phases of WSIS — [[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]] (Geneva, December 2003) and [[wsis-phase-ii-tunis-2005]] (Tunis, November 2005). The idea for the summit was first raised at the **1998 ITU Plenipotentiary Conference** (Minneapolis) by Tunisia, and ITU's Secretary-General Yoshio Utsumi served as the Summit Secretary-General throughout. ITU continues to host the annual WSIS Forum and maintains the WSIS Stocktaking database.

## Detail

### History and Structure

ITU was created in 1865 to coordinate telegraph communications across national borders — one of the world's first international regulatory bodies. It became a UN specialised agency in 1947. ITU today operates through three sectors:

1. **ITU-T (Telecommunication Standardization Sector):** Develops international technical standards (Recommendations) for telecommunications and ICTs
2. **ITU-R (Radiocommunication Sector):** Manages international radio spectrum and satellite orbit resources through the Radio Regulations
3. **ITU-D (Telecommunication Development Sector):** Assists developing countries in building ICT capacity and infrastructure

ITU's highest governing body is the **Plenipotentiary Conference**, held every four years, which elects the Secretary-General, Deputy Secretary-General, and other elected officials, and sets ITU's strategic direction. The **Council** meets annually and manages day-to-day governance between Plenipotentiary Conferences.

### ITU's Role in WSIS

ITU's involvement in WSIS was not accidental — it was instrumental from the beginning. Tunisia proposed the WSIS concept at the 1998 ITU Plenipotentiary Conference. The UNGA resolution 56/183 (2001) that authorised WSIS explicitly placed ITU in the lead management role.

**As summit organiser:** ITU handled the logistics, secretariat, and preparatory process for both Geneva 2003 and Tunis 2005. The Summit Secretary-General — Yoshio Utsumi — held the role by virtue of being ITU's Secretary-General.

**In outcome documents:** The [[geneva-declaration-of-principles]] explicitly recognises ITU's core competences in paragraph 64: "assistance in bridging the digital divide, international and regional cooperation, radio spectrum management, standards development and the dissemination of information — are of crucial importance for building the Information Society."

**As Action Line facilitator:** The [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] Annex designates ITU as sole facilitator or co-facilitator for the following Action Lines:
- **C2** (Infrastructure) — sole facilitator
- **C5** (Confidence and security) — sole facilitator
- **C1, C3, C4, C6, C7, C11** — co-facilitator with other UN agencies

**WSIS Stocktaking database:** ITU maintains the global database documenting all WSIS-related activities and projects — more than 2,500 registered at the close of the Geneva phase. This continues to serve as the primary accountability tool for WSIS implementation.

**Connect the World initiative:** ITU's flagship programme for bridging the digital divide, referenced in the Tunis Agenda (para. 98) as an example of effective multi-stakeholder partnership.

**WSIS Forum (2009–present):** ITU hosts the annual [[wsis-forum]], co-organised with [[unesco]], [[undp]], and [[unctad]] plus 40+ UN entities. It has grown from 1,200 participants in 2009 to 50,000+ annually. The WSIS Forum is the primary ongoing implementation and review mechanism for all 11 WSIS Action Lines.

**WSIS Prizes (2012–present):** Launched at the [[wsis-forum]] in 2012, the WSIS Prizes recognise excellence in ICT project implementation aligned with WSIS Action Lines and SDGs. By 2025, over 9,000 projects submitted; 252 winners and 720 champions recognised.

**WSIS Stocktaking database:** Maintained by ITU since 2004 (launched as required by the Tunis Agenda). As of 2025, over **19,000 entries** documenting ICT-for-development projects worldwide, categorised by WSIS Action Lines and SDGs. 2 million+ registered users.

**ITU Academy:** Provides training to 45,000+ learners globally on topics including broadband Internet, IoT, AI, big data, spectrum management, cybersecurity, and AI governance.

**Digital Transformation Centres (DTC) Initiative:** Provides digital skills training to underserved and remote communities. Over 77,000 participants (55% women) in H1 2024 alone.

**Giga Initiative (ITU-UNICEF):** Joint initiative to connect every school to the Internet by 2030. As of 2025, 280,000+ schools mapped across 49 countries with connected status.

**AI for Good:** ITU's leading UN platform for AI and sustainable development, co-organized with 40+ UN agencies and co-convened with Switzerland. Covers AI standards (ITU-T Study Groups), AI governance (AI Governance Day), capacity building (AI Skills Coalition), and AI deployment for development. IAWG-AI, co-chaired by ITU and UNESCO, coordinates 50 UN entities on AI.

**Broadband Commission for Sustainable Development:** Co-led by ITU and UNESCO since 2010. Tracks and advocates for universal broadband access; publishes annual "State of Broadband" report. 7 Advocacy Targets set for 2025.

**Partner2Connect Digital Coalition:** Mobilises public and private commitments to invest in connectivity for all, particularly in underserved regions.

**UNGIS (UN Group on the Information Society):** Established 2006. ITU is the **permanent secretariat** and holds a rotating chair position. UNGIS coordinates digital initiatives across UN Chief Executives Board (CEB) members to achieve WSIS objectives.

**Partnership on Measuring ICT for Development:** ITU serves as secretariat; Steering Committee member from the outset. Grown from 11 to 14 partner organisations. Coordinates internationally comparable ICT statistics and indicators.

**CWG-Internet:** ITU's Council Working Group on International Internet-related Public Policy Issues, established 2010. Has held 15+ open multistakeholder consultations on international Internet public policy.

**International Internet Connectivity (IIC):** The Tunis Agenda (para. 50d) called on ITU to study the question of IIC pricing "as a matter of urgency" — reflecting developing countries' concerns about high international transit costs.

**WSIS Forum:** Post-2005, ITU co-organises the annual WSIS Forum (with [[undp]], [[unesco]], and [[unctad]]) — a multi-stakeholder meeting held in Geneva to review progress on WSIS Action Lines.

### ITU and Internet Governance

ITU's relationship to Internet governance is complex and often contentious. As an intergovernmental organisation with a treaty-based mandate covering spectrum and standards, ITU represents the traditional model of telecommunications governance — state-centric, formal, binding. This stands in tension with the multistakeholder model that governs the Internet, centred on bodies like [[icann]], the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF), and [[isoc]].

During WSIS, some governments (particularly from developing countries and authoritarian states) sought to expand ITU's mandate to cover Internet governance — including oversight of IP addressing and the domain name system. This was resisted by the US, EU, private sector, and technical community. The compromise — the [[internet-governance-forum]] without oversight functions — sidestepped rather than resolved this tension.

The tension recurs periodically at ITU events, most notably at the **2012 World Conference on International Telecommunications (WCIT-12)**, where proposals to expand ITU's mandate over the Internet were rejected by a coalition of mainly Western countries in a closely contested vote.

### WSIS Foreword Attribution

The published WSIS Outcome Documents (Geneva 2006) carry a foreword by Yoshio Utsumi in his dual capacity as "Secretary-General of the World Summit on the Information Society (WSIS)" and "Secretary-General of the International Telecommunication Union (ITU)" — the two roles being held by the same person, making clear the institutional identification between ITU and the WSIS process.

### WSIS+20 Mandates for ITU (A/RES/80/173)

The [[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025) assigned ITU several specific mandates going forward:
- **Para. 67:** ITU (as UNGIS secretariat) to **establish an internal task force** to assess gaps in financial mechanisms for digital development in developing countries; submit concrete recommendations to CSTD 30th session (2027); monitor financial commitments
- **Para. 107:** ITU commended on WSIS Stocktaking platform and WSIS Prizes; all stakeholders encouraged to contribute to the stocktaking database
- **Para. 110:** WSIS Forum (co-organised by ITU with UNESCO, UNDP, UNCTAD) to continue annually
- **Para. 121:** UNGIS to continue with **secretariat provided by ITU**; rotating annual Chair; expand membership; enhance agility and effectiveness for multi-stakeholder dialogue and partnership-building

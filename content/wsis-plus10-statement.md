# WSIS+10 Statement on the Implementation of WSIS Outcomes

## Overview
The WSIS+10 Statement on the Implementation of WSIS Outcomes is one of two consensus documents produced at the **WSIS+10 High-Level Event** (Geneva, 10–13 June 2014). It was developed through the Multistakeholder Preparatory Platform (MPP) under the coordination of [[itu]], and represents the multistakeholder community's collective assessment of the first decade of WSIS implementation — the progress made, the challenges that remain, and the new challenges that have emerged since 2005.

The Statement is structured in three parts: a **Preamble** establishing the political and normative context; an **Overview of Action Line Implementation** reviewing what had been achieved across all eleven Action Lines (C1–C11); and a **Challenges section** identifying 30 specific areas requiring continued or new attention beyond 2015. It is paired with the **WSIS+10 Vision for WSIS Beyond 2015**, which sets out the forward-looking framework; together, these two documents fed into the UNGA's 2015 overall review and the adoption of Resolution A/RES/70/125.

A defining characteristic of the Statement is its deliberate scope restriction: it focused only on the Geneva Plan of Action Action Lines and avoided prejudging the Tunis-phase outcomes (Internet governance, IGF, enhanced cooperation, CSTD), which were addressed separately in the Vision document and the UNGA process.

## Key Facts
- **Full title:** WSIS+10 Statement on the Implementation of WSIS Outcomes
- **Status:** Final Agreed Draft
- **Adopted at:** WSIS+10 High-Level Event, Geneva, 10–13 June 2014
- **Convened by:** [[itu]] (coordinating), in collaboration with all UN agencies under their respective mandates; pursuant to ITU Council Resolution 1334
- **Document type:** Multistakeholder consensus statement (not an intergovernmental resolution)
- **Companion document:** WSIS+10 Vision for WSIS Beyond 2015
- **Structure:** 3 sections — A. Preamble; B. Overview of Action Line Implementation; C. Challenges
- **Key reaffirmation:** Geneva Declaration and Geneva Plan of Action "still remain valid until further decisions by General Assembly" (Preamble, point c)
- **Scope:** Deliberately restricted to [[geneva-plan-of-action]] Action Lines; excluded Tunis-phase Internet governance, IGF, enhanced cooperation, and CSTD to avoid prejudging the UNGA modalities process

## Detail

### Drafting Principles (Preamble, points a–g)

The document opens by stating seven guiding principles that shaped its drafting:
- Focus on WSIS Action Lines and measuring their implementation
- Develop a vision upon the existing Action Lines framework, identifying emerging trends and new decade priorities, **without making existing Action Lines obsolete**
- Geneva Declaration and Geneva Plan of Action **still remain valid until further decisions by General Assembly**
- Restrict proposals on Action Lines to the Geneva Plan of Action; respect respective UN agency mandates
- Avoid proposals on Tunis-phase outcomes (Internet governance, IGF, enhanced cooperation, CSTD)
- Avoid prejudging the outcomes of the 68th UNGA Session on review modalities
- Respect multistakeholder principles as set out in Tunis Agenda para. 35

These constraints were politically important: they protected the Tunis-phase architecture from being reopened during the WSIS+10 process, and they preserved the Geneva Plan of Action as the operational baseline.

### Section A: Preamble — Core Reaffirmations

**The ICT landscape ten years on (para. 1):** ICTs have "developed considerably and become a part of everyday life since the second phase of WSIS in 2005, accelerating social and economic growth, sustainable development, increasing transparency and accountability." This is the baseline positive assessment after a decade.

**ICTs and MDGs (para. 2):** ICTs "demonstrated their value as a facilitator and development enabler in reaching the Millennium Development Goals." This established the ICT-for-development linkage explicitly, laying groundwork for alignment with the post-2015 agenda (SDGs).

**ICTs for peace (para. 3):** Reaffirms Tunis Commitment para. 36 on the potential of ICTs to promote peace and prevent conflict. Notes that international law and the UN Charter apply to the ICT environment — a significant statement in the context of growing awareness of cyberwarfare and state surveillance (the Snowden revelations had been published in June 2013, one year before this document).

**Multistakeholder approach (para. 4):** Reaffirms the multistakeholder approach as vital to WSIS implementation, referencing Tunis Agenda para. 35.

**Eight normative reaffirmations (paras. 1–8):**

1. **Youth** — Reaffirms Geneva Declaration para. 11 on empowering youth as learners, developers, entrepreneurs, and decision-makers. References the BYND 2015 Global Youth Declaration from ITU's Global Youth Summit (2013).

2. **Human rights** — Reaffirms the Universal Declaration of Human Rights, the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights; and Geneva Declaration paras. 3, 4, 5, and 18. Notably applies human rights framework to the ICT environment.

3. **Gender equality** — Reaffirms "gender divide" as an identified problem; calls for mainstreaming gender equality across WSIS strategies. References UN-WOMEN, the High-Level Panel on the post-2015 agenda, and the **Beijing Declaration and Platform for Action** (Fourth World Conference on Women, 1995). Cites Geneva Declaration para. 12 on women as integral actors in the Information Society.

4. **Digital divide** — Acknowledges that bridging the digital divide "still remains inadequately addressed" and requires sustainable investments in infrastructure, services, capacity building, and technology transfer on mutually agreed terms.

5. **Disability and vulnerable groups** — References the **UN Convention on the Rights of People with Disabilities**, Geneva Declaration paras. 11, 13, 14, 15, and Tunis Commitment paras. 20, 22, 24. Calls for equitable access for youth, older persons, women, indigenous and nomadic peoples, people with disabilities, the unemployed, the poor, migrants, refugees, displaced persons, and remote/rural communities.

6. **ICTs as development enablers** — ICTs have "potential to be a key enabler of development" and a "critical component of innovative development solutions in the Post-2015 Development Agenda." Calls for recognition of content, skills, and enabling environment alongside infrastructure.

7. **ICT professionals** — New emphasis: calls for attention to "professionals that empower the users of ICT" — recognising that the rate of ICT development requires skilled human capital, not just infrastructure.

8. **Knowledge societies** — The evolution of the Information Society is "contributing towards development of knowledge societies around the world that are based on principles of freedom of expression, quality education for all, universal and non-discriminatory access to information and knowledge, and respect for cultural and linguistic diversity."

### Section B: Overview of Action Line Implementation

The Statement documents 22 points of progress across the Action Lines, organised as things we "note with satisfaction," "acknowledge as significant," "particularly emphasise," and "recognize."

**Progress noted with satisfaction (paras. 1–4):**
1. WSIS Action Lines built a "common understanding of the desirability to realize a truly global interconnected and inclusive Information Society" and drew attention to ICTs' role in poverty reduction and literacy
2. Geneva Plan of Action led to development of international, regional, and national strategies for inclusive Information Society
3. Greater awareness of digital inclusion for youth, women, vulnerable and marginalized groups, indigenous peoples, and linguistic diversity
4. The enabling environment (policies, content, capacities) "is understood today as being as important as infrastructure investments" — a significant shift from earlier infrastructure-first assumptions

**Significant contributions acknowledged (paras. 5–6):**
5. Efforts towards a global ICT-based economy through national ICT policies engaging all stakeholders
6. All UN agencies and stakeholders actively contributing to WSIS implementation

**Particularly emphasised (para. 7):**
7. The **Partnership on Measuring ICT for Development** and its core ICT indicators, adopted by the UN Statistical Commission; acknowledges the **ICT Development Index (IDI)**

**Progress recognized (paras. 8–22):**
8. Many countries made considerable progress in implementing Action Lines across sectors
9. **ICT infrastructure** boosted by new policies, broadband and mobile solutions, Universal Service Funds, national/regional Internet Exchange Points (IXPs), and international standards
10. **Access to information and knowledge** widened due to connectivity advances, mobile penetration, broadband expansion, and new platforms including **social media and cloud computing** — the first explicit mention of these platforms in a WSIS outcome context
11. **Improved ICT access in education** enhanced human development and skilled labour force
12. Increased efforts to improve affordable ICT access in developing countries and LDCs
13. Growing policymaker awareness of public ICT access and tools to combat the digital divide
14. Growing awareness of the need for collaboration on **confidence, security, privacy, and personal data protection** — with international cooperation recognised as key
15. WSIS Plan of Action deepened understanding of ICT for development, including ethical dimensions
16. ICTs play an important role in **socio-economic development through job creation and entrepreneurship**
17. WSIS Plan of Action supported research in **e-Science** with better understanding of emerging trends
18. Cultural diversity recognised as integral to the Information Society
19. Innovative financial mechanisms, public-private partnerships, and sustainable investment contributed to an inclusive Information Society
20. **The annual WSIS Forum has become an efficient global multistakeholder platform** for coordinating WSIS implementation; its decennial country and Action Line facilitator reports served as basis for the WSIS+10 High-Level Event
21. **WSIS Stocktaking Process**, including WSIS Project Prizes, has become an "efficient and effective platform for collection of ICT-related projects" and sharing best practices globally
22. Notes the "First WSIS+10 Review Event Towards Knowledge Societies, for peace and sustainable development" hosted by [[unesco]] and co-organised with [[itu]], [[unctad]], and [[undp]] in February 2013

### Section C: Challenges in Implementation and New Challenges

This is the most substantive and prescriptive section — 30 numbered challenges identifying what still needs to be done beyond 2015. These range from familiar themes (digital divide, affordability) to new issues (e-waste, privacy, media literacy) that had not been foregrounded in the original WSIS documents.

**Human rights and governance (paras. 1, 20):**
1. Protect and reinforce all human rights; ensure equal respect and enforcement of human rights **online and offline** — a significant formulation reflecting post-Snowden debate about whether offline rights frameworks apply to the Internet
20. Call upon all States to avoid unilateral measures not in accordance with international law and the UN Charter that impede economic and social development

**Equity and inclusion (paras. 2–5, 7, 29):**
2. Fully integrate gender equality perspectives in WSIS strategies; ensure women's empowerment and full participation in all spheres
3. Greater youth engagement and participation in WSIS process at all levels
4. Continued extension of ICT access for people with disabilities and vulnerable groups, especially in developing countries
5. More than half the world's population still not connected — infrastructure, capacity, and local content must continue to be addressed, especially in rural and remote areas
7. Greater efforts still required for affordable access to ICTs for all, particularly in developing countries and LDCs; equity of access (human capacities, public access) between urban and rural and between countries
29. Sufficient investment in digital inclusion measures, including access to software and hardware on non-discriminatory basis

**Infrastructure and connectivity (paras. 5–6, 9, 11, 21):**
6. Further improve management and use of radio-frequency spectrum and satellite orbits for low-cost networks, including satellite networks for developing countries (within ITU Radio Regulations framework)
9. North-South cooperation, complemented by South-South cooperation, to facilitate technology transfer and digital economy transition
11. Broadband deployment needed for the sustainable development agenda beyond 2015 and to make ICT applications available to everyone
21. Develop frameworks and measures (e.g. IXPs) to enhance ICT access, especially in developing countries

**Knowledge access and open resources (paras. 9, 12, 14):**
9. Promote access to information while respecting privacy; encourage open access to publications including research
12. Promote access to public information and knowledge, including **open access to scientific information**, particularly in developing countries
14. Education and lifelong learning using **Open Educational Resources (OER)** and applications to build ICT competencies responsive to societal and user needs

**Cybersecurity and privacy (paras. 15–16, 30):**
15. Exchange best practices on confidence and security in ICT use; develop international cooperation among stakeholders
16. Strengthen network security and privacy; support capacity building and coordination on incident response; encourage national and regional **Computer Incident Response Teams (CIRTs)**
30. **Promote further dialogue on the protection of privacy in light of technological developments** — the most direct reference to post-Snowden concerns in any WSIS outcome document to this point

**Policy coherence and investment (paras. 13, 17–19, 22–24):**
13. Adequate policies to enable SMEs to benefit from ICTs
17. Increase global, regional, and national awareness of WSIS process relevance to economic development strategies
18. Enhance policies; reduce the **skills gap** (growing between rich and poor within and between countries)
19. Continue setting realistic goals; reduce the **technology gap** (growing between developed and developing countries)
22. Promote investment and entrepreneurship in ICTs at national, regional, and international levels
23. Recall importance of trustworthy, transparent, non-discriminatory legal, regulatory and policy environment; ITU and regional organisations to ensure rational, efficient spectrum use
24. Coherent linkage between WSIS at international level and national/regional initiatives

**Cultural diversity and linguistic heritage (para. 26):**
26. Respect cultural and linguistic diversity; develop measures to **safeguard endangered languages**; preserve cultural and linguistic heritage including by supporting multilingualism

**New challenges — environment, media literacy, ethics (paras. 25, 27, 28):**
25. **E-waste** — "The need to ensure environmental sustainability, including by avoiding any harmful impacts that may result from the disposal of massive e-waste" — one of the earliest appearances of e-waste as a formal WSIS challenge
27. **Media and information literacy** — "People need media and information literacy skills that are indispensable in order to fully participate in an inclusive Information Society" — reflects UNESCO's MIL agenda entering WSIS
28. **Ethics of emerging technologies** — Increase awareness of ethical dimensions of ICT use; encourage "international and interdisciplinary reflection and dialogue on the ethical challenges of emerging technologies"

## Open Questions
- The Statement was a "Final Agreed Draft" — was it formally adopted by governments at the WSIS+10 High-Level Event, or was it a multistakeholder consensus document with different legal status from an intergovernmental resolution?
- How did the scope restriction (no Internet governance, IGF, enhanced cooperation topics) affect the usefulness of the document for governments who wanted to reopen those questions?
- The privacy challenge (para. 30) was a direct response to the Snowden revelations — did it produce any concrete follow-up in WSIS processes or the UNGA review?
- Challenge para. 19 notes the technology gap between developed and developing countries is "growing" — has subsequent data (2015–2025) confirmed or contradicted this assessment?
- E-waste (para. 25) was new to WSIS outcomes in 2014 — how was it addressed in subsequent WSIS Forum discussions and the WSIS+20 process?

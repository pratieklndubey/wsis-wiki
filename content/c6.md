# C6: Enabling Environment

## Overview
Action Line C6 is the regulatory and policy pillar of the WSIS framework, addressing the legal, institutional, and governance conditions that create a predictable, transparent, and competitive environment for ICT development and deployment. While [[c2]] builds the infrastructure and [[c4]] builds the human capital, C6 builds the rules of the game — the policy frameworks, regulatory institutions, and legal instruments that determine how digital markets function and who has power over them.

C6 carries a unique historical significance: Section C6 of the [[geneva-plan-of-action]] is where the mandate for the [[wgig]] (Working Group on Internet Governance) was embedded. The provision calling on the UN Secretary-General to establish a working group to investigate internet governance — a direct response to the ICANN controversy — was embedded in C6 rather than as a standalone decision, signalling that internet governance was understood as an "enabling environment" issue rather than a standalone political question. This decision led directly to the creation of [[wgig]] (2004–2005) and ultimately to the [[internet-governance-forum]] (2006-present).

C6 is co-facilitated by [[itu]], [[undp]], UN Regional Commissions, and [[unctad]] — reflecting the multi-dimensional nature of enabling environment: ITU leads on telecommunications regulation; UNDP on governance and policy; UNCTAD on trade and investment law; and Regional Commissions on regional regulatory harmonisation.

## Detail

### Scope and Purpose

C6 addresses seven major enabling environment dimensions:

**1. Legal and regulatory frameworks:** National telecommunications laws; independent regulatory authorities; licensing regimes; competition policy for digital markets; consumer protection; e-commerce law; electronic signatures; e-government legal frameworks.

**2. Internet governance:** The policies and processes by which the internet's technical infrastructure, standards, and protocols are managed. C6's mandate on internet governance led directly to the WGIG and subsequently the IGF. This is the most politically sensitive dimension.

**3. Intellectual property:** Balancing IP protection (patents, copyright) with public interest access; technology transfer to developing countries; traditional knowledge protection; software patents debate.

**4. Radio frequency spectrum:** National and international spectrum management; WRC (World Radiocommunication Conference) processes; equitable spectrum access for developing countries.

**5. E-commerce and trade:** Legal frameworks enabling cross-border e-commerce; consumer protection in online transactions; dispute resolution; trade facilitation through ICT.

**6. Privacy and data flows:** National data protection laws; cross-border data flows; adequacy frameworks; the right to be forgotten; data localisation debates.

**7. Domain name management:** Country-code top-level domains (ccTLDs) as national resources; ICANN's role; internationalised domain names (IDNs) for non-Latin scripts.

### The WGIG Mandate

The most historically significant provision of C6 is the instruction embedded in para. C6 of the [[geneva-plan-of-action]]:

> "We ask the Secretary-General of the United Nations to set up a working group on Internet governance, in an open and inclusive process that ensures a mechanism for the full and active participation of governments, the private sector and civil society from both developing and developed countries."

This mandate created the [[wgig]], which met four times (2004–2005) and produced a landmark report defining [[internet-governance]] and proposing four oversight models. The WGIG report directly shaped the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] and the creation of the [[internet-governance-forum]].

The placement of this mandate within C6 (enabling environment) rather than as a standalone agenda item was politically significant: it framed internet governance as a policy and regulatory issue within the WSIS framework, not a separate process.

### Telecommunications Regulation

ITU's Regulatory and Market Environment (RME) Division — led by C6 contact Sofie Maddens — produces the ICT Regulatory Tracker and is the principal international reference for telecommunications regulation best practices. Key themes:
- **Spectrum management:** The ITU Radio Regulations and WRC processes are the international legal framework; C6 calls for equitable spectrum access for developing countries
- **Universal access/service policies:** Government obligations to extend connectivity; universal service funds
- **Market liberalisation:** Moving from monopoly to competitive markets; independent regulators
- **Platform regulation:** The newest dimension — regulatory frameworks for digital platforms

### SDG Connections

C6's regulatory focus connects to SDGs through institutional quality and market conditions:
- **SDG 16.3** (Access to justice for all): E-justice, legal aid via ICT
- **SDG 16.6** (Effective, accountable institutions): E-government and transparency
- **SDG 16.7** (Inclusive decision-making): Multistakeholder regulatory processes
- **SDG 16.10** (Public access to information): Freedom of information laws, open government data
- **SDG 9.c** (Universal ICT access): Regulatory environment as enabler of connectivity investment
- **SDG 17.14** (Policy coherence for sustainable development): Coherent digital policy frameworks
- **SDG 17.16** (Multi-stakeholder partnerships): Governance processes for digital cooperation

### WSIS+20 Mandate (2025)

[[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025) addressed enabling environment:
- **Para. 18:** Reaffirmed C6 and all Action Lines as "the main framework"
- **Para. 77–83** (Internet governance section): Reaffirmed the Tunis Agenda principles; endorsed [[internet-governance-forum]] as permanent forum (para. 99); recognised AI governance as a new enabling environment challenge; called for enhanced regulatory frameworks for digital platforms and data markets
- **Para. 113:** C6 facilitators to develop implementation roadmap by CSTD 2027

### Facilitation Meeting History

16 facilitation meetings were held under C6 (2006–2021), co-organised by [[itu]], [[undp]], UN Regional Commissions, and [[unctad]]. The meetings have been politically significant — addressing spectrum, internet governance, and IP — and have attracted participants from national regulators, trade ministers, and the technical community. The evolution of topics mirrors the global digital policy agenda: from basic telecom regulation (2006–2010) through platform governance and data protection (2016–2021).

## Connections
- Defined in [[geneva-plan-of-action]] Section C (2003); facilitators confirmed in [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] Annex (2005)
- **Critical historical link:** C6 contained the mandate that created [[wgig]] and ultimately [[internet-governance-forum]]
- Addresses the foundational concept of [[internet-governance]] as a policy issue
- [[multistakeholder-model]] institutionalised by C6 provisions on inclusive regulatory processes
- Linked to [[c1]] (Government roles): C6 is the regulatory/legal dimension; C1 is the political/stakeholder dimension
- Linked to [[c5]] (Cybersecurity): Legal frameworks for cybercrime overlap C5 and C6
- Linked to [[icann]] and [[rirs]]: C6 addresses domain name management and IP address policy
- UNCTAD's regulatory work on e-commerce directly implements C6 provisions
- Facilitation meetings held annually at [[wsis-forum]]; coordination through [[ungis]]

## Open Questions
- C6 addressed "internet governance" in 2003 primarily as a question of ICANN oversight — how has the facilitation evolved to address AI governance, platform regulation, and content moderation as new enabling environment challenges?
- The WGIG produced four governance models for internet oversight; none was adopted. Has C6 facilitation revisited these models in the WSIS+20 context?
- Data localisation requirements — increasingly adopted by major economies — may conflict with C6's open e-commerce provisions: how should this tension be addressed in the 2027 roadmap?
- Is there adequate coordination between C6 (enabling environment) and the [[internet-governance-forum]] (which operates under C6's direct institutional legacy)?

# WSIS Stocktaking Database

## Overview
The WSIS Stocktaking Database is the primary global repository for documenting ICT-for-development initiatives and projects aligned with the WSIS [[wsis-action-lines]] (C1–C11). Maintained by [[itu]] since **2004** — as mandated by the Tunis Agenda — it is a publicly accessible system that serves as the principal accountability and monitoring tool for WSIS implementation worldwide.

As of 2025, the database contains over **19,000 entries** from governments, international organisations, businesses, civil society groups, and academic institutions, and has attracted over **2 million registered users**. The associated **WSIS Prizes** programme (launched 2012) recognises outstanding projects from the database in 18 categories aligned to the Action Lines, further incentivising participation.

The [[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025) commended the stocktaking platform and encouraged all stakeholders to contribute to it as the primary WSIS implementation monitoring mechanism going forward.

## Detail

### Purpose and History

The WSIS Stocktaking was launched in 2004 by ITU following the Geneva Phase (2003), as an early-action measure ahead of the Tunis Phase. The **Tunis Agenda** (2005) formalised it as a key WSIS implementation tool, with the stocktaking database serving as evidence of "what is being done" in the global ICT-for-development space.

The database evolved from a registry of projects into a comprehensive monitoring, reporting, and recognition platform:

**2004–2006:** Launch and initial population, primarily from governments and UN agencies
**2007–2010:** Expanded to include civil society and private sector; reporting tool for WSIS Forum facilitation meetings
**2011–2015:** Central to the WSIS+10 review process; ITU used stocktaking data to evidence progress on WSIS targets; **Partnership on Measuring ICT for Development** coordinated statistics
**2012:** WSIS Prizes launched — dramatically increased participation
**2015–2025:** SDG alignment added; database cross-references entries to SDG targets; COVID-19 special repository (2020–2021)
**2025:** 19,000+ entries; commended by A/RES/80/173; positioned as key implementation tool post-2025

### Submission Process

Any organisation — government, international body, private company, NGO, academic institution, local community group — can register and submit a project. The submission form asks for:
- Project name and description
- Relevant WSIS Action Line(s)
- Relevant SDG(s)
- Geographic scope
- Stakeholders involved
- Implementation status
- Website and contact

Submissions are reviewed and validated by ITU before inclusion. The platform sends periodic calls for submissions, particularly around:
- WSIS Prize nomination cycles (annual)
- WSIS Forum (annual)
- Review processes (WSIS+10, WSIS+20)

### WSIS Prizes

The WSIS Prizes programme recognises the most impactful projects in the stocktaking database. Since their 2012 launch at the [[wsis-forum]], the Prizes have become one of the most visible elements of the WSIS implementation architecture.

**Categories:** 18 categories aligned to:
- All 11 Action Lines (some with sub-categories for C7)
- Overarching categories for multi-Action-Line projects

**Process:**
1. Projects submitted to stocktaking database become eligible
2. Nominees announced (up to 5 per category)
3. Open online vote across registered WSIS Forum participants
4. Winners announced at WSIS Forum ceremony

**Scale (as of 2025):**
- 9,000+ submissions received total
- 252 winners recognised
- 720 champion projects recognised (runners-up)

The Prizes serve dual purposes: recognition and knowledge-sharing. Award ceremonies and case studies disseminate good practices across the global WSIS community.

### Stocktaking and WSIS+10 Review

The WSIS Stocktaking database was central to the WSIS+10 review process. ITU produced a series of stocktaking reports documenting progress across all 11 Action Lines between 2005 and 2014. These reports — based on database analysis — formed the evidential basis for the [[wsis-plus10-statement]] (2014) and the UNGA review (2015).

### Stocktaking and WSIS+20

The [[wsis-review-2025]] (WSIS+20) similarly relied on stocktaking data to demonstrate 20 years of WSIS outcomes. The [[unga-res-80-173]] (para. 107) specifically:
- Commended ITU for the WSIS Stocktaking platform and WSIS Prizes
- Encouraged **all stakeholders** to contribute to the database
- Requested continued reporting of stocktaking data to CSTD and the WSIS Forum

### Reporting Platform

Beyond project submission, the stocktaking platform serves as a **reporting tool** for Action Line facilitation meetings. Each year, facilitating agencies report on their Action Line progress using stocktaking data as evidence. The annual **WSIS Forum** includes a dedicated stocktaking presentation.

### COVID-19 Repository

During the COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2021), ITU launched a special stocktaking call for **ICT-based COVID-19 response projects**, creating a dedicated sub-repository. This demonstrated the database's flexibility as a policy-relevant monitoring tool for emerging challenges.

## Connections

- Primary evidence base for [[wsis-action-lines]] progress review at annual [[wsis-forum]]

- WSIS Prizes (18 categories) incentivise submissions and recognise excellence
- Cross-referenced to [[sdgs]] — supports WSIS-SDG Matrix

## Open Questions
- With 19,000+ entries, how does ITU ensure data quality and prevent duplication or inflation of project claims?
- The 2 million+ registered users figure — is this genuinely active users or cumulative registrations including dormant accounts?
- The Action Line implementation roadmaps mandated for 2027 (A/RES/80/173 para. 113) will require specific indicators — how will the stocktaking database be adapted to collect against these indicators?
- As the WSIS+30 horizon (2035) comes into view, should the database be redesigned to track GDC commitments alongside Action Lines?

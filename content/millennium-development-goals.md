# Millennium Development Goals (MDGs)

## Overview
The Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) were eight international development targets adopted by the United Nations at the Millennium Summit in September 2000, with the signing of the UN Millennium Declaration. They set specific, time-bound goals for reducing poverty, improving health and education, and promoting gender equality and environmental sustainability — to be achieved by 2015. They were the dominant global development framework in force when the World Summit on the Information Society convened in Geneva in December 2003.

The MDGs directly shaped WSIS: the [[geneva-declaration-of-principles]] and the [[geneva-plan-of-action]] were explicitly designed to advance the MDG agenda through ICTs. WSIS positioned information and communication technologies as enablers of MDG achievement — a framing that established ICT-for-development as the conceptual bridge between the digital and development agendas. When the MDGs expired in 2015 and were succeeded by the [[sdgs]], the WSIS framework adapted to align with the new goals, but the foundational logic — ICTs as development accelerators — remained continuous.

## Key Facts
- **Adopted:** September 2000, UN Millennium Summit, New York
- **Declared in:** UN Millennium Declaration (A/RES/55/2, 18 September 2000)
- **Number of goals:** 8 (with 21 targets and 60 indicators)
- **Time frame:** 2000–2015
- **Succeeded by:** [[sdgs]] (Sustainable Development Goals, adopted September 2015)
- **ICT-relevant goal:** MDG 8 — Global Partnership for Development, Target 8.F
- **Connection to WSIS:** Explicitly referenced in the Geneva Declaration of Principles (2003) and Geneva Plan of Action (2003) as the development framework that ICTs should serve

## Detail

### The Eight MDGs

1. **MDG 1** — Eradicate extreme poverty and hunger
2. **MDG 2** — Achieve universal primary education
3. **MDG 3** — Promote gender equality and empower women
4. **MDG 4** — Reduce child mortality
5. **MDG 5** — Improve maternal health
6. **MDG 6** — Combat HIV/AIDS, malaria, and other diseases
7. **MDG 7** — Ensure environmental sustainability
8. **MDG 8** — Develop a global partnership for development

### MDG 8 and ICTs

MDG 8 (Global Partnership for Development) was the MDG most directly relevant to WSIS. Its **Target 8.F** read: *"In cooperation with the private sector, make available the benefits of new technologies, especially information and communications."*

The associated indicators for Target 8.F tracked:
- Fixed telephone lines per 100 population
- Mobile cellular subscriptions per 100 population
- Internet users per 100 population

These were the first internationally agreed ICT indicators integrated into a UN development accountability framework, and they directly informed the statistical work later undertaken under WSIS [[c2]] (Infrastructure) and through the Partnership on Measuring ICT for Development.

### The WSIS–MDG Link

The Geneva documents make the MDG connection explicit. The [[geneva-declaration-of-principles]] states in its preamble that the Information Society must be "an integral part of the efforts to achieve the internationally agreed development goals and objectives, including those contained in the Millennium Declaration." The [[geneva-plan-of-action]] opens by declaring that its connectivity targets for 2015 are intended to help achieve MDG 8.F.

This framing served an important political function at WSIS: it anchored a technology-focused summit within the mainstream development agenda, helping developing countries and civil society see WSIS as relevant to poverty reduction rather than merely a commercial or infrastructure exercise. The digital divide was explicitly presented as a barrier to MDG achievement.

The connectivity targets in the [[geneva-plan-of-action]] — connect schools, villages, libraries, hospitals, and governments to ICTs by 2015; bring half the world's population online by 2015 — were calibrated to the MDG timetable. These targets, and their partial achievement, were reviewed in the [[wsis-review-2015]] and later the WSIS+20 process.

### ICTs as MDG Enablers: The WSIS+10 Assessment

By the time of the WSIS+10 High-Level Event (Geneva, June 2014), the [[wsis-plus10-statement]] explicitly affirmed that "ICTs demonstrated their value as a facilitator and development enabler in reaching the Millennium Development Goals." This assessed a decade of progress: mobile penetration had expanded dramatically across the developing world, e-health and e-agriculture applications had contributed to MDG targets on health and hunger, and ICT access had become recognised as a cross-cutting enabler rather than a standalone goal.

### Transition to the SDGs

The MDGs expired in 2015 and were succeeded by the 17 [[sdgs]] (Sustainable Development Goals), adopted at the UN Sustainable Development Summit in September 2015. The SDGs broadened the framework significantly — from 8 goals to 17, from a developing-country focus to a universal agenda — and embedded ICTs more deeply and specifically. Where MDG 8.F was a single general target, the SDGs reference ICTs directly in Goals 4, 5, 9, 10, and 17 (among others), with specific targets and indicators.

The WSIS+10 Review (UNGA Resolution A/RES/70/125, December 2015) explicitly aligned WSIS with the SDGs, repositioning WSIS as an implementation mechanism for the digital dimensions of the 2030 Agenda. The [[wsis-sdg-matrix]] (2015), produced by 12 UN agencies, maps all WSIS Action Lines to specific SDG targets — completing the transition from MDG to SDG framing.

## Connections
- [[geneva-declaration-of-principles]] — explicitly references MDGs as the development framework WSIS serves
- [[geneva-plan-of-action]] — connectivity targets for 2015 were calibrated to the MDG timetable; MDG 8.F is the primary ICT hook
- [[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]] — WSIS was conceived partly as an ICT-for-MDGs initiative
- [[wsis-plus10-statement]] — formally assessed ICTs' contribution to MDG achievement at the decade mark
- [[sdgs]] — successor framework; WSIS repositioned to serve SDG implementation from 2015 onward
- [[digital-divide]] — bridging the digital divide was presented as necessary for MDG achievement
- [[c2]] — infrastructure targets for 2015 aligned with MDG 8.F indicators
- [[c4]] — capacity building framed as MDG-enabling

## Open Questions
- Did the WSIS connectivity targets (connect villages, schools, hospitals by 2015) succeed in advancing MDG 8.F, or did mobile penetration outpace and render the WSIS targets less relevant?
- How much of the ICT-for-development framing in the MDGs translated into actual policy changes in developing countries?
- The MDGs were criticised for being developed without adequate participation from the Global South — was WSIS's alignment with the MDGs a strength or a liability given this critique?

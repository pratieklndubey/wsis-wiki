# Tunis Commitment

## Overview
The Tunis Commitment is one of two outcome documents adopted at the second and final phase of the World Summit on the Information Society ([[wsis-phase-ii-tunis-2005]]), in Tunis, Tunisia, on 18 November 2005. It is primarily a political reaffirmation document — world leaders and government delegations renewing their commitment to the vision established in the [[geneva-declaration-of-principles]] and the [[geneva-plan-of-action]] from December 2003, while also extending and deepening certain commitments in light of developments since Geneva.

The Tunis Commitment does not introduce entirely new frameworks; rather, it reaffirms, builds upon, and refocuses attention on the areas that Geneva left unresolved or underspecified — particularly financing mechanisms for bridging the digital divide, Internet governance, and the follow-up and implementation machinery. These substantive elaborations appear in the companion document, the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]].

The Commitment is significant as a political signal: it demonstrates that the multistakeholder WSIS process, despite its tensions and criticisms, produced genuine high-level buy-in from governments at both the 2003 and 2005 phases.

## Key Facts
- **Adopted:** 18 November 2005, Tunis, Tunisia
- **Forum:** WSIS Phase II (second phase of the World Summit on the Information Society)
- **Structure:** 40 paragraphs
- **Lead agency:** [[itu]] (International Telecommunication Union)
- **Relationship to Geneva:** Reaffirms [[geneva-declaration-of-principles]] and [[geneva-plan-of-action]] in full; extends commitments on digital divide, Internet governance, and implementation
- **Notable additions:** Explicitly names gender divide as part of digital divide; references the 2005 UN World Summit Outcome; recognises Digital Solidarity Fund established in Geneva

## Detail

### Core Reaffirmations (paras. 1–9)

Paragraph 1 formally reiterates "unequivocal support" for the Geneva Declaration of Principles and Plan of Action. Paragraphs 2–5 re-state the fundamental vision of a people-centred, inclusive, development-oriented Information Society grounded in human rights, the UN Charter, and multilateralism.

Paragraph 4 specifically reaffirms freedom of expression and the free flow of information — a provision included partly to address civil society and private sector concerns that the WSIS process had been used to increase state control over the Internet.

Paragraph 7 identifies the Tunis phase's particular focus areas: "financial mechanisms for bridging the digital divide, on Internet governance and related issues, as well as on follow-up and implementation of the Geneva and Tunis decisions."

### ICT and Development (paras. 10–22)

These paragraphs build on Geneva's development framing with additional specificity:
- **Para. 10:** Access to information and knowledge contributes to economic, social, and cultural development; barriers to universal access must be removed
- **Para. 11:** ICTs can expand access to quality education, boost literacy and universal primary education — an alignment with what would later become SDG 4
- **Para. 12:** ICTs enable enterprise development and SME growth; enterprise and labour market policies are key to ICT adoption
- **Para. 14:** Infrastructure alone is insufficient — human capacity and local language content are equally necessary
- **Para. 18:** Universal, ubiquitous, equitable, and affordable access to ICTs — including for persons with disabilities — is a core goal
- **Paras. 20–22:** Special attention to marginalized groups, LDCs, Small Island Developing States, landlocked developing countries, post-conflict countries, and indigenous peoples

### Gender and Youth (paras. 23–25)

**Paragraph 23** explicitly recognises the gender divide as part of the digital divide — a direct extension of Geneva's language — and commits to women's empowerment and full participation in the Information Society. This was a more emphatic statement than 2003's text.

**Paragraph 24** affirms ICTs' role in child protection and development, emphasising the "best interests of the child" as a primary consideration.

**Paragraph 25** renews commitment to youth as active contributors to and participants in building the Information Society.

### Technology, Openness, and Standards (paras. 28–29)

Paragraph 28 commits to building ICT networks and applications "based on open or interoperable standards" accessible anywhere, anytime, on any device — foreshadowing later debates about net neutrality and platform openness.

Paragraph 29 acknowledges both proprietary and open-source/free software models, reiterating the need to "encourage and foster collaborative development, inter-operative platforms and free and open-source software" — a compromise text reflecting sustained tension between software industry and open-source advocates.

### Digital Solidarity and Financing (paras. 31–35)

Paragraph 31 commits to implementing the Digital Solidarity Agenda from the Geneva Plan of Action, including fair trade, debt relief, and concrete international cooperation mechanisms.

Paragraph 34 acknowledges the need to mobilise both human and financial resources, referencing Chapter Two of the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] for the financing framework.

### ICTs and Peace (para. 36)

A notable addition: paragraph 36 explicitly values ICTs as a tool for promoting peace — including early-warning systems, conflict prevention, supporting peacekeeping missions, and post-conflict reconstruction. This is more explicit than Geneva's formulations.

### Conclusion (paras. 37–40)

The Commitment closes by calling for continued cooperation among all stakeholders, affirming that the Information Society's emergence creates "increasing opportunities for all our peoples," and expressing "strong resolve to develop and implement an effective and sustainable response to the challenges and opportunities of building a truly global Information Society."

## Open Questions
- How binding was this political commitment in practice? What accountability mechanisms ensured follow-through?
- The tension between "freedom of expression" (para. 4) and sovereign control over ICTs was never fully resolved — how has this tension evolved since 2005?
- Paragraph 36 on ICTs and peace was added partly at the insistence of developing countries facing conflict — how has this principle been applied in practice?
- How does the Tunis Commitment's language compare with the [[global-digital-compact]] (2024) — continuity or rupture?

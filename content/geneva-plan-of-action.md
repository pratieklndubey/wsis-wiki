# Geneva Plan of Action

## Overview
The Geneva Plan of Action is the second outcome document from the first phase of the World Summit on the Information Society ([[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]]), adopted on 12 December 2003. While the [[geneva-declaration-of-principles]] articulates the vision and values, the Plan of Action translates those principles into concrete action lines, targets, and responsibilities to be implemented by 2015. It is the operational companion to the Declaration.

The Plan identifies eleven Action Lines (C1–C11), each addressing a distinct thematic dimension of the Information Society. It also includes a Digital Solidarity Agenda (Section D), a follow-up and evaluation framework (Section E), and a brief forward-looking section on the Tunis phase (Section F). The Action Lines became the backbone of WSIS implementation — each assigned to one or more UN agency "moderators/facilitators," as confirmed in the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] Annex.

The Plan's indicative targets — connecting schools, hospitals, libraries, villages, and governments to ICTs by 2015 — were explicitly linked to the Millennium Development Goals and subsequently influenced the [[sdgs]] (especially SDGs 4, 9, 16, and 17).

## Key Facts
- **Adopted:** 12 December 2003, Geneva, Switzerland
- **Structure:** Six sections (A–F) plus eleven Action Lines (C1–C11)
- **Target year:** 2015 for indicative connectivity targets
- **Action Lines:** C1 through C11
- **Lead agency for implementation:** [[itu]] (plus [[undp]], [[unesco]], [[unctad]], and other UN agencies per action line)
- **Digital Solidarity Fund:** Proposed as a voluntary mechanism to mobilise financing for ICT development
- **WGIG mandate:** Embedded in C6 (Enabling Environment) — calls on UN Secretary-General to establish the Working Group on Internet Governance

## Detail

### Section A: Introduction

The Plan frames itself as an "evolving platform" reflecting different stages of development worldwide. It assigns distinct roles to each stakeholder group:
- **Governments:** Lead role in developing national e-strategies
- **Private sector:** Critical for infrastructure, content, and applications; also has a sustainable development role
- **Civil society:** Essential for equitable outcomes and ICT-related initiatives
- **International/regional institutions:** Integrate ICTs into development processes

### Section B: Objectives, Goals and Targets

The Plan sets out ten indicative targets to be achieved by 2015, including:
- Connect villages with ICTs and establish community access points
- Connect universities, colleges, secondary and primary schools with ICTs
- Connect scientific and research centres
- Connect public libraries, cultural centres, museums, post offices and archives
- Connect health centres and hospitals
- Connect all local and central government departments; establish websites and email addresses
- Adapt all primary and secondary school curricula to the Information Society
- Ensure access to television and radio services for the whole world's population
- Facilitate the presence and use of all world languages on the Internet
- Ensure more than half the world's inhabitants have access to ICTs within their reach

These targets were never formally binding but served as benchmarks guiding ODA, national e-strategies, and multi-stakeholder projects.

### Section C: The Eleven Action Lines

**C1 — The role of governments and all stakeholders in the promotion of ICTs for development**
- Moderators: ECOSOC / UN Regional Commissions / [[itu]]
- Calls for national e-strategies by 2005; multi-sector partnerships; public/private partnerships as showcases; ICT mainstreaming by international organizations

**C2 — Information and communication infrastructure**
- Moderator: [[itu]]
- Universal, sustainable, ubiquitous and affordable access; broadband; satellite; radio spectrum management; regional ICT backbones and Internet Exchange Points

**C3 — Access to information and knowledge**
- Moderators: [[itu]] / [[unesco]]
- Public domain policy; open access; community access points; software diversity (proprietary, open-source, free software); digital public libraries

**C4 — Capacity building**
- Moderators: [[undp]] / [[unesco]] / [[itu]] / [[unctad]]
- ICT in education; e-literacy; gender equality in ICT training; teacher training; distance learning; volunteering programmes

**C5 — Building confidence and security in the use of ICTs**
- Moderator: [[itu]]
- Cybersecurity cooperation; cybercrime prevention; privacy protection; anti-spam measures; incident response networks

**C6 — Enabling environment**
- Moderators: [[itu]] / [[undp]] / UN Regional Commissions / [[unctad]]
- Pro-competitive policy frameworks; radio spectrum management; e-government strategies; importantly — mandates UN Secretary-General to establish the **Working Group on Internet Governance (WGIG)** by 2005

**C7 — ICT applications: benefits in all aspects of life**
- Sub-themes and moderators:
  - E-government: [[undp]] / [[itu]]
  - E-business: WTO / [[unctad]] / [[itu]] / UPU
  - E-learning: [[unesco]] / [[itu]] / UNIDO
  - E-health: WHO / [[itu]]
  - E-employment: ILO / [[itu]]
  - E-environment: WHO / WMO / UNEP / UN-Habitat / [[itu]] / ICAO
  - E-agriculture: FAO / [[itu]]
  - E-science: [[unesco]] / [[itu]] / [[unctad]]

**C8 — Cultural diversity and identity, linguistic diversity and local content**
- Moderator: [[unesco]]
- Multilingualism; preservation of heritage; local content; indigenous languages; machine translation tools; internationalized domain names

**C9 — Media**
- Moderator: [[unesco]]
- Freedom of the press; independence and pluralism; media literacy; North-South media partnerships

**C10 — Ethical dimensions of the Information Society**
- Moderators: [[unesco]] / ECOSOC
- Prevention of abusive uses; ethics research; child protection; racism and hate speech online

**C11 — International and regional cooperation**
- Moderators: UN Regional Commissions / [[undp]] / [[itu]] / [[unesco]] / ECOSOC
- Development aid for ICT; North-South cooperation; regional integration; mainstreaming ICTs in UN programmes

### Section D: Digital Solidarity Agenda

The Plan introduces the concept of "digital solidarity" — mobilising human, financial, and technological resources for inclusive participation in the Information Society. It calls for:
- ICT strategies integrated into national development plans and Poverty Reduction Strategies
- ODA donors to mainstream ICTs in their programmes
- A review of existing financial mechanisms by end of 2004 (via a Task Force under the UN Secretary-General)
- Consideration of a voluntary **Digital Solidarity Fund** — a novel financing mechanism proposed particularly by developing countries

The Digital Solidarity Fund was subsequently established and welcomed by the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] (para. 28).

### Section E: Follow-up and Evaluation

Calls for a composite ICT Development (Digital Opportunity) Index; gender-specific indicators; community connectivity indicators; a best practices website; and national statistical capacity building. These led to the Partnership on Measuring ICT for Development (launched June 2004) and the Digital Opportunity Index.

### Section F: Towards WSIS Phase 2 (Tunis)

Instructs that a preparatory meeting be held in the first half of 2004 to structure the Tunis phase, which should: finalize documents building on Geneva outcomes; and follow up implementation at national, regional, and international levels through multi-stakeholder partnerships.

## Connections
- Operationalises the principles of [[geneva-declaration-of-principles]]
- Each Action Line links to custodian UN agencies: [[itu]], [[undp]], [[unesco]], [[unctad]], WHO, FAO, ILO, WMO, UNEP, WTO, UPU
- C6 mandated the Working Group on Internet Governance, feeding into [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] and the creation of [[internet-governance-forum]]
- Digital Solidarity Agenda led to the Digital Solidarity Fund, confirmed in [[tunis-commitment]] and [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]]
- Part of [[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]]
- Formally reaffirmed as still valid by the [[wsis-plus10-statement]] (2014): "Geneva Declaration and Geneva Plan of Action still remain valid until further decisions by General Assembly" (Preamble, point c); the 11 Action Lines (C1–C11) remained the operational framework through the [[wsis-review-2015]] process and UNGA Resolution A/RES/70/125
- Reaffirmed again as the "main framework" by [[unga-res-80-173]] para. 2 (reaffirms all four WSIS documents) and para. 18: "We reaffirm the centrality of the World Summit action lines, as defined in the Geneva Plan of Action, as the main framework for translating the World Summit vision into concrete outcomes" — the Action Lines remain operational 20+ years after adoption; see [[wsis-action-lines]] for current facilitators, SDG connections, and 2027 roadmap mandate
- Targets aligned with [[millennium-development-goals]]; influence visible in [[sdgs]] (especially SDG 4, 9, 16, 17)
- Implementation framework feeds into [[wsis-phase-ii-tunis-2005]]

## Open Questions
- Were the 2015 connectivity targets achieved? By how much did implementation fall short?
- How effective was the Digital Solidarity Fund in practice, given its voluntary nature?
- The assignment of Action Line C6 (enabling environment) to [[itu]] alongside the WGIG mandate created tension between ITU's intergovernmental character and the multistakeholder principles in the Declaration — how was this resolved?
- How did the Action Lines interact with national e-strategies, and which countries produced the most substantive ones?
- The Plan assigns "lead" roles to many UN agencies — did coordination actually occur, or did each act in silos?

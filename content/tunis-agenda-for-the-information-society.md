# Tunis Agenda for the Information Society

## Overview
The Tunis Agenda for the Information Society is the second of two outcome documents from the second phase of the World Summit on the Information Society ([[wsis-phase-ii-tunis-2005]]), adopted on 18 November 2005. It is by far the most operationally significant document produced by WSIS — it created the **Internet Governance Forum (IGF)**, established the enhanced cooperation process for Internet governance, resolved (partially) the contested question of international Internet connectivity costs, and put in place the machinery for implementing and following up the Geneva and Tunis WSIS outcomes.

The Tunis Agenda addressed two issues that Geneva left unresolved: (1) financial mechanisms for ICT development, and (2) Internet governance. On financing, it reviewed the Task Force on Financial Mechanisms report and set out concrete recommendations. On Internet governance, it provided a working definition, acknowledged the WGIG report, affirmed the multistakeholder model, and — most consequentially — mandated the UN Secretary-General to convene the inaugural meeting of the Internet Governance Forum by mid-2006.

The document also assigns specific UN agencies as facilitators/moderators of each Action Line from the [[geneva-plan-of-action]], creating the institutional scaffolding for WSIS implementation through 2015 and beyond.

## Key Facts
- **Adopted:** 18 November 2005, Tunis, Tunisia
- **Structure:** Four sections (A–D) plus an Annex
- **Lead agency:** [[itu]] (with [[undp]], [[unesco]], and others)
- **Key creation:** Internet Governance Forum (IGF) — mandated in paragraph 72
- **Working definition of Internet governance** (para. 34): "the development and application by governments, the private sector and civil society, in their respective roles, of shared principles, norms, rules, decision-making procedures, and programmes that shape the evolution and use of the Internet"
- **First IGF meeting:** Athens, Greece, 2006 (Greece offered to host — para. 82)
- **WSIS review:** Called for a UNGA review of WSIS outcomes in 2015 (para. 111)
- **World Information Society Day:** Called on UNGA to declare 17 May as World Information Society Day (para. 121)

## Detail

### Section A: Introduction

The Agenda opens by stating: "We recognize that it is now time to move from principles to action." It frames Tunis as building on Geneva by "focusing on financial mechanisms for bridging the digital divide, on Internet governance and related issues, as well as on implementation and follow-up." This two-part focus — money and governance — defines the document's structure.

### Section B: Financial Mechanisms for Meeting the Challenges of ICT for Development (paras. 3–28)

This section responds to the report of the **Task Force on Financial Mechanisms (TFFM)**, set up by the UN Secretary-General as called for in the [[geneva-plan-of-action]]. Key findings and commitments:

**Scale of the problem (paras. 7–8):** The digital divide is real, persistent, and requires adequate and sustainable long-term investment in ICT infrastructure, services, and capacity building.

**Private sector and enabling environment (paras. 13–16):** The shift from public to private ICT investment is noted positively, but market forces alone cannot ensure equitable outcomes. Enabling policy and regulatory frameworks are essential to attract private investment while ensuring universal access.

**Financing gaps identified (para. 23):** Specific underserved areas include:
- ICT capacity-building programmes and training
- Connectivity for remote/rural areas, Small Island Developing States, and LDCs
- Regional backbone infrastructure and Internet Exchange Points
- Broadband expansion
- ICT applications for poverty reduction (health, education, agriculture)
- Local content and digital applications for development

**Recommendations (para. 27):** Improving existing mechanisms to be more predictable, preferably untied, and sustainable; regional backbone investment; reducing international Internet transit costs; microfinance for ICT; North-South and South-South cooperation.

**Digital Solidarity Fund (para. 28):** The Agenda formally welcomes the Digital Solidarity Fund (DSF) established in Geneva as "an innovative financial mechanism of a voluntary nature" focused on specific and urgent needs at the local level. The DSF was intended to complement — not replace — existing financing mechanisms.

### Section C: Internet Governance (paras. 29–82)

This is the most significant section of the Tunis Agenda and the one with the most lasting impact.

#### Working Definition (para. 34)
The Agenda adopts the WGIG's working definition: Internet governance is "the development and application by governments, the private sector and civil society, in their respective roles, of shared principles, norms, rules, decision-making procedures, and programmes that shape the evolution and use of the Internet."

This definition is deliberately broad — it covers technical, economic, and public policy dimensions — and deliberately non-exclusive in its stakeholder references.

#### Multistakeholder Roles (para. 35)
The Agenda reaffirms the 2003 Geneva position on roles:
- **Governments:** Sovereign right over public policy for Internet-related issues
- **Private sector:** Important role in technical and economic development
- **Civil society:** Important role especially at community level
- **Intergovernmental organizations:** Facilitating role in coordination
- **International organizations:** Important role in technical standards

#### Cybersecurity and Trust (paras. 39–46)
Calls for global cybersecurity culture (referencing UNGA Resolution 57/239); cooperation on cybercrime prosecution; fighting spam; protection of privacy and personal data; and commitment to freedom of expression online.

#### International Internet Connectivity Costs (para. 50)
The contentious issue of high international Internet connectivity charges — which disadvantaged developing countries — is addressed. The Agenda calls for commercially-negotiated, transparent, non-discriminatory pricing; regional high-speed backbone networks; Internet Exchange Points (IXPs); and encourages [[itu]] to study International Internet Connectivity (IIC) as a matter of urgency.

#### Enhanced Cooperation (paras. 69–71)
The Agenda calls for a new process of "enhanced cooperation" to enable governments to carry out their roles in international public policy for the Internet — but explicitly *not* in day-to-day technical and operational matters. This was a compromise: developing countries wanted more governmental control over the Internet; developed countries and the technical community resisted. The enhanced cooperation process was tasked to begin by the first quarter of 2006 through the UN Secretary-General.

#### The Internet Governance Forum (paras. 67, 72–82)

**Paragraph 67:** Agrees to invite the UN Secretary-General to convene a new forum for multi-stakeholder policy dialogue.

**Paragraph 72:** The key paragraph. The UN Secretary-General is asked to convene, by the second quarter of 2006, a meeting of the **Internet Governance Forum (IGF)**. The IGF's mandate includes:
- Discussing public policy issues related to key elements of Internet governance
- Facilitating discourse between bodies dealing with cross-cutting Internet policy issues
- Interfacing with appropriate intergovernmental organizations
- Facilitating exchange of information and best practices
- Advising stakeholders on accelerating Internet availability and affordability in the developing world
- Strengthening engagement of stakeholders from developing countries
- Identifying emerging issues and bringing them to relevant bodies
- Contributing to capacity building for Internet governance in developing countries
- Promoting and assessing WSIS principles in Internet governance processes
- Discussing critical Internet resources issues
- Helping find solutions to problems from use and misuse of the Internet
- Publishing its proceedings

**Paragraph 73:** The IGF shall be "multilateral, multi-stakeholder, democratic and transparent." It should have a "lightweight and decentralised structure" subject to periodic review; meet periodically; and may hold meetings in parallel with major UN conferences.

**Paragraph 77:** Critical limitation — "The IGF would have no oversight function and would not replace existing arrangements, mechanisms, institutions or organizations." It would be "a neutral, non-duplicative and non-binding process" with no involvement in day-to-day or technical operations of the Internet. This language was essential to prevent the IGF from being seen as a vehicle to transfer control of the Internet from [[icann]] and existing bodies to an intergovernmental entity.

**Paragraph 82:** Welcomes Greece's offer to host the first IGF meeting in Athens, no later than 2006.

### Section D: Implementation and Follow-up (paras. 83–122)

This section establishes the institutional machinery for WSIS implementation:

**National level (para. 100):** Governments encouraged to establish national implementation mechanisms; national e-strategies should be integral to national development plans and poverty reduction strategies by 2010.

**Regional level (para. 101):** UN Regional Commissions and regional intergovernmental organizations to carry out WSIS implementation; regional WSIS follow-up activities; multi-stakeholder approach essential.

**International level (paras. 102–111):**
- UN agencies act according to their mandates and governing bodies
- **Paragraph 103:** Requests UN Secretary-General to establish within the CEB a "UN group on the Information Society" — considering [[itu]], [[unesco]], and [[undp]] as lead agencies
- **Paragraph 105:** ECOSOC requested to oversee system-wide WSIS follow-up; review of the Commission on Science and Technology for Development (CSTD) mandate
- **Paragraph 109:** [[itu]], [[unesco]], and [[undp]] designated as the three lead facilitating agencies; they should organise a meeting of Action Line moderators/facilitators
- **Paragraph 111:** Requests UNGA to make an **overall review of WSIS outcomes in 2015** — this became WSIS+10

**ICT measurement (paras. 113–120):** ICT indicators, the Partnership on Measuring ICT for Development, the Digital Opportunity Index, gender-disaggregated indicators.

**World Information Society Day (para. 121):** Calls on UNGA to declare **17 May as World Information Society Day** to raise annual awareness of the Information Society.

## Connections
- Paired with [[tunis-commitment]] as the two Tunis phase outcome documents
- Created the [[internet-governance-forum]] (IGF) — para. 72
- Confirmed the Digital Solidarity Fund (established after Geneva 2003)
- Designated [[itu]], [[unesco]], and [[undp]] as lead WSIS implementation agencies
- Assigned UN agencies to all eleven WSIS Action Lines (C1–C11)
- Called for WSIS+10 review in 2015 — leading to [[wsis-review-2015]], which in turn called for [[wsis-review-2025]] (UNGA Overall Review, December 2025)
- Launched the [[wsis-forum]] implementation cycle (annual follow-up meetings from 2006, WSIS Forum from 2009)
- Called for 17 May as World Information Society Day
- Builds on [[geneva-plan-of-action]] Action Lines
- Part of [[wsis-phase-ii-tunis-2005]]
- Internet governance working definition influenced all subsequent [[internet-governance]] discourse
- Enhanced cooperation process connected to ICANN's evolution and [[icann]] reform debates
- WSIS outcomes form part of the lineage leading to [[global-digital-compact]] (2024)

## Open Questions
- The enhanced cooperation process (paras. 69–71) was tasked to begin in early 2006 — what became of it? Did it produce meaningful outcomes or stall?
- The IGF was initially designed for five years and subject to review. It has survived and expanded — what explains its durability despite having no binding mandate?
- Was the compromise on Internet governance (IGF without oversight functions) a genuine settlement or a deferral of the underlying conflict?
- The Digital Solidarity Fund proved marginal in scale — why did it fail to attract significant funding, and what replaced it?
- How did the designation of [[itu]], [[unesco]], and [[undp]] as lead agencies work in practice given their different mandates and constituencies?
- The WSIS+10 review in 2015 (called for in para. 111) — was it adequate given the scale of transformation in the Internet landscape between 2005 and 2015?

# WSIS+10 Vision for WSIS Beyond 2015

## Overview
The WSIS+10 Vision for WSIS Beyond 2015 is one of two consensus documents produced at the **WSIS+10 High-Level Event** (Geneva, 10–13 June 2014). Where its companion document — the [[wsis-plus10-statement]] — looked backward to assess a decade of WSIS Action Line implementation, the Vision looked forward: it set out the multistakeholder community's collective view on what the WSIS framework should become after 2015.

The Vision was developed through the same Multistakeholder Preparatory Platform (MPP) process coordinated by [[itu]], but it addressed the topics that the [[wsis-plus10-statement]] deliberately left aside: Internet governance, the future mandate of the [[internet-governance-forum]], enhanced cooperation among governments, and the role of [[cstd]] in WSIS follow-up. These were the politically contested Tunis-phase issues that could not be resolved within the scope of a multistakeholder review, and the Vision surfaced the key positions and aspirations of different stakeholder communities as input to the UNGA process.

Together, the Vision and the Statement fed directly into the UNGA's 2015 overall review of WSIS, which produced Resolution A/RES/70/125 ([[unga-res-80-173]] predecessor) — the intergovernmental document that formally aligned WSIS with the [[sdgs]] and called for the WSIS+20 Review in 2025.

## Key Facts
- **Full title:** WSIS+10 Vision for WSIS Beyond 2015
- **Status:** Final Agreed Draft (multistakeholder consensus; not an intergovernmental resolution)
- **Adopted at:** WSIS+10 High-Level Event, Geneva, 10–13 June 2014
- **Convened by:** [[itu]] (coordinating), pursuant to ITU Council Resolution 1334
- **Companion document:** [[wsis-plus10-statement]] on the Implementation of WSIS Outcomes
- **Primary audience:** UNGA and its 68th/69th session processes for determining WSIS review modalities
- **Outcome:** Fed into UNGA Resolution A/RES/70/125 (December 2015), the WSIS+10 overall review outcome
- **Thematic scope:** Forward-looking; focused on Internet governance, IGF future, enhanced cooperation, CSTD role, and post-2015 development agenda

## Detail

### Purpose and Political Context

By 2014, three major processes were converging that gave the Vision document its stakes:

1. **End of the 2015 MDG horizon** — the [[millennium-development-goals]] were expiring, and a post-2015 development agenda was being negotiated. WSIS needed to demonstrate its relevance to whatever came next (ultimately, the [[sdgs]]).

2. **IGF mandate renewal** — the [[internet-governance-forum]] had been created by the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] with a renewable five-year mandate. The UNGA had renewed it once (2010). A further renewal was needed, and some governments wished to use the WSIS+10 process to fundamentally restructure or strengthen the IGF.

3. **Enhanced cooperation unresolved** — the Tunis Agenda's call for "enhanced cooperation" on Internet public policy had produced no agreed outcome in a decade. Different stakeholders read "enhanced cooperation" differently: some saw it as a pathway to increased governmental authority over Internet governance; others saw it as an affirmation of the existing multistakeholder model.

The Vision document navigated these tensions without resolving them, surfacing the range of views as structured input to the UNGA.

### Key Themes of the Vision

**1. Post-2015 Development Framework**

The Vision positioned WSIS and its Action Lines as natural implementation vehicles for the post-2015 development agenda that would succeed the MDGs. It argued that the 11 Action Lines (C1–C11) remained a valid and adaptable framework, and that the governance machinery built since 2003 — the WSIS Forum, the Action Line facilitation meetings, the WSIS Stocktaking database — should be continued and strengthened rather than replaced. This argument was ultimately vindicated when the [[wsis-sdg-matrix]] (2015) mapped all Action Lines to specific SDG targets.

**2. Internet Governance**

The Vision addressed the future of Internet governance — the topic most deliberately excluded from the [[wsis-plus10-statement]]. The multistakeholder community's positions, as captured in the Vision, broadly favoured:
- Continuation of the [[multistakeholder-model]] as the appropriate framework for Internet governance
- Resistance to intergovernmental bodies (including [[itu]]) acquiring decisional authority over Internet naming, numbering, or routing
- Support for the NETmundial process (the Global Multistakeholder Meeting on the Future of Internet Governance, Sao Paulo, April 2014 — convened partly in response to the Snowden revelations and the US government's announced intention to transition IANA oversight)

The Vision thus captured a moment of particular intensity in the global Internet governance debate.

**3. Future of the Internet Governance Forum**

The Vision expressed strong support for renewing and strengthening the [[internet-governance-forum]] mandate. The IGF's institutional weaknesses — no secretariat budget security, no decisional authority, limited follow-up mechanisms — were acknowledged, but the Vision supported addressing these within the existing multistakeholder framework rather than replacing the IGF with an intergovernmental body.

This position shaped the UNGA's 2015 decision: A/RES/70/125 renewed the IGF mandate for another ten years (to 2025), with some improvements to its working methods. The permanent IGF status came only a decade later, with [[unga-res-80-173]] (2025).

**4. Enhanced Cooperation**

The Tunis Agenda para. 69 called for enhanced cooperation "to enable governments, on an equal footing, to carry out their roles and responsibilities in international public policy issues pertaining to the Internet." The Vision surfaced the decade-long disagreement over what this meant:
- Some governments (primarily developing countries, including those sceptical of US dominance over critical Internet infrastructure) saw enhanced cooperation as a mandate for a new intergovernmental mechanism
- Others (Western governments, private sector, technical community) argued the existing arrangements — [[icann]], [[isoc]], ITU's CWG-Internet, and the IGF — constituted adequate enhanced cooperation

The Vision did not resolve this tension but provided structured input to the UNGA, which also declined to resolve it definitively in A/RES/70/125. The question of enhanced cooperation remained an open issue through the WSIS+20 process.

**5. Role of CSTD**

The Vision addressed the role of the [[cstd]] (Commission on Science and Technology for Development) in WSIS follow-up. As the primary UN intergovernmental body for WSIS accountability, CSTD's capacity and mandate were considered adequate by some stakeholders and insufficient by others. The Vision's contribution was to affirm CSTD's role in receiving and synthesising WSIS review reports — a function explicitly reaffirmed in A/RES/70/125 and again in [[unga-res-80-173]].

### Relationship to the WSIS+10 Statement

The two WSIS+10 documents were deliberately designed to be complementary and non-overlapping:

| | WSIS+10 Statement | WSIS+10 Vision |
|---|---|---|
| **Orientation** | Backward-looking | Forward-looking |
| **Scope** | Geneva Plan of Action (Action Lines C1–C11) | Tunis-phase outcomes; Internet governance; post-2015 |
| **Topics avoided** | Internet governance; IGF; enhanced cooperation; CSTD | (none — this is where those topics live) |
| **Legal status** | Multistakeholder consensus | Multistakeholder consensus |
| **Audience** | WSIS community; UN agencies | UNGA; member states |

### Legacy and Use

The WSIS+10 Vision fed directly into the UNGA's modalities process for the 2015 overall review. The UNGA resolution A/RES/70/125 (December 2015):
- Renewed the IGF mandate until 2025
- Reaffirmed the Tunis Agenda's enhanced cooperation provisions without resolving the underlying disagreement
- Affirmed WSIS as an implementation mechanism for the SDGs
- Called for a WSIS+20 Review in 2025

The Vision is thus historically important as the bridge document between the WSIS+10 multistakeholder process and the intergovernmental UNGA decision that set the agenda for the next decade — culminating in the [[wsis-plus20-preparatory-process]] and the [[wsis-review-2025]].

## Open Questions
- The Vision surfaced but did not resolve the enhanced cooperation debate — did this deliberate ambiguity serve the multistakeholder community well, or did it allow governments to continue pursuing conflicting agendas without accountability?
- The NETmundial Multistakeholder Statement (2014) was the other major multistakeholder document from this period — how did it interact with and influence the WSIS+10 Vision?
- The IGF mandate was renewed by the Vision's recommendation, but the structural weaknesses the Vision acknowledged (funding, follow-up) persisted for another decade — was this a failure of the Vision's advocacy?
- Has the full text of the Vision document been preserved in accessible archives, or does it risk being lost as WSIS documentation migrates?

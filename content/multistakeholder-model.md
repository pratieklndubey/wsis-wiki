# Multistakeholder Model

## Overview
The multistakeholder model is the governance approach that "brings multiple stakeholders together to participate in dialogue, decision making, and implementation" of responses to shared challenges. It must minimally include "two or more actors from different social, political, or economic groups." In the context of WSIS and internet governance, it refers specifically to a model in which governments, the private sector, civil society, and the technical/academic community all participate — as opposed to exclusively intergovernmental (multilateral) governance where only nation-states are formal participants.

The multistakeholder model became institutionalised in internet governance through WSIS. The [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] (2005) "enshrined a particular type of multistakeholder model for internet governance" — one that delegated naming and addressing to the private sector through [[icann]], while creating the [[internet-governance-forum]] (IGF) as an inclusive dialogue platform. This outcome reflected intense political negotiations at [[wsis-phase-ii-tunis-2005]] and remains contested today, with some governments preferring greater intergovernmental control and others defending the current model.

The model has broader roots than WSIS — it emerged from the Rio Earth Summit (1992), the WEF Global Redesign Initiative, and other multilateral processes — but WSIS is its most detailed and consequential articulation for global digital governance.

## Key Facts
- **Core definition:** Governance involving two or more actors from different stakeholder groups (governments, private sector, civil society, technical community)
- **Origins in UN system:** Rio Earth Summit (1992) — 9 Major Groups as stakeholder categories
- **WSIS institutionalisation:** Tunis Agenda (2005) — "enshrined" multistakeholder model for internet governance
- **Four WSIS stakeholder groups:** Governments; Private sector; Civil society; Technical and academic community
- **Principal WSIS embodiment:** [[internet-governance-forum]] (IGF) — non-binding, non-decisional, all-stakeholder dialogue
- **Contrast:** Multilateral model — only nation-states as formal actors
- **Key critique:** Democratic deficit — no standardised stakeholder selection, no appeals mechanism, unequal power
- **WSIS+20 reaffirmation:** [[unga-res-80-173]] (2025) reaffirmed multistakeholder approach as core to WSIS and GDC follow-up

## Detail

### Historical Development

**1992 — Rio Earth Summit:**
The UN Conference on Environment and Development in Rio de Janeiro established nine "Major Groups" as stakeholder categories for environmental governance: women; children and youth; indigenous peoples; NGOs; local authorities; workers and trade unions; business and industry; scientific and technological community; farmers. This represented the first systematic institutionalisation of non-state actors in global governance at the UN level.

**2002 — Johannesburg Declaration:**
The World Summit on Sustainable Development created "type II conference outcomes" — multistakeholder implementation partnerships alongside intergovernmental type I agreements. This established the precedent of formal partnerships as governance outputs alongside state-negotiated declarations.

**2003–2005 — WSIS and Internet Governance:**
WSIS became the dominant arena for multistakeholder governance debates in the early 2000s. The central question was whether [[icann]]'s existing multistakeholder governance model — in which the U.S. government held ultimate oversight but technical management was delegated to the private sector — was appropriate for a global resource.

The [[wgig]] (2004–2005) studied the question and proposed four oversight models. The Tunis Agenda outcome (2005) neither accepted nor rejected multistakeholderism in full — it accepted it for internet coordination (retaining ICANN), created the IGF as a multistakeholder dialogue, but also affirmed that governments have "specific roles and responsibilities" that are distinct from other stakeholders.

**2009–2010 — WEF Global Redesign Initiative:**
The World Economic Forum's Global Redesign Initiative (750 experts, 60 task forces, 2009–2010) proposed replacing some G20-style intergovernmental governance with "plurilateral, often multi-stakeholder, coalitions" — promoting multistakeholderism beyond internet governance into global economic governance broadly.

**2013–2016 — IANA Transition:**
Following the Snowden surveillance revelations (2013), the Montevideo Statement by I* organisations called for globalisation of ICANN/IANA. The U.S. DOC announced in March 2014 that it would not renew its IANA contract, and in October 2016, ICANN completed the IANA stewardship transition — the most significant structural change to internet governance since ICANN's founding. This represented a victory for the multistakeholder model over proposals for UN-based intergovernmental oversight.

### The Four WSIS Stakeholder Groups

The [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] (2005) formally recognised four stakeholder groups in internet governance:

**1. Governments**
Governments engage in multistakeholder processes for multiple reasons: to develop complex policies beyond traditional bureaucratic capacity; to avoid the slowness of traditional multilateral channels; to delegate standard-setting to private technical bodies; to participate in public-private partnerships; and to shift from mandatory to voluntary standards. Key tension: governments have unique democratic accountability but unequal technical capacity.

**2. Private Sector**
Corporations benefit from the multistakeholder model in several ways: it provides an alternative to state regulatory oversight; it gives them institutional entry into global governance; it allows them to lead policy definition outside the UN system; and it enables them to shape technical specifications directly. Key critique: private sector actors are accountable primarily to shareholders, not citizens.

**3. Civil Society**
Civil society organisations are deeply divided on multistakeholder participation:
- **Participating CSOs:** Engage to avoid exclusion from consequential decisions, even if participation is imperfect
- **Resistant CSOs:** Actively reject multistakeholder processes, citing lack of legitimate stakeholder selection processes and inherent power imbalances that marginalise civil society relative to states and corporations

**4. Technical and Academic Community**
Scholars and technical experts occupy an unusual governance role — increasingly moving from advisory positions to functions "functionally similar to a state regulatory official" in technical standards bodies. IETF RFCs (Requests for Comments) have real-world regulatory consequences equivalent to law in many jurisdictions.

### Multistakeholder vs. Multilateral: Comparison

| Aspect | Multilateral | Multistakeholder |
|--------|--------------|------------------|
| Primary actors | Nation-states | States, corporations, civil society, academics |
| Decision legitimacy | Democratic representation | Consensual input from diverse stakeholders |
| Actor selection | State sovereignty (universal membership) | Variable; often founder-determined |
| Accountability | To citizens through governments | Ambiguous; to member constituencies |
| Speed | Slow (consensus among 190+ states) | Faster (smaller, self-selected communities) |
| Inclusiveness | Formally universal (all states) | Variable; poorly resourced actors often marginalised |
| Key examples | ITU, WTO, UN General Assembly | ICANN, IETF, IGF |

### Debates and Criticisms

**Democratic Deficit:**
The most fundamental critique is that multistakeholder processes lack democratic accountability. There is no standard process for selecting stakeholder representatives, no review or appeals mechanism, and no clear principle for how competing stakeholder interests are weighted. In practice, well-resourced actors (major corporations, large governments) exercise disproportionate influence.

**Power Asymmetry:**
Participation in multistakeholder processes is formally open but effectively restricted by resources — travel costs, language barriers, technical expertise requirements, and the capacity to engage in ongoing processes. This systematically disadvantages civil society from the Global South and small developing states.

**Selection Ambiguity:**
There are no standardised definitions of who qualifies as a "stakeholder" or how representatives are chosen for each category. Many multistakeholder forums are dominated by whoever showed up at founding meetings, creating path dependencies that are hard to reform.

**Legitimacy Challenges:**
The Transnational Institute (2019) argues that multistakeholderism "threatens democracy through business intrusion into formal international decision-making" — replacing elected governmental accountability with corporate co-governance. Conversely, defenders argue it represents "positive transformation from representative democracy to stakeholder-based participatory democracy," incorporating expertise and affected interests more directly.

**IBSA Proposal (2011):**
India, Brazil, and South Africa proposed a "UN Committee on Internet-Related Policy" that would replace multistakeholder models with intergovernmental structures. This proposal was debated but not adopted.

**NetMundial (April 2014):**
A Brazil-hosted multistakeholder conference following the Snowden revelations produced the "Multistakeholder Statement of São Paulo" affirming open, interoperable internet governance principles. Russia, India, Cuba, and others dissented from elements of the statement — highlighting the ongoing divide between multistakeholder and multilateral approaches.

### WSIS and the Multistakeholder Model: Legacy

The WSIS institutionalisation of multistakeholderism has been both consequential and contested:

1. **[[internet-governance-forum]] (IGF):** The IGF is the most prominent ongoing expression of the WSIS multistakeholder model — all stakeholders participate equally in discussions, though outcomes are non-binding. [[unga-res-80-173]] (2025) made the IGF a permanent UN forum, the strongest endorsement it has received.

2. **Action Line Facilitation:** WSIS [[wsis-action-lines]] implementation involves governments, UN agencies, private sector, and civil society in facilitation meetings — a practical expression of multistakeholderism for digital development.

3. **[[global-digital-compact]] (2024):** The GDC reaffirmed the multistakeholder model and built on WSIS governance architecture; it also designates the IGF as the primary multistakeholder forum for internet governance (GDC para. 28).

4. **Ongoing tension:** The WSIS+20 review revealed continued tension between those who view WSIS as an effective multistakeholder framework and those (primarily governments) who want stronger intergovernmental structures for AI, data governance, and digital regulation.

## Open Questions
- Can the multistakeholder model be made more democratic and equitable without becoming purely intergovernmental?
- Should AI governance (now a major WSIS/GDC issue) follow the WSIS multistakeholder model or require new structures?
- The IGF's permanent status ([[unga-res-80-173]], 2025) — does this strengthen multistakeholderism or risk co-opting the forum into UN bureaucracy?
- How should "meaningful participation" for underrepresented stakeholders be measured and enforced?

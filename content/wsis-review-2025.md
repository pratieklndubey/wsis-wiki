# WSIS+20 Review (2025)

## Overview
The WSIS+20 Review was the second overall review of the implementation of the World Summit on the Information Society outcomes, mandated by UNGA Resolution A/RES/70/125 (2015) — itself the outcome of the [[wsis-review-2015]] (WSIS+10). The review took stock of twenty years of WSIS implementation since the Geneva Plan of Action (2003) and established the framework for digital cooperation beyond 2025.

The UNGA's overall review was held on **16–17 December 2025** — formally titled the "UNGA High-Level Meeting on the Overall Review of the Implementation of WSIS Outcomes." The review was co-facilitated by H.E. Mr Ekitela Lokaale (Permanent Representative of Kenya) and H.E. Ms Suela Janina (Permanent Representative of Albania) at the UN in New York. The preparatory process involved [[itu]], [[unesco]], [[undp]], [[unctad]], UN DESA, CSTD, and the UN Regional Commissions.

**The review concluded with the adoption of [[unga-res-80-173]] on 17 December 2025** — the WSIS+20 outcome resolution. Key landmark decisions: the [[internet-governance-forum]] was made a **permanent forum of the United Nations**; a WSIS+30 review was called for in **2035**; action line facilitators were mandated to develop implementation roadmaps by 2027; and strong coherence provisions were established between WSIS and the [[global-digital-compact]]. The resolution reaffirmed the [[geneva-plan-of-action]] Action Lines as the "main framework" and recorded Internet usage at 74% globally (up from ~15% in 2005).

## Detail

### Mandate and Background

UNGA Resolution A/RES/70/125 (adopted 16 December 2015) called for a high-level meeting of the General Assembly in 2025 to review WSIS outcomes — the WSIS+20 Review. This review:
- Takes stock of progress since the [[geneva-plan-of-action]] (2003) and the WSIS+10 outcomes (2015)
- Identifies areas of continued focus and challenges
- Considers the next phase of WSIS or successor arrangements beyond 2025
- Feeds into the broader 2030 Agenda review processes

### WSIS+20 Preparatory Process

A joint preparatory process has been established among key UN entities. Regular coordination meetings have been held since 2023 involving [[itu]], [[unesco]], [[undp]], [[unctad]], UN DESA, CSTD, and the Regional Commissions. Key preparatory milestones:

- **Regional events (2023–2025):** UN ESCAP (Yerevan, November 2023); UN ECE (Geneva, March 2024); UN ESCWA (Dubai, May 2024; Amman, February 2025)
- **SDG Summit, September 2023**
- **IGF 2023 (Kyoto, October 2023):** WSIS+20 high-level panel and WSIS high-level dialogue
- **CSTD WSIS+20 Open Consultation**
- **WSIS+20 Forum High-Level Event 2024:** 27–31 May 2024, Geneva — primary stakeholder preparatory platform
- **WSIS+20 High-Level Event 2025:** 7–11 July 2025, Geneva (alongside AI for Good Global Summit) — final major stakeholder event before the UNGA review

### WSIS+20 Forum High-Level Events

**2024 (27–31 May, Geneva):**
- 4,000+ participants from 160 countries; ~80 ministers, deputies, and regulatory authority heads
- Chaired by H.E. Albert Rösti (Federal Councillor, Switzerland)
- Covered: WSIS+20 review, GDC/Summit of the Future, digital public infrastructure (DPI), AI governance, virtual reality, quantum technologies, generative AI, cybersecurity, digital trust
- Outcome documents: Chair's Summary; Outcome Document; High-Level Outcomes and Executive Brief
- Key message: WSIS is "an existing and proven framework" for implementing global digital commitments — avoid duplication with GDC

**2025 (7–11 July, Geneva — WSIS+20 High-Level Event 2025):**

- Co-located with the AI for Good Global Summit — a landmark convergence
- Chaired by H.E. Solly Malatsi (Minister of Communications and Digital Technologies, South Africa)
- Keynote by H.E. Alar Karis (President, Republic of Estonia)
- Themes: AI governance; generative AI; digital public infrastructure; cybersecurity and digital trust; 5G and next-generation connectivity; space technologies; smart sustainable cities; digital public goods; youth and gender in emerging tech
- 19 WSIS Prizes 2025 winners recognised; 71 champions; 270 nominees
- WSIS+20 Ministerial Roundtable: 59 ministers discussed national digital priorities, emerging tech, and WSIS beyond 2025; emphasised avoiding duplication with GDC, strengthening WSIS architecture
- Chair's Summary called for: enhanced progress-tracking mechanisms; clear targets under WSIS Action Lines; WSIS as the central multistakeholder platform grounded in dialogue, human rights, and sustainability

### Key Focus Areas for the Review

Based on preparatory process consultations and ITU reporting, the six focus areas for the WSIS+20 Review are:

1. **Universal and meaningful connectivity** — Assessing progress in expanding connectivity and bridging the digital divide. With 68% of the world population online (2024) but 2.6 billion still offline, and 1.8 billion offline in rural areas, the gap remains significant.

2. **Capacity building and digital skills** — Evaluating initiatives promoting digital literacy and ensuring equitable access to digital technologies. AI skills now a prominent sub-theme.

3. **Cybersecurity** — Reviewing efforts to enhance cybersecurity and build trust in digital technologies, including child online protection, CIRT development, and national cybersecurity strategies.

4. **Emerging technologies** — Addressing AI, IoT, 5G, quantum computing, and their implications for development, governance, ethics, and inclusion.

5. **Digital cooperation** — Strengthening multistakeholder partnerships; coordinating global efforts; relationship between WSIS and GDC.

6. **Sustainable use of technology** — Analysing ICT's impact on global goals; e-waste; green ICT; tech for climate action.

### WSIS and the Global Digital Compact

The adoption of the **Global Digital Compact (GDC)** at the UN Summit of the Future (September 2024) is a major contextual factor for the WSIS+20 Review. The GDC:
- Was adopted alongside the Pact for the Future
- Emphasises universal connectivity, digital inclusion, AI governance, and multistakeholder collaboration — closely aligned with WSIS principles
- Raises the question of how WSIS and GDC relate institutionally

ITU's position, reflected throughout the WSIS+20 Report, is that WSIS and GDC are complementary, not duplicative. The UNGIS-WSIS-GDC Matrix maps UN-led initiatives against GDC objectives, WSIS Action Lines, and SDGs to demonstrate alignment. The 2025 Ministerial Roundtable explicitly emphasised "avoiding duplication and strengthening the WSIS architecture as the foundation for global digital policy."

### The Case for WSIS Beyond 2025

The WSIS+20 Report articulates ITU's argument for continuing the WSIS process beyond 2025:
- **Active and proven:** WSIS Stocktaking database has over 19,000 entries; WSIS Prizes has attracted 9,000+ submissions; WSIS Forum gathers 50,000+ participants annually
- **Adaptive:** The framework has absorbed AI, IoT, 5G, and other technologies without requiring fundamental restructuring
- **Multistakeholder legitimacy:** The model — involving governments, private sector, civil society, academia, and technical community — is well-established and trusted
- **GDC alignment:** WSIS provides the ground-level implementation architecture that can carry the GDC's aspirations into practice
- Chair's reports from WSIS+20 High-Level Events in 2024 and 2025 notably underline the need for WSIS to continue beyond 2025

### Progress on Geneva Plan of Action Targets (2003–2024)

The WSIS+20 Report provides a baseline comparison:

| Target | 2003 Baseline | 2024 Status | Remaining Gaps |
|---|---|---|---|
| Connect villages with ICTs | 16% of world online | 68% online (5.5 billion); 9 billion+ mobile broadband subscriptions | 2.6 billion offline; 1.8 billion in rural areas |
| Connect schools | Lack of global data | 40% primary, 50% lower secondary, 65% upper secondary connected (2023) | Data lacking for 65% of schools across 143 countries |
| Connect libraries | Lack of global data | 369,000+ libraries with Internet access (IFLA) | 87% of libraries still offline |
| Connect health centres | Lack of global data | EHR uptake mainly in developed countries; telemedicine expanded (especially COVID-19) | Significant disparities between developed/developing and urban/rural hospitals |
| Connect governments | Lack of global data | ~98% of countries offer e-government services | African, LDC, SIDS regions below global EGDI average |
| Adapt curricula | Lack of global data | 54% of countries have digital skill standards for curricula | Teacher concerns about AI, screen addiction; deepening inequalities risk |
| Access to ICTs for half world | 16% online | 68% online; 96% covered by mobile broadband network | 2.6 billion offline; gender gap: 189 million more men than women online |
| All world languages online | English dominance | 400+ languages fully accessible online | 6,600+ languages not fully represented online (7,000 total in use) |

### Outcome: UNGA Resolution A/RES/80/173

The high-level meeting adopted [[unga-res-80-173]] on 17 December 2025. Key provisions beyond what was already described in the Key Facts:

**Affirmed continuity:** Para. 2 reaffirmed all four WSIS documents (Geneva Declaration, [[geneva-plan-of-action]], Tunis Commitment, [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]]). Para. 18 reaffirmed Action Lines as "the main framework." Para. 104 confirmed Tunis Agenda arrangements provide "a sound foundation for continued implementation."

**IGF made permanent (para. 99):** The IGF "shall be made a permanent forum of the United Nations" with secretariat hosted by UN DESA on a stable and sustainable basis — ending the periodic mandate renewal cycle that had been a source of institutional uncertainty since 2006.

**AI governance (paras. 84–87):** Notes GDC as primary AI governance document; calls for AI capacity-building fellowship; notes establishment of Independent International Scientific Panel on AI; welcomes Global Dialogue on AI Governance (2026).

**Environment (paras. 42–47):** Substantially new thematic section addressing e-waste, energy consumption from data centres and AI, sustainable digital product design, and critical minerals.

**Internet shutdowns (para. 76):** Calls on all stakeholders to refrain from "Internet shutdowns and measures that target Internet access" — the strongest anti-shutdown language in any WSIS outcome document.

**WSIS+30 (para. 127):** Next WSIS review requested for 2035 — extending the WSIS mandate for another decade.

## Open Questions
- The IGF is now "permanent" but sustainable funding was still to be proposed — has the 80th UNGA session funded the IGF's secretariat adequately?
- The WSIS-GDC joint implementation roadmap was due to CSTD 2026 — does it resolve the GDC/WSIS overlap question in practice?
- The review did not produce new quantitative connectivity targets beyond the 2030 school connectivity commitment — is this a gap, or was the WSIS-SDG-GDC framework sufficient?
- The WSIS+30 review (2035) will be the first review under the new biennial reporting cycle — will the action line roadmaps make accountability more substantive?
- How did the north-south co-facilitator pairing (Kenya-Albania) shape the final outcome text?

# C10: Ethical Dimensions of the Information Society

## Overview
Action Line C10 is the most philosophically grounded of the WSIS Action Lines, addressing the ethical principles, human values, and rights frameworks that should govern the Information Society. It applies universally held human values — human dignity, justice, solidarity, equity, the common good — to the digital environment, and calls for preventing abusive uses of ICTs including surveillance, exploitation, discrimination, and censorship.

C10 is co-facilitated by [[unesco]] and ECOSOC, reflecting the breadth of its mandate: UNESCO carries the ethics and human rights in communication dimension; ECOSOC provides the broader social and economic ethics framework. UNESCO's groundbreaking Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence (2021) — the first global standard-setting instrument on AI ethics — is now the primary C10 implementation vehicle and has given the Action Line new relevance in the AI era.

C10 was the most forward-looking Action Line when adopted in 2003. Many of the ethical issues it anticipates — surveillance, algorithmic discrimination, digital rights, the ethics of AI — have only become acute a decade or more later. C10 is now perhaps the most urgently relevant Action Line for the AI era, addressing questions that were barely imagined when WSIS met in Geneva.

## Detail

### Scope and Purpose

C10 addresses four major ethical dimensions:

**1. The common good:** The Information Society must be oriented toward the common good — not merely commercial advantage or government interest. ICT governance processes must weigh public benefit. This principle underlies the WSIS commitment to universal access, public domain information, and multistakeholder governance.

**2. Human rights:** Existing human rights frameworks — freedom of expression, right to privacy, right to education, right to participate in cultural life, right to development — apply fully in the digital environment. C10 calls for a human-rights-based approach to ICT governance and application.

**3. Prevention of abusive uses:** ICTs can be weaponised for exploitation, trafficking, child abuse, surveillance, hate speech, and discrimination. C10 mandates preventive approaches — technical, legal, and ethical — to these harms.

**4. Ethics frameworks:** Development of codes of conduct, professional ethics standards, and governance frameworks applying ethical principles to ICT design, deployment, and governance. This anticipates what would become AI ethics, algorithmic accountability, and responsible technology frameworks.

### Key Geneva Plan of Action Provisions

The C10 section called for:
- Developing standards and guidelines for ethical conduct in the Information Society
- Applying universal ethical principles to ICT design, development, and use
- Preventing abusive uses of ICTs, including for trafficking, child exploitation, and hate speech
- Ensuring that ethics research and education keep pace with technological change
- Promoting a culture of non-violence in the Information Society
- Integrating ethics in ICT curricula and professional training

The text explicitly invokes "universally shared values" — a formulation that accommodated different political and cultural perspectives on what counts as ethical while maintaining a core commitment to human dignity.

### UNESCO's AI Ethics Recommendation (2021)

The most significant C10 implementation development since 2003 is UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of Artificial Intelligence, adopted by the UNESCO General Conference in November 2021 — the first global standard-setting instrument on AI ethics. The Recommendation:
- Applies to the entire AI system lifecycle (design, development, deployment, use, governance)
- Addresses bias, discrimination, privacy, transparency, accountability, human oversight
- Calls for impact assessments of AI systems
- Addresses the specific concerns of marginalised groups, including gender, race, disability
- Has been used by 40+ countries as a reference for national AI ethics frameworks

This Recommendation is UNESCO's primary vehicle for C10 in the AI era and gives C10 concrete implementation substance it previously lacked.

### From ICT Ethics to AI Ethics

C10's scope has expanded enormously from the relatively narrow "prevent abusive uses" framing of 2003 to encompass the full AI ethics agenda:

**2003–2015:** Focus on preventing child exploitation online, cybercrime ethics, professional codes for ICT workers, hate speech online.

**2016–2020:** Algorithmic discrimination in criminal justice, credit, employment; surveillance capitalism and data ethics; GDPR and data rights; facial recognition ethics.

**2021–2025:** Generative AI and deepfakes; autonomous weapons systems ethics; AI in judicial and administrative decisions; AI labour displacement ethics; AI environmental ethics; AI in healthcare decision-making. UNESCO's Recommendation (2021) and the inter-agency AI ethics working group (IAWG-AI, co-chaired by UNESCO and [[itu]]) are the principal C10 institutional vehicles.

### SDG Connections

C10's ethical framework connects to rights-based SDG dimensions:
- **SDG 10.2** (Social, economic, political inclusion): Preventing algorithmic discrimination; inclusive AI
- **SDG 10.3** (Eliminating discriminatory laws and policies): Digital discrimination; biased AI systems
- **SDG 16.7** (Inclusive decision-making): Ethics of algorithmic public decisions
- **SDG 16.10** (Access to information; fundamental freedoms): Digital rights and privacy
- **SDG 5.1** (Gender discrimination): AI gender bias; online gender-based violence
- **SDG 17.6–17.8** (Technology cooperation, capacity building): Equitable AI development

### WSIS+20 Mandate (2025)

[[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025) addressed ethics prominently:
- **Para. 18:** Reaffirmed C10 and all Action Lines as "the main framework"
- **Para. 84–87** (AI governance): Noted the GDC as the primary AI governance document; welcomed UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of AI; called for the establishment of an Independent International Scientific Panel on AI; welcomed the Global Dialogue on AI Governance (2026)
- **Para. 112:** Human rights and gender equality to be explicitly embedded in all Action Lines — a provision most directly relevant to C10
- **Para. 113:** C10 facilitators to develop implementation roadmap by CSTD 2027

### Facilitation Meeting History

12 facilitation meetings were held under C10 (2006–2021), co-facilitated by [[unesco]] and ECOSOC. Meeting themes evolved from online content ethics and professional codes (2006–2010), through privacy and surveillance (2011–2015), to AI ethics and data ethics (2016–2021). The IAWG-AI (Inter-Agency Working Group on AI) co-chaired by UNESCO and ITU has provided additional coordination infrastructure for C10 in the AI era.

## Open Questions
- C10's ethical framework is grounded in "universally shared values" — but AI ethics debates reveal deep disagreements about what counts as fairness, transparency, and accountability across cultures and political systems. How should C10 address this pluralism?
- The GDC is now designated as the "primary" AI governance document — does this risk marginalising C10's ethics mandate, or does it give C10 a clearer complementary role?
- UNESCO's Recommendation on AI Ethics is a UNESCO instrument, not a WSIS document — should the 2027 C10 implementation roadmap formally incorporate it?
- C10 addresses "prevention of abusive uses" — does this adequately cover the systemic harms of AI systems that are not intentionally abusive but produce discriminatory or harmful outcomes?

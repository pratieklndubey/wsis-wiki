# Internet Governance

## Overview
Internet governance is defined by the Working Group on Internet Governance (WGIG, 2005) as "the development and application by Governments, the private sector and civil society, in their respective roles, of shared principles, norms, rules, decision-making procedures, and programmes that shape the evolution and use of the Internet." This definition — still the standard reference in UN contexts — was significant for recognising that governance is not only technical or intergovernmental: it involves all stakeholder groups.

Internet governance encompasses a complex ecosystem: the technical layers of infrastructure; the logical layers of protocols, addressing, and naming; the policy layers of regulation, legislation, and norms; and the political layers of international power and sovereignty. The question of who governs the internet — and through what mechanisms — became the most contentious issue in the WSIS process (2003–2005), driving the creation of the [[wgig]] and ultimately the [[internet-governance-forum]] (IGF).

Since Tunis (2005), the internet governance landscape has continued evolving: the IANA transition (2016) removed formal U.S. government oversight of [[icann]]; the Snowden revelations (2013) intensified debates about surveillance and sovereignty; AI governance has emerged as a new frontier; and internet fragmentation ("splinternet") has become a central concern. The WSIS+20 outcome ([[unga-res-80-173]], 2025) reaffirmed the multistakeholder model while strengthening the IGF as a permanent forum.

## Key Facts
- **WGIG working definition (2005):** Development and application of shared principles, norms, rules, decision-making procedures, and programmes shaping the internet's evolution and use — by governments, private sector, and civil society in their respective roles
- **Key WSIS outcome for internet governance:** [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] (2005) — created IGF; affirmed distinct government roles; retained ICANN structure
- **IANA Transition:** October 1, 2016 — [[icann]] freed from U.S. DOC contract; multistakeholder community stewardship
- **Internet shutdowns:** 54 countries conducted shutdowns in 2024; 296 cumulative shutdowns recorded
- **AI governance:** OECD expanded AI governance resources in 2024; GDC para. 56 establishes AI governance mechanisms
- **Fragmentation concern:** UNESCO ROAM principles (2015); NETmundial Statement (2014): internet must remain "globally coherent, interconnected, stable, unfragmented, scalable and accessible"

## Detail

### Benkler's Three Layers of Internet Governance

Legal scholar Yochai Benkler identified three layers at which governance acts on the internet:

1. **Physical infrastructure layer:** Transmission channels, cables, routers, spectrum — subject to telecommunications regulation, investment policy, and access rules
2. **Code/logical layer:** Protocols and software controlling how infrastructure operates — TCP/IP, DNS, BGP; governed by technical bodies (IETF, [[icann]])
3. **Content layer:** Information flowing through the network — subject to copyright, defamation, hate speech regulation, surveillance law, and platform governance

This framework clarifies why "internet governance" cannot be reduced to any single layer: regulating content without addressing infrastructure, or managing naming/addressing without content policy, produces incomplete results. WSIS Action Lines (especially C2, C5, C6) address different layers.

### Central Debates

**Centralization vs. Multistakeholder Model**

The fundamental debate in internet governance since 2003 has been between:
- **Multilateral/intergovernmental model:** Nation-states should control internet governance through UN-based processes; reflects traditional international law sovereignty
- **Multistakeholder model:** Internet should be governed by all stakeholders — governments, private sector, civil society, and technical community — in their respective roles; reflects internet's technical origins and distributed character

Key moments:
- **IBSA Proposal (2011):** India, Brazil, South Africa proposed a "UN Committee on Internet-Related Policy" — a fully intergovernmental body to replace ICANN/WSIS structures. Not adopted.
- **Montevideo Statement (October 7, 2013):** The I* organisations ([[icann]], IETF, IAB, [[isoc]], [[rirs]]/NRO) expressed "strong concern" over internet surveillance and fragmentation; called for "accelerating the globalization of ICANN and IANA functions." This positioned the technical community firmly against both unilateral U.S. control and intergovernmental replacement.
- **NetMundial (April 2014):** Brazil-hosted multistakeholder conference; produced "Multistakeholder Statement of São Paulo" on internet governance principles; Russia, India, Cuba dissented from key provisions. Demonstrated both the breadth and the limits of multistakeholder consensus.
- **IANA Stewardship Transition (October 1, 2016):** Contract ended; stewardship transferred from U.S. government to the "global Internet community" — represented by ICANN's multistakeholder structure. The biggest structural change in internet governance since ICANN's founding.

**Fragmentation vs. Internet Universality**

A second major debate concerns whether the internet should remain a single global network or whether national "internet sovereignty" is legitimate:

- **UNESCO ROAM Principles (2015):** The internet should be "(i) Human Rights-based, (ii) Open, (iii) Accessible to all, and (iv) Nurtured by Multistakeholder participation"
- **NETmundial Statement (2014):** Internet should remain "globally coherent, interconnected, stable, unfragmented, scalable and accessible"
- **Counter-position:** China (Great Firewall), Russia (sovereign internet law), and others have asserted national sovereignty over internet infrastructure and content, creating concerns about "splinternet" — multiple incompatible national internets

[[unga-res-80-173]] (2025) explicitly affirmed commitment to "a single, open and interoperable Internet" as a WSIS+20 outcome.

### Emerging Issues

**Internet Shutdowns:**
State-ordered shutdowns of internet or mobile services to prevent protests, control information, or enforce curfews have escalated dramatically:
- 54 countries conducted shutdowns in 2024 (296 cumulative historical shutdowns documented)
- Myanmar led with 85 outages in a single year
- Internet shutdowns directly undermine WSIS objectives of universal connectivity and human rights online
- [[global-digital-compact]] (GDC) para. 15 explicitly addresses freedom of expression online

**AI Governance:**
Artificial intelligence has emerged as the dominant new internet governance challenge:
- OECD expanded AI governance resources in 2024
- [[global-digital-compact]] para. 56 establishes: (a) International Scientific Panel on AI; (b) Global Dialogue on AI Governance
- WSIS Action Line C5 (Security) and C6 (Enabling Environment) have AI governance implications
- The WSIS+20 High-Level Events 2024–2025 co-located with AI for Good Global Summit, signalling convergence of WSIS and AI governance communities

**Cybersecurity:**
- **Paris Call for Trust and Security (November 12, 2018):** French-led initiative with 1,000+ signatories (governments, companies, civil society) on norms for responsible state behaviour in cyberspace
- **Tallinn Manual 2.0 (2017):** Academic expert analysis of how international law applies to cyber operations
- WSIS Action Line C5 addresses cybersecurity; ITU manages Global Cybersecurity Agenda (GCA) and CIRT development
- [[global-digital-compact]] Objective 4 addresses cybersecurity

### WSIS Contributions to Internet Governance

WSIS made several lasting contributions to internet governance:

1. **WGIG Definition:** The first internationally recognised definition of internet governance; still cited in all subsequent UN frameworks
2. **Internet Governance Forum:** The IGF — created by the Tunis Agenda — has become the principal multistakeholder forum for internet governance dialogue; now permanent per [[unga-res-80-173]]
3. **Stakeholder framework:** Formalised four stakeholder groups (governments, private sector, civil society, technical community) as the model for inclusive governance
4. **Tunis Agenda para. 35:** Affirmed that "Internet governance, carried out according to the Geneva principles, should constitute a core issue of the Information Society agenda"
5. **Separation of policy and operations:** Tunis Agenda affirmed governments' role in public policy while delegating technical operations to private/technical bodies — a compromise that enabled both ICANN continuity and governmental participation

## Open Questions
- Can the multistakeholder model remain legitimate as internet governance increasingly intersects with national security and democratic processes?
- Internet fragmentation: is it a realistic threat or a rhetorical framing? What concrete indicators would show the internet is fragmenting?
- Should AI governance follow the WSIS/IGF multistakeholder model, or does its potential impact require stronger intergovernmental structures?
- Internet shutdowns are growing — can any governance mechanism (WSIS, GDC, IGF) create accountability for states that shut down the internet?

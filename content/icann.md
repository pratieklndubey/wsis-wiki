# ICANN (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers)

## Overview
ICANN is the global nonprofit organization responsible for coordinating the Internet's domain name system (DNS) and IP address allocation. Founded on September 30, 1998, and headquartered in Los Angeles, California, it operates as a "multistakeholder" body managing critical internet infrastructure previously controlled by the U.S. government.

Before ICANN's creation, computer scientist Jon Postel performed these functions at UCLA as a side task to his research work, with informal oversight from the U.S. Department of Defense. The U.S. Department of Commerce (DOC) initiated privatization and established ICANN as its successor, retaining oversight through a series of contracts and agreements until October 1, 2016, when ICANN formally transitioned to global multistakeholder stewardship.

ICANN sits at the intersection of technical internet infrastructure and internet governance politics. The question of whether U.S. control over ICANN constituted inappropriate unilateral control over a global resource was a central controversy throughout [[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]] (2003) and [[wsis-phase-ii-tunis-2005]] (2005), and drove the creation of the [[wgig]] (Working Group on Internet Governance).

- **Core mandate:** DNS root zone, IP address allocation, gTLD/ccTLD policy, domain dispute resolution

## Detail

### Core Functions

ICANN manages the Internet Assigned Numbers Authority (IANA) functions, which include:

1. **DNS root zone management:** Maintains the root zone of the Domain Name System, which is the authoritative database of top-level domains (.com, .org, country codes, etc.)
2. **IP address allocation:** Allocates IPv4 and IPv6 address blocks to the five [[rirs]] (Regional Internet Registries), which in turn distribute them to ISPs and end users
3. **Generic and country-code TLD policy:** Sets standards and policies for generic top-level domains (gTLDs) and the framework within which country-code TLDs (ccTLDs) operate
4. **Domain name dispute resolution:** Manages the Uniform Domain-Name Dispute-Resolution Policy (UDRP), the international mechanism for resolving trademark conflicts in domain names

### Governance Structure

ICANN operates through a distinctive "bottom-up, consensus-driven" multistakeholder model:

**Board of Directors (16 members):**
- 8 members nominated through the Nominating Committee process
- 6 members nominated by Supporting Organizations (2 each)
- 1 At-Large representative
- 1 CEO/President (non-voting)

**Supporting Organizations:**
- **Generic Names Supporting Organization (GNSO):** Policy for generic TLDs (gTLDs)
- **Country Code Names Supporting Organization (ccNSO):** Policy for country-code TLDs (ccTLDs)
- **Address Supporting Organization (ASO):** IP address policy; liaised through the Number Resource Organization ([[rirs]])

**Advisory Committees:**
- **Governmental Advisory Committee (GAC):** Representatives of 179 member states; advises the Board on public policy issues
- **At-Large Advisory Committee (ALAC):** Represents end-users globally
- **Security and Stability Advisory Committee (SSAC):** Technical security advice
- **Root Server System Advisory Committee (RSSAC):** DNS root server operators

### Historical Development

**1998 — Formation**
ICANN emerged from a U.S. Department of Commerce initiative (the "Green Paper" and "White Paper" of 1998) to privatize internet addressing management. Jon Postel had previously managed IANA functions informally since the early ARPANET era. ICANN was incorporated as a California nonprofit on September 30, 1998.

**1998–2016 — DOC Oversight Period**
For 18 years, ICANN operated under U.S. DOC oversight through a series of agreements:
- Memorandum of Understanding (1998-2006)
- Joint Project Agreement (2006-2009)
- Affirmation of Commitments (2009-2016): Confirmed multistakeholder governance model; established independent accountability reviews
- March 10, 2016: Historic agreement reached to remove DOC control by October 1, 2016

**October 1, 2016 — IANA Transition**
Following years of planning by the "IANA Stewardship Transition Coordination Group" (coordinating input from thousands of stakeholders), the DOC contract for IANA functions expired and was not renewed. ICANN became fully accountable to the global internet community — no single government.

### ICANN and WSIS

ICANN's governance status was the single most contentious issue at [[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]] (2003). The Geneva phase "failed to agree on the future of Internet governance" regarding ICANN — specifically whether U.S. control was legitimate. This directly motivated the creation of the [[wgig]] (Working Group on Internet Governance), mandated to develop options for oversight reform.

The WGIG (2004–2005) proposed four different oversight models, but the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] (2005) ultimately declined to transfer oversight from the U.S. to a multilateral body. Instead, it created the [[internet-governance-forum]] as a non-binding dialogue forum, and ICANN retained its existing structure — but with an enhanced Governmental Advisory Committee role.

Key WSIS/ICANN debates:
- **Governments vs. private sector control:** Many governments (particularly developing countries, the EU, and IBSA) sought greater intergovernmental oversight; the U.S. maintained that privatised, multistakeholder control was preferable
- **NetMundial (April 2014, Brazil):** Following the Snowden revelations, Brazil hosted a multistakeholder conference on internet governance reform; produced "Multistakeholder Statement of São Paulo" on internet governance principles; Russia, China, India, and Cuba dissented
- **IANA Transition (October 1, 2016):** The major governance reform achieved post-WSIS; ICANN is now accountable to its multistakeholder community rather than the U.S. government exclusively

### TLD Expansion and Controversies

Beginning in 2011, ICANN launched a major new generic TLD (gTLD) program, allowing nearly arbitrary domain extensions (e.g., .google, .amazon, .dubai) with an application fee of $185,000. This was controversial:
- Major corporations including Coca-Cola and Samsung initially opposed expansion due to defensive registration costs
- Resulted in over 1,200 new gTLDs being delegated
- Critics argued this primarily enriched registrars while confusing users

## Open Questions
- Does the IANA transition truly represent full internationalisation, or is ICANN's California incorporation and U.S. headquarters still a form of structural U.S. influence?
- The GAC (179 states) is advisory only — should governments have stronger formal roles in ICANN decisions?
- The proliferation of gTLDs — has it enhanced the internet's diversity, or mainly increased costs for trademark holders?
- How will ICANN adapt as AI and quantum computing affect DNS security requirements?

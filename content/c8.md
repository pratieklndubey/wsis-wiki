# C8: Cultural Diversity and Identity, Linguistic Diversity and Local Content

## Overview
Action Line C8 addresses the cultural and linguistic dimensions of the Information Society — ensuring that the digital environment reflects and sustains human cultural diversity rather than homogenising it around a few dominant languages and cultural forms. C8 recognises that language exclusion is a form of digital exclusion: if content is unavailable in a person's language, their ability to participate in the Information Society is fundamentally constrained even if they have connectivity and devices.

C8 is facilitated solely by [[unesco]], which holds the principal UN mandate on culture, education, and communication. UNESCO's 2005 Convention on the Protection and Promotion of the Diversity of Cultural Expressions — adopted in the same year as the WSIS Tunis phase — is the primary international legal instrument supporting C8's goals.

The Action Line addresses four interconnected concerns: cultural heritage digitisation (preserving what exists), multilingual internet (ensuring future content is linguistically diverse), local content creation (developing-country communities as content producers, not just consumers), and indigenous peoples' digital rights (protecting traditional knowledge and languages in the digital environment).

## Detail

### Scope and Purpose

C8 addresses four major dimensions:

**1. Cultural heritage digitisation:** Converting analogue cultural heritage — manuscripts, photographs, artefacts, oral traditions, film archives — into digital formats for preservation and access. UNESCO leads global efforts through the Memory of the World Programme, Europeana (European heritage), and national digitisation initiatives.

**2. Multilingual internet:** Ensuring that all the world's languages can be represented and used online. This includes internationalised domain names (IDNs — domain names in non-Latin scripts such as Arabic, Chinese, Devanagari), multilingual email, and support for low-resource languages in AI and machine translation.

**3. Local content creation:** Supporting communities in developing countries and minority communities globally to produce digital content in their own languages and cultural contexts. This addresses the asymmetry in which global internet content is dominated by English-language, Western-produced material.

**4. Indigenous peoples' rights:** Protecting traditional knowledge, cultural expressions, and languages in digital environments; ensuring free, prior, and informed consent for digitisation of indigenous cultural heritage; recognising indigenous peoples' digital sovereignty.

### Key Geneva Plan of Action Provisions

The C8 section called for:
- Promoting respect for cultural identity, diversity, and traditions in the Information Society
- Strengthening cultural and linguistic diversity online, including through multilingual content and localisation
- ICT tools supporting language learning and preservation, including for endangered languages
- Digitisation of cultural heritage with appropriate intellectual property arrangements
- Indigenous peoples' right to participate in information society decision-making
- North-South cooperation for local content development

### UNESCO Convention on Cultural Diversity (2005)

UNESCO's Convention on the Protection and Promotion of the Diversity of Cultural Expressions (adopted October 2005) is the primary international instrument implementing C8. It entered into force in 2007 and recognises:
- Culture as both a driver of and condition for sustainable development
- States' rights to adopt cultural policies, including for digital environments
- The need for special measures to protect and promote cultural diversity in the face of globalisation

The Convention's Digital Roadmap (2021) specifically addresses cultural diversity in digital platforms — a direct C8 implementation tool for the streaming and platform era.

### Multilingualism and AI

The rise of large language models (LLMs) has transformed the multilingualism debate. By 2025:
- Major LLMs perform well in English and other high-resource languages but poorly in the 6,000+ low-resource languages
- AI translation tools (DeepL, Google Translate) have improved dramatically for European languages but remain inadequate for many African, Pacific, and indigenous languages
- The training data used for AI systems is predominantly in English, reproducing and potentially amplifying existing linguistic inequalities
- UNESCO's Recommendation on the Ethics of AI (2021) specifically addresses linguistic diversity and calls for AI systems to support multilingualism

These developments have given C8 new urgency and new operational focus in the WSIS+20 context.

### SDG Connections

- **SDG 4.7** (Global citizenship, cultural diversity in education): Local content and multilingual education
- **SDG 11.4** (Strengthen cultural heritage protection and safeguarding): Heritage digitisation
- **SDG 8.3, 8.9** (Creative industries; sustainable tourism): Local digital content as economic asset
- **SDG 12.b** (Sustainable tourism monitoring): ICTs tracking cultural tourism
- **SDG 6.b** (Local participation in water/sanitation): ICT for indigenous community governance (cross-cutting)

### WSIS+20 Mandate (2025)

[[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025) addressed cultural and linguistic diversity:
- **Para. 18:** Reaffirmed C8 and all Action Lines as "the main framework"
- **Para. 50–55** (Information and knowledge): Called for multilingual AI applications; recognised that AI risks amplifying linguistic inequality; called for enhanced support for low-resource languages
- **Para. 113:** C8 facilitators to develop implementation roadmap by CSTD 2027

### Facilitation Meeting History

11 facilitation meetings were held under C8 (2006–2021), all facilitated by [[unesco]]. Fewer meetings than most other Action Lines reflects both UNESCO's focused mandate and the political complexity of cultural diversity negotiations. Meeting themes have evolved from heritage digitisation and multilingualism (2006–2015) to indigenous digital rights, platform cultural policies, and AI and language diversity (2016–2021).

## Open Questions
- Only 400 languages are fully accessible online despite 7,000 languages being in active use — what would it take to make C8's multilingualism goal achievable by 2035 (WSIS+30)?
- AI language models replicate and may intensify linguistic inequality — should C8 facilitators work with AI developers on low-resource language inclusion requirements?
- The digitisation of indigenous cultural heritage is often done without free, prior, and informed consent — how should C8 address intellectual property rights of indigenous communities in digital contexts?
- Platform algorithms that favour high-engagement content may systematically disadvantage local and minority cultural content — does C8 have adequate tools to address this?

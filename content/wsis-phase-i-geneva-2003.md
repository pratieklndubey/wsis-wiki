# WSIS Phase I — Geneva 2003

## Overview
The first phase of the World Summit on the Information Society (WSIS Phase I) was held in Geneva, Switzerland from 10–12 December 2003. It brought together heads of state, government ministers, senior UN officials, private sector leaders, and civil society representatives from over 175 countries — making it one of the most broadly attended UN-convened summits in history at that time. The meeting was the first phase of a unique two-phase UN World Summit structure, with the second phase planned for Tunis in 2005.

The Geneva phase produced two core outcome documents: the [[geneva-declaration-of-principles]] and the [[geneva-plan-of-action]]. The Declaration established a shared vision of a people-centred, inclusive, and development-oriented Information Society grounded in human rights and the UN Charter. The Plan of Action translated this vision into eleven concrete Action Lines (C1–C11), connectivity targets for 2015, a Digital Solidarity Agenda, and a mandate for the Working Group on Internet Governance (WGIG).

The Summit was convened under the auspices of the United Nations, with [[itu]] (International Telecommunication Union) serving as the lead UN agency. The idea for WSIS was first proposed by Tunisia at the 1998 ITU Plenipotentiary Conference — seven years before the summit took place.

## Detail

### Origins and Mandate

The concept of a UN World Summit on the Information Society originated with Tunisia's proposal at the 1998 ITU Plenipotentiary Conference in Minneapolis. The United Nations General Assembly formally authorised WSIS through Resolution 56/183 (21 December 2001), calling for a two-phase summit and placing [[itu]] in the lead management role.

The UNGA resolution was significant: it gave WSIS a formal UN mandate while designating a specialised technical agency (ITU) as the operational lead — an arrangement that would shape the summit's character throughout. [[itu]]'s prominence ensured strong governmental representation (ITU is an intergovernmental organisation) while also channelling the technical community's expertise.

### The Preparatory Process (PrepComs)

WSIS Phase I was preceded by three preparatory committee meetings (PrepCom-1, PrepCom-2, and PrepCom-3) held in Geneva between 2002 and 2003. These were open to all UN member states plus accredited civil society and private sector entities. The preparatory process was notable for allowing extensive civil society participation — including at the plenary — which was unusual for UN summits at the time.

The preparatory process was also contentious. Internet governance emerged as a deeply divisive issue, with some governments (notably the United States and European Union) insisting that existing arrangements (centred on [[icann]] and the US Department of Commerce oversight) should continue, while developing countries, led by the G77, pushed for greater intergovernmental control over Internet resources. The compromise language in the Declaration (paras. 48–50) mandated the WGIG to study the issue and report back to the Tunis phase.

### Summit Outcomes

**Geneva Declaration of Principles** [[geneva-declaration-of-principles]]:
- 67 paragraphs, three sections
- Committed world leaders to an inclusive, people-centred Information Society
- Eleven key principles covering infrastructure, access, capacity building, security, enabling environment, ICT applications, culture, media, ethics, and international cooperation
- Recognised [[itu]]'s central role (para. 64)
- Mandated WGIG (para. 50)

**Geneva Plan of Action** [[geneva-plan-of-action]]:
- Eleven Action Lines (C1–C11) assigned to UN agency moderators
- Ten indicative connectivity targets to be achieved by 2015
- Digital Solidarity Agenda with proposals for a voluntary Digital Solidarity Fund
- Mandated review of financial mechanisms by end of 2004
- Called for composite ICT Development Index
- Set up follow-up and evaluation framework

### Multistakeholder Character

WSIS Phase I was notable for its attempt to operationalise a multistakeholder model at the highest level. Civil society organisations, academic institutions, and private sector entities were accredited as observers and allowed to speak in plenary — a departure from the standard UN intergovernmental model. However, civil society groups frequently expressed frustration that their input was not adequately reflected in the negotiated texts. A parallel Civil Society Declaration was produced alongside the official documents, capturing perspectives that did not make it into the official text.

The debate about how "genuine" the multistakeholder participation was remains open, but the Geneva phase did establish participation norms that were carried forward into the Tunis phase and into the IGF.

### Legacy

The Geneva phase launched more than 2,500 projects documented in the WSIS Stocktaking database (maintained by [[itu]]). Its Action Lines framework provided a template for organising ICT-for-development activity at national and international levels for the next decade. The WGIG mandate it created led directly to the Internet Governance Forum ([[internet-governance-forum]]) created by the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] in 2005.

Twenty years later, the WSIS Stocktaking database has grown to **19,000+ entries** and the annual [[wsis-forum]] gathers 50,000+ participants. The ten indicative targets from the [[geneva-plan-of-action]] have seen uneven progress: Internet usage rose from 16% (2005) to 68% (2024), mobile broadband subscriptions surpassed 9 billion, and ~98% of countries offer e-government services — yet 2.6 billion people remain offline (1.8 billion in rural areas), 87% of libraries are still not connected, and only 400 of 7,000 world languages are fully accessible online. The Geneva phase established the benchmarks that continue to drive the [[wsis-review-2025]] (WSIS+20) agenda.

## Open Questions
- How representative was civil society participation in reality? Which voices were included vs. excluded?
- How did the parallel Civil Society Declaration differ from the official outcome, and did it have any policy impact?
- Was the compromise on Internet governance (para. 50) a deliberate deferral, and was it expected that the Tunis phase would fully resolve it?
- To what degree did the 175-country participation translate into genuine implementation at the national level?

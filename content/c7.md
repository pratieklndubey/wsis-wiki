# C7: ICT Applications — Benefits in All Aspects of Life

## Overview
Action Line C7 is the broadest and most operationally diverse of the WSIS Action Lines, covering ICT applications across eight distinct sectors of social and economic life. While other Action Lines address infrastructure ([[c2]]), skills ([[c4]]), or governance ([[c6]]), C7 asks: *what can ICTs actually do* for human welfare in concrete sectors? The answer, as structured by the [[geneva-plan-of-action]], encompasses government, business, education, health, employment, the environment, agriculture, and scientific research.

C7 is effectively eight sub-Action Lines — each with its own facilitating arrangement, its own set of UN agencies, and its own set of SDG connections. This makes C7 simultaneously the most impactful Action Line (because it covers the most ground) and the most complex to coordinate. The eight sub-themes collectively involve more than 15 UN agencies, including WHO, FAO, ILO, WMO, UNESCO, UNDP, UNCTAD, WTO, UPU, UN-Habitat, ICAO, and UNIDO.

By 2025, every C7 sub-theme had been transformed by two decades of technological change. E-government now encompasses AI-driven public services; e-health encompasses telemedicine and AI diagnostics; e-learning encompasses AI tutors and personalised learning; e-agriculture encompasses precision farming and drone surveillance. C7 is where the broad WSIS vision meets the concrete reality of digital transformation.

## Key Facts
- **Full name:** ICT Applications: Benefits in All Aspects of Life
- **Code:** C7 (Action Line 7)
- **Origin:** [[geneva-plan-of-action]], Section C, para. C7 (adopted 12 December 2003)
- **Sub-themes:** 8 (E-Government, E-Business, E-Learning, E-Health, E-Employment, E-Environment, E-Agriculture, E-Science)
- **Facilitating organisations:** See sub-themes below; primary: [[undp]], [[itu]], [[unesco]], WHO, FAO, ILO, WMO, [[unctad]], WTO, UPU, UN-Habitat, ICAO, UNIDO
- **WSIS+20 mandate:** Implementation roadmap for each sub-theme, due to CSTD 30th session (2027)

## Detail

---

### C7: E-Government

**Scope:** Implementing transparent, efficient, accessible, and democratic public administration through ICT. Covers online public services, digital identity, open government data, anti-corruption tools, and parliamentary ICT systems.

**Facilitators:** [[undp]] / [[itu]]
**Current lead:** Deniz Susar, Governance and Public Administration Officer, UN DESA

**Key provisions (Geneva Plan of Action):**
- Implement e-government strategies at national level; online access to public information
- Promote universal online access to public documents
- Develop minimum standards for digital government services
- Foster participation in decision-making processes through ICT

**SDG connections:** 9.c; 16.6 (effective institutions); 16.7 (inclusive decision-making); 16.10 (public access to information); 17.8 (technology bank)

**WSIS-SDG Matrix targets:** 9.c, 16.6, 16.7, 16.10, 17.8

**Progress by 2025:** ~98% of countries offer some e-government services; UN e-Government Survey (DESA) tracks the E-Government Development Index (EGDI); African, LDC, and SIDS regions remain below the global average. AI-enabled public services (chatbots, automated decision-making) represent the frontier.

---

### C7: E-Business

**Scope:** Promoting international trade and e-commerce, including support for SMEs to participate in digital markets; digital payment systems; electronic contracts and signatures; consumer protection in online trade.

**Facilitators:** WTO / [[unctad]] / [[itu]] / UPU
**Current leads:** Radka Sibille (UPU); James Howe (ITC); Scarlett Fondeur Gil (UNCTAD)

**Key provisions (Geneva Plan of Action):**
- Support for developing-country SMEs to access digital markets
- Enable cross-border e-commerce through harmonised legal frameworks
- Postal services as e-commerce delivery infrastructure
- Consumer protection frameworks for online transactions

**SDG connections:** 1.4, 2.3, 5.b, 8.3, 8.9, 8.10, 9.3, 17.11

**Progress by 2025:** Global e-commerce exceeded $6 trillion annually; developing-country participation growing but still limited by logistics, payments, and legal barriers. UNCTAD's eTrade for All initiative is a primary C7 E-Business implementation vehicle.

---

### C7: E-Learning

**Scope:** Delivering education and training through ICT; improving lifelong learning; expanding access to quality education in remote and underserved areas; open educational resources; AI-enhanced learning.

**Facilitators:** [[unesco]] / [[itu]] / UNIDO
**Current lead:** Zeynep Varoglu, UNESCO

**Key provisions (Geneva Plan of Action):**
- Adapt teaching to the Information Society; integrate ICT in school curricula
- Develop online learning platforms for distance education
- Open educational resources accessible in local languages
- ICT training for teachers

**SDG connections:** Goal 4 (all targets) — E-Learning maps to the entirety of SDG 4 (Quality Education)

**Progress by 2025:** Massively accelerated by COVID-19 (2020–2022); MOOCs, LMS platforms, and AI tutors now mainstream in developed countries; significant digital divides in e-learning access persist between and within countries. UNESCO's Open Educational Resources (OER) recommendation (2019) is the authoritative C7 E-Learning policy framework.

---

### C7: E-Health

**Scope:** Using ICTs to deliver reliable, affordable, and quality health care; protecting health data privacy; telemedicine; health management information systems; mHealth (mobile health); AI diagnostics.

**Facilitators:** WHO / [[itu]]
**Current leads:** Hani Eskandar, Digital Services Senior Coordinator, ITU; Derrick Muneene, Unit Head, Capacity Building, Digital Health and Innovation, WHO

**Key provisions (Geneva Plan of Action):**
- Deploy ICTs to improve health systems and delivery; protect health data privacy
- Promote telemedicine for rural and remote communities
- Support WHO e-health standards and interoperability
- Address counterfeit medicines and public health information online

**SDG connections:** 1.3, 1.4, 1.5; 2.1, 2.2; Goal 3 (all targets), 3.3, 3.8; 5.6, 5.b; 17.8, 17.19

**Progress by 2025:** COVID-19 dramatically accelerated telemedicine adoption globally; AI diagnostics (imaging, genomics) are transforming clinical practice; electronic health records (EHRs) widespread in developed countries but limited in LDCs; health data privacy concerns have intensified with large-scale digital health platforms.

---

### C7: E-Employment

**Scope:** Using ICTs to create employment, enable remote work, support labour market information systems, and address the digital workplace transformation. Includes telework, platform-based work (gig economy), and digital skills for employment.

**Facilitators:** ILO / [[itu]]
**Current lead:** Maria Prieto, Employment and Future of Work Specialist, ILO

**Key provisions (Geneva Plan of Action):**
- Promote telework as an employment opportunity, particularly for rural areas
- Use ICT for labour market information systems
- Address ICT-enabled displacement of workers
- Promote gender equality in ICT-based employment

**SDG connections:** 4.5; 8.5 (full employment); 10.2; 12.6; 17.9

**Progress by 2025:** COVID-19 made remote/telework mainstream; the gig economy (platform-based work) has created new forms of employment and new labour rights challenges; AI automation threatens significant segments of formal employment — a major new C7 E-Employment challenge not anticipated in 2003.

---

### C7: E-Environment

**Scope:** Using ICTs for environmental monitoring, protection, and sustainable resource management; addressing the environmental footprint of ICTs themselves (e-waste, energy consumption); climate modelling and early warning systems.

**Facilitators:** WHO / WMO / [[unep]] / UN-Habitat / [[itu]] / ICAO
**Current leads:** Enrico Fucile (WMO); Maritza Delgado (ITU); Magda Biesiada (UNEP)

**Key provisions (Geneva Plan of Action):**
- Use ICTs for environmental monitoring, including climate change and biodiversity
- Develop ICT tools for natural disaster early warning and response
- Address e-waste and promote sustainable ICT production and use
- Apply ICTs in smart sustainable cities

**SDG connections:** 9.4; 11.6, 11.b; 13.1, 13.3, 13.b; Goal 14; Goal 15

**Progress by 2025:** Climate monitoring systems (WMO, Copernicus) have advanced dramatically; e-waste reached 62 million tonnes annually by 2022 (Global E-waste Monitor); data centre energy consumption has become a major concern, particularly with AI workloads. [[unga-res-80-173]] para. 42–47 added new language on ICT's environmental footprint — the first time a WSIS outcome document addressed data centre sustainability explicitly.

---

### C7: E-Agriculture

**Scope:** Using ICTs to improve agricultural productivity, food security, and rural livelihoods; providing farmers with market information, weather forecasts, and agronomic advice; precision agriculture; extension services via mobile.

**Facilitators:** FAO / [[itu]]
**Current leads:** Henry Burgsteden, Senior Innovation Officer, FAO; Hani Eskandar, Digital Services Senior Coordinator, ITU

**Key provisions (Geneva Plan of Action):**
- Deploy ICTs for agricultural information systems accessible to rural farmers
- Promote agri-market price information services via SMS and mobile
- Develop digital agricultural extension services
- Support food security monitoring through ICTs

**SDG connections:** 1.5; 2.3, 2.4, 2.a; 3.d; Goal 4; 5.5; 8.2; 9.1, 9.c; 12.8; 13.1, 13.3; 17.16, 17.17

**Progress by 2025:** Mobile agricultural information services (e-Choupal in India, ESOKO in Ghana) have reached millions of farmers; precision agriculture using drones, sensors, and satellite imagery is transforming commercial farming; smallholder farmers in LDCs remain the hardest to reach and benefit least from digital agriculture tools.

---

### C7: E-Science

**Scope:** Supporting research through high-speed connectivity; open access to scientific data; cross-border research collaboration platforms; citizen science; computational resources for developing-country research institutions.

**Facilitators:** [[unesco]] / [[itu]] / [[unctad]]
**Current lead:** Amal Kasry, UNESCO

**Key provisions (Geneva Plan of Action):**
- Connect research and development institutions and universities to high-speed networks
- Promote open access to scientific publications and data
- Support developing-country research capacities
- Use ICTs for citizen science and public participation in research

**SDG connections:** 1.5; 4.7; 6.1, 6.a; 7.a; 13.1, 13.2, 13.3; 14.a; 15.9; 17.6, 17.7

**Progress by 2025:** Open access to scientific publishing has advanced significantly (Plan S, Diamond OA models); research data management frameworks emerging; AI is transforming scientific research (AlphaFold, drug discovery); developing-country research institutions remain hampered by bandwidth costs and infrastructure gaps.

## C7 Overall SDG Connections

Across all eight sub-themes, C7 connects to nearly every SDG. Key cross-cutting links:
- **SDG 4** (Education): E-Learning sub-theme maps to all SDG 4 targets
- **SDG 3** (Good Health): E-Health sub-theme maps to virtually all SDG 3 targets
- **SDG 2** (Zero Hunger): E-Agriculture directly addresses food security
- **SDG 8** (Decent Work): E-Employment addresses the digital future of work
- **SDG 13** (Climate Action): E-Environment addresses ICT for climate monitoring and resilience

## WSIS+20 Mandate (2025)

[[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025) addressed C7 themes across multiple sections:
- **Para. 18:** Reaffirmed all C7 sub-themes as part of the main Action Line framework
- **Para. 48–49** (E-Health): Welcomed AI in health; called for equitable access to digital health tools
- **Para. 42–47** (Environment/E-Environment): First explicit WSIS mention of data centre energy; calls for sustainable ICT production and design
- **Para. 113:** C7 facilitators to develop implementation roadmaps for each sub-theme by CSTD 2027

## Connections
- Defined in [[geneva-plan-of-action]] Section C (2003); facilitators confirmed in [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] Annex (2005)
- The most agency-diverse Action Line: 15+ UN agencies involved across eight sub-themes
- Each sub-theme underpinned by [[c2]] (infrastructure) and [[c4]] (skills)
- [[c5]] security provisions apply to all C7 applications — e-health data privacy; e-government authentication
- E-Environment sub-theme overlaps with [[unep]]'s broader environmental mandate
- Facilitation meetings held annually at [[wsis-forum]]; coordination through [[ungis]]
- Partner2Connect (P2C) and ITU Giga directly implement C7 sub-theme goals

## Open Questions
- C7 has 8 sub-themes with 15+ facilitating agencies — is this architecture coherent, or has coordination become so complex that it reduces effectiveness?
- AI applications are transforming all eight C7 sub-themes simultaneously — should C7 be restructured to have a cross-cutting AI dimension?
- E-Environment now encompasses both "ICTs for the environment" (monitoring, early warning) and "environment for ICTs" (e-waste, data centre energy) — should these be treated as separate sub-themes?
- The gig economy as a C7 E-Employment phenomenon was not anticipated in 2003 — has ILO facilitation of C7 adequately addressed platform labour rights?

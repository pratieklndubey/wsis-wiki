# C9: Media

## Overview
Action Line C9 recognises the essential role of all forms of media — print, broadcast, online — in the Information Society. It addresses media freedom, independence, pluralism, and diversity as foundational conditions for a genuinely open information environment. Without free and independent media, the technical infrastructure of the Information Society risks serving power rather than people.

C9 is facilitated solely by [[unesco]], which holds the UN mandate on freedom of information and the safety of journalists through its Communication and Information Sector. UNESCO's landmark indicators for media development (MIDs) and its extensive work on journalist safety directly implement C9.

The Action Line has grown in importance since 2003 as the digital transformation has fundamentally disrupted media business models, enabled information operations and disinformation at scale, and created new threats to press freedom. The "traditional media" focus of the 2003 text has expanded to encompass online news, social media platforms, and the algorithmic mediation of public information.

## Detail

### Scope and Purpose

C9 addresses five dimensions of media in the Information Society:

**1. Media freedom and independence:** Protecting journalists and media organisations from political pressure, censorship, and violence. Freedom of expression online as a digital rights issue. UNESCO's World Press Freedom Index (co-produced with Reporters Without Borders) is the primary measurement tool.

**2. Media pluralism and diversity:** Ensuring diversity of media ownership; preventing media concentration and monopolisation; supporting community media, public service broadcasting, and minority-language media; promoting gender balance in media leadership and content.

**3. Reducing international imbalances:** The north-south media imbalance — most global news flow is from North to South, not the reverse — was a longstanding concern from the New World Information and Communication Order (NWICO) debate of the 1970s–1980s. WSIS C9 continues this agenda.

**4. Media literacy:** The capacity of individuals to critically evaluate media content, identify disinformation, and participate in media production. UNESCO's Media and Information Literacy (MIL) curriculum is the principal implementation tool.

**5. Professional development:** Training and capacity building for journalists and media professionals, particularly in developing countries; ethical standards for digital journalism; journalist safety protocols.

### Key Geneva Plan of Action Provisions

The C9 section called for:
- Encouraging media to contribute to an open, transparent, and diverse Information Society
- Promoting role of all media in combating poverty and promoting development
- Reducing barriers to international flow of information (while respecting IP and privacy)
- Support for training media professionals for the Information Society
- Promoting international South-South media cooperation

Notably, the Geneva Plan of Action text does *not* explicitly endorse freedom of the press by name — a reflection of the political sensitivity of press freedom at WSIS, where some governments resisted strong language. UNESCO's supplementary work on press freedom goes beyond what WSIS C9 formally mandates.

### The Digital Disruption of Media

The period since 2003 has seen radical disruption of media:

**2003–2010:** Rise of blogs and citizen journalism; Wikipedia; early social media (Facebook 2004, Twitter 2006)

**2011–2015:** Social media as news source; Arab Spring and digital media; paywall models emerging; decline of print advertising revenue

**2016–2020:** Disinformation as an electoral and public health threat; "fake news" political weaponisation; platform algorithmic amplification of sensational content; journalist doxxing and online harassment

**2021–2025:** AI-generated content and deepfakes; information operations at scale; decline of local news ecosystems; media viability crisis in developing countries; UNESCO's Internet Universality Indicators (ROAM-X) and MIL frameworks as responses

### Safety of Journalists

Journalist safety is a major C9 implementation theme. UNESCO coordinates the UN Plan of Action on the Safety of Journalists (adopted 2012), which operationalises C9 in the most concrete way: tracking attacks on journalists, promoting impunity investigations, and building regional journalist safety networks. UNESCO reports approximately 55–65 journalists killed annually (2020–2024), with online harassment — particularly targeting women journalists — reaching epidemic proportions.

### SDG Connections

- **SDG 5.b** (Women's technology empowerment): Gender and media; online harassment of women journalists
- **SDG 9.c** (Universal ICT access): Online media access — C9's media reach requires connectivity
- **SDG 12.8** (Consumer information for sustainable lifestyles): Media role in sustainability communication
- **SDG 16.10** (Public access to information; fundamental freedoms): The core SDG for press freedom and free media

### WSIS+20 Mandate (2025)

[[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025) addressed media:
- **Para. 18:** Reaffirmed C9 and all Action Lines as "the main framework"
- **Para. 62–67** (Freedom of expression, information, and media): Called for ensuring press freedom online; condemned online harassment of journalists; recognised disinformation as a threat to information ecosystems; called for platform transparency in content moderation; endorsed media and information literacy
- **Para. 113:** C9 facilitators to develop implementation roadmap by CSTD 2027

### Facilitation Meeting History

15 facilitation meetings were held under C9 (2006–2021), all facilitated by [[unesco]]. Meetings have tracked the evolution of media: from traditional media development (2006–2010) through digital journalism and media viability (2011–2015) to disinformation, platform governance, and journalist safety (2016–2021). UNESCO's annual World Press Freedom Day (3 May) provides a recurring international platform for C9 themes.

## Open Questions
- The 2003 Geneva text was deliberately vague on press freedom — has C9 facilitation been able to build a more robust press freedom agenda despite this weak textual foundation?
- Disinformation, information operations, and AI-generated content were not contemplated in 2003 — does C9's mandate adequately address these threats, or is new architecture needed?
- The collapse of local news ecosystems in many countries represents a media pluralism crisis — how should C9 address the structural economic problems of media viability?
- Content moderation by platforms now has more impact on media access than most national regulations — should C9 engage more directly with platform governance as a media freedom issue?

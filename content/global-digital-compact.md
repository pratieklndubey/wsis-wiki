# Global Digital Compact (GDC)

## Overview
The Global Digital Compact (GDC) is a landmark UN agreement on digital cooperation adopted at the **UN Summit of the Future** on 22–23 September 2024, alongside the Pact for the Future. It represents the most significant multilateral digital governance commitment since the WSIS process itself (2003–2005). The GDC was developed through a years-long consultative process convened by the UN Secretary-General, involving governments, civil society, private sector, academia, and technical organisations.

The GDC reinforces and echoes core WSIS principles — particularly universal connectivity, digital inclusion, and multistakeholder collaboration — while also addressing newer challenges that post-date WSIS, notably AI governance, data governance, and digital public infrastructure. The key institutional question for the [[wsis-review-2025]] (WSIS+20) is how the GDC and the WSIS framework relate: are they complementary or duplicative? [[itu]] and other WSIS lead agencies argue strongly for complementarity, positioning WSIS as the ground-level implementation mechanism for GDC aspirations.

## Key Facts
- **Adopted:** 22–23 September 2024, at the UN Summit of the Future, New York
- **Document symbol:** A/79/L.2 (pre-adoption draft); adopted as annex to the Pact for the Future
- **Adopted alongside:** The Pact for the Future
- **Initiated by:** UN Secretary-General António Guterres (within the context of the "Our Common Agenda" report, 2021)
- **Co-facilitators:** Sweden and Rwanda (led the negotiation process)
- **WSIS foundation:** GDC explicitly built on WSIS Geneva Declaration (fn18) and Tunis Agenda (fn19)
- **SDG foundation:** 2030 Agenda is foundational document (preamble)
- **5 objectives:** (1) Close digital divides; (2) Expand inclusion in digital economy; (3) Inclusive, open, safe, secure digital space; (4) Responsible data governance; (5) International AI governance
- **13 guiding principles:** Inclusive participation; development-oriented/2030 Agenda; international human rights law; gender equality; environmental sustainability; equitable distribution; accessible/affordable data/tech; interoperability; safe/secure/trustworthy AI; creativity/competition; multistakeholder model; means of implementation; forward-looking
- **Relationship to WSIS:** Explicitly reinforces WSIS principles; builds on WSIS Geneva Declaration and Tunis Agenda; GDC para. 68 commits to building on IGF and WSIS Forum
- **Relationship to SDGs:** GDC is positioned as a digital implementation mechanism for the 2030 Agenda
- **AI governance:** GDC Objective 5 — Independent International Scientific Panel on AI (para. 56a) and Global Dialogue on AI Governance (para. 56b)
- **Implementation map:** Para. 71 mandated SG to produce implementation map; produced by WGDT (see [[gdc-implementation-map]])
- **High-Level Review:** Para. 74 — High-Level Review at 82nd GA session (2027)
- **CSTD role:** Para. 70 — CSTD reviews GDC/WSIS implementation jointly
- **UNGIS-WSIS-GDC Matrix:** Developed by 28 UN agencies to map UN initiatives against GDC objectives, WSIS Action Lines, and SDGs (see [[ungis-gdc-matrix]])
- **ITU's role in GDC follow-up:** ITU, as the UN specialised agency for ICTs, has a central role in implementing GDC commitments

## Detail

### Background and Process

The GDC grew out of the UN Secretary-General's "Our Common Agenda" report (2021), which called for a new social contract for the digital age. A co-facilitation process led by Sweden and Rwanda produced the negotiated text, with extensive consultations including a dedicated Technology Track within the Summit of the Future process.

The process explicitly engaged WSIS stakeholders and was informed by two decades of WSIS Action Line implementation, the WSIS Forum, and the [[internet-governance-forum]] — reflecting institutional continuity with the WSIS multistakeholder model.

### 13 Guiding Principles (Para. 8)

The GDC is governed by 13 principles (para. 8a–m):
1. **Inclusive participation** — all people, governments, private sector, civil society, technical community, academia
2. **Development-oriented** — aligned with and advancing the 2030 Agenda
3. **International human rights law** — binding on all digital governance
4. **Gender equality** — throughout all objectives
5. **Environmental sustainability** — digital must serve climate and nature goals
6. **Equitable distribution** — especially for developing countries and LDCs
7. **Accessible and affordable data and technology** — no-one left behind
8. **Interoperability** — of systems, standards, platforms
9. **Safe, secure and trustworthy AI** — human-centric, risk-managed
10. **Creativity and competition** — open innovation ecosystems
11. **Multistakeholder model** (para. k) — explicitly referenced
12. **Means of implementation** — financing, technology transfer, capacity building
13. **Forward-looking** — adaptive governance for emerging technologies

### Five Objectives: Full Structure

#### Objective 1: Close Digital Divides and Accelerate SDG Progress
Sub-themes:
- **Connectivity** (a–g): Universal access, blended financing, resilient infrastructure, schools/hospitals (Giga), sustainability, vulnerable populations, gender
- **Digital literacy and skills** (a–h): National strategies, marginalized groups, public officials, reskilling, STEM for women/girls
- **Digital public goods and DPI** (a–f): Open-source, open data, open AI, safeguards for Digital Public Infrastructure (DPI), best practice sharing

#### Objective 2: Expand Inclusion in and Benefits from the Digital Economy
Sub-themes:
- **Enabling environment** (a–d): MSMEs, open/fair digital markets, national digital assessments, technical assistance to developing countries
- **Digital entrepreneurship** (e–j): Supply chains, technology transfer, South-South cooperation, innovation/entrepreneurship ecosystems (especially women and youth)

#### Objective 3: Foster an Inclusive, Open, Safe and Secure Digital Space
Sub-themes:
- **Human rights** (a–d): Legislation compliant with international human rights law; safeguards; rights of the child; free flow of information
- **Internet governance** (a–d): Open/global/interoperable Internet; support IGF (para. 28: IGF as primary multistakeholder platform); international cooperation; refrain from Internet shutdowns
- **Digital trust and safety** (a–f): Online safety; child protection; privacy and freedom of expression; surveillance/encryption compliance; measure and counter online violence; child sexual abuse material
- **Information integrity** (a–e): Media/information literacy; diverse media ecosystems; counter misinformation; crisis information access

#### Objective 4: Advance Responsible, Equitable and Interoperable Data Governance
Sub-themes:
- **Data privacy and security** (a–e): Privacy guidelines, national frameworks, individual consent rights, safe data practices, skilled workforces
- **Data exchanges and standards** (a–c): Bias-free standards, definitions/classifications for interoperability, standards for data reuse
- **Data for the SDGs** (a–c): Financing for development data, disaggregated monitoring data, disaster early warning systems
- **Cross-border data flows**: Consultations on cross-border governance frameworks
- **Interoperable data governance**: IOP recommendations (para. 48: CSTD to establish dedicated working group on data governance)

#### Objective 5: Enhance International Governance of AI
Key commitments:
- **Para. 55:** Unique opportunity to advance international AI governance
- **Para. 56(a):** Establish Independent International Scientific Panel on AI — balanced geographic representation; evidence-based; draws on existing national/regional initiatives
- **Para. 56(b):** Initiate Global Dialogue on AI Governance — involving Governments and all stakeholders, held in margins of existing UN conferences
- **Para. 57:** President of GA to appoint co-facilitators to identify terms of reference for Panel and Dialogue
- **Para. 58:** Call on standards development organizations to develop interoperable AI standards upholding safety, reliability, sustainability, human rights
- **Para. 59:** Safe, trustworthy AI preserving linguistic and cultural diversity
- **Para. 60:** International AI partnerships for education, open models, training data access
- **Para. 61:** Leverage UN mechanisms to bridge AI divides; facilitate AI applications; build high-performance computing capacity in developing countries
- **Para. 62:** North-South/South-South cooperation for representative datasets and affordable compute

### Relationship to WSIS

The relationship between GDC and WSIS has been a central topic of both the WSIS+20 preparatory process and the GDC negotiations. Several key points:

**Reinforcement:** The GDC explicitly builds on WSIS principles. As ITU's WSIS+20 Report states: "The Global Digital Compact adopted along with the Pact for the Future at the UN Summit of the Future in 2024 reinforces WSIS principles, emphasizing universal connectivity, digital inclusion, and multistakeholder collaboration."

**Complementarity, not duplication:** Both the WSIS Forum 2024 Chair's Summary and the 2025 ministerial roundtable emphasised "avoiding duplication of efforts while acknowledging existing mechanisms." The UNGIS-WSIS-GDC Matrix was developed precisely to demonstrate how the two frameworks align rather than duplicate.

**Implementation architecture:** ITU and other WSIS lead agencies position WSIS — with its 19,000+ stocktaking projects, annual Forum, Action Line roadmaps, and 20 years of institutional memory — as the natural ground-level implementation mechanism for GDC commitments. "WSIS can drive on-the-ground implementation of these worthy aims, further strengthening the drive to build a digital future for all."

**AI governance:** The GDC's attention to AI governance is the most significant new dimension it brings beyond WSIS's original 11 Action Lines. While the WSIS Forum has discussed AI since 2016 (AI featured in the WSIS Forum themes from 2016 onwards), the WSIS Action Lines do not explicitly cover AI. The GDC effectively extends the WSIS architecture into AI territory, and ITU's AI for Good initiative provides one implementation pathway.

**WSIS+20 integration:** A key focus area for the [[wsis-review-2025]] is "the importance of integrating the implementation of the Global Digital Compact commitments into the WSIS architecture in order to avoid duplications and ensure a cohesive and consistent approach to digital cooperation."

### GDC Follow-up and Review Provisions

Key follow-up paragraphs from the official text:
- **Para. 64:** Implement GDC at national, regional, and global levels
- **Para. 65:** Private sector, technical community, civil society contributions are "fundamental and irreplaceable"
- **Para. 66:** SG to put in place modalities for voluntary endorsement of the Compact
- **Para. 67:** Recognize importance of financing; call on private sector and philanthropic actors to pledge financial support
- **Para. 68:** Build on WSIS processes — IGF and WSIS Forum — for implementation; look forward to WSIS+20 review (2025)
- **Para. 69:** Recognize role of UN regional economic commissions and UN country teams
- **Para. 70:** CSTD role in reviewing GDC/WSIS implementation
- **Para. 71:** SG to provide implementation map (→ [[gdc-implementation-map]])
- **Para. 72:** Strengthen UN system-wide coordination; SG to submit proposal for office incorporating SG's Envoy on Technology (→ ODET)
- **Para. 73:** Role of HLPF and ECOSOC in reviewing GDC progress
- **Para. 74:** High-Level Review of the GDC at 82nd GA session (2027); co-facilitators to be appointed at 81st GA session

### UNGIS-WSIS-GDC Matrix

[[ungis]], with [[itu]] as permanent secretariat, produced the [[ungis-gdc-matrix]] (WSIS Process and 2030 Agenda – GDC Matrix) — a comprehensive three-way mapping developed jointly by 28 UN agencies, covering:
- Every GDC commitment (5 objectives, 74 paragraphs)
- Against WSIS Action Lines (C1–C11)
- Against SDGs (1–17)
- With named existing UN mechanisms for each link

This matrix demonstrates that the overwhelming majority of GDC commitments already have institutional homes within the WSIS framework.

### AI for Good and GDC

ITU's **AI for Good** platform is directly relevant to GDC implementation in the AI governance domain:
- Guided by ITU Plenipotentiary Resolution 214 (Bucharest, 2022) and amplified by UNGA Resolutions 78/265 and 78/311
- Co-organized by ITU with 40+ UN partner agencies; co-convened with Switzerland
- Addresses: identifying trustworthy AI applications; building AI skills and standards; advancing AI governance for sustainable development
- The IAWG-AI (Inter-Agency Working Group on AI), co-chaired by ITU and UNESCO, coordinates 50 UN entities and developed the UN System-wide Ethical Principles for AI

### WSIS+20 Resolution: GDC Coherence Provisions

The WSIS+20 outcome, [[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025), established a formal coherence framework between WSIS and the GDC:

- **Para. 106:** Calls for "coherence in the implementation of the outcomes of WSIS, the 2030 Agenda, the Pact for the Future and the GDC, by building synergies between them to ensure effective implementation and avoiding wasteful duplication of resources and decision-making processes"
- **Para. 109:** Notes establishment of **Office for Digital and Emerging Technologies (ODET)** to facilitate UN system-wide coordination on digital cooperation and support GDC follow-up
- **Para. 120:** "Ensure coherence between the implementation of World Summit outcomes and the GDC to avoid duplication and enhance synergies"
- **Para. 122:** Requests UNGIS to prepare a **joint WSIS-GDC implementation road map** for consideration by CSTD at its 29th session (2026); road map to "strengthen coherence between WSIS and GDC, ensuring a unified, resource-efficient approach to digital cooperation"
- **Para. 125:** IGF to contribute to GDC review at UNGA 82nd session (2027)

The resolution also directly references GDC on AI governance (paras. 84–87) and data governance (paras. 81–83) — treating the GDC as the primary framework for those newer domains, while WSIS Action Lines remain the primary framework for ICT-for-development implementation.

## Connections
- Reinforces and extends [[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]] and [[wsis-phase-ii-tunis-2005]] principles
- Formally integrated with WSIS framework by [[unga-res-80-173]] (paras. 106, 120, 122)
- Joint WSIS-GDC implementation roadmap mandated (CSTD 2026)
- Complementary to [[internet-governance-forum]] and [[wsis-action-lines]]
- Implemented in part through [[itu]] and [[wsis-forum]] mechanisms
- Aligned with [[sdgs]] and the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development
- UNGIS (with [[itu]] as permanent secretariat) bridges WSIS and GDC
- AI governance dimension connects to ITU's AI for Good initiative; GDC is now primary AI governance document in WSIS+20 outcome

## Open Questions
- The joint WSIS-GDC implementation roadmap was due to CSTD 2026 — does it resolve the duplication question in practice, or does it remain aspirational?
- How will the GDC's AI governance provisions be operationalised — through existing WSIS mechanisms, new ODET-led bodies, or a hybrid?
- The GDC was adopted with some controversy (particularly over AI provisions and data governance) — how do those tensions affect its interaction with WSIS?
- What accountability mechanisms will ensure GDC commitments are implemented?
- The IGF is now permanent (A/RES/80/173, para. 99) and will contribute to GDC review (2027) — does this resolve the concern that GDC bypassed the IGF?

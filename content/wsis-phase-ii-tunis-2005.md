# WSIS Phase II — Tunis 2005

## Overview
The second and final phase of the World Summit on the Information Society (WSIS Phase II) was held in Tunis, Tunisia from 16–18 November 2005. It completed the unique two-phase summit process that began with [[wsis-phase-i-geneva-2003]] in December 2003. The Tunis phase was tasked with resolving two issues that Geneva had left open: (1) financing mechanisms for bridging the digital divide, and (2) Internet governance — including what to do with the contentious question of who controls the Internet's critical resources.

The Tunis phase produced two outcome documents: the [[tunis-commitment]] (a political reaffirmation and extension of Geneva's vision) and the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] (the operational document). The Tunis Agenda's most lasting achievement was the creation of the **Internet Governance Forum (IGF)** — a new multistakeholder body mandated to discuss Internet policy issues, convened by the UN Secretary-General, which has met annually since 2006.

The Tunis Summit also formalised the implementation and follow-up machinery for WSIS outcomes, assigned [[itu]], [[unesco]], and [[undp]] as lead facilitating agencies, and called for a UNGA review of WSIS outcomes in 2015 (WSIS+10). It declared 17 May as **World Information Society Day**.

## Key Facts
- **Dates:** 16–18 November 2005
- **Location:** Tunis, Tunisia
- **Host government:** Tunisia
- **Lead agency:** [[itu]] (International Telecommunication Union)
- **Summit Secretary-General:** Yoshio Utsumi (also ITU Secretary-General)
- **Key outputs:** [[tunis-commitment]], [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]]
- **Most significant creation:** [[internet-governance-forum]] (IGF) — mandated in para. 72 of the Tunis Agenda
- **First IGF meeting:** Athens, Greece, 2006
- **World Information Society Day:** 17 May, declared by UNGA following Tunis Agenda para. 121
- **WSIS+10 review:** Called for in para. 111 of the Tunis Agenda; held in 2015
- **Lead facilitating agencies post-Tunis:** [[itu]], [[unesco]], [[undp]]

## Detail

### Background and Context

By 2004–2005, the Internet governance debate had intensified significantly. The Working Group on Internet Governance (WGIG) — mandated by Geneva — had produced its report in 2005, proposing several models for Internet governance reform, including options for new intergovernmental oversight. The US government, facing the prospect of losing its privileged position over ICANN and the root zone, issued a strong statement in June 2005 asserting its intent to maintain control of critical Internet infrastructure. The EU proposed a compromise "cooperation model" that eventually influenced the Tunis outcome.

The Tunis phase also had to navigate the contentious question of the Digital Solidarity Fund, Internet connectivity pricing asymmetries that disadvantaged developing countries, and broader questions about how WSIS outcomes would be implemented across the UN system.

### The Preparatory Process

Three PrepComs (and intersessional meetings) were held between 2003 and 2005. The Internet governance debates dominated PrepCom-3 in particular, with intense negotiations producing the compromise text in the Tunis Agenda. Civil society remained engaged throughout, though frustration with governmental dominance continued.

### Summit Outcomes

**Tunis Commitment** [[tunis-commitment]]:
- 40 paragraphs
- Political reaffirmation of Geneva Declaration and Plan of Action
- Extended commitments on gender divide, ICT for peace, children's protection
- Acknowledged the 2005 World Summit Outcome (Millennium+5 review)
- Welcomed the Digital Solidarity Fund established after Geneva

**Tunis Agenda for the Information Society** [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]]:
- Four sections (A–D) plus Annex
- Financial mechanisms section responding to TFFM report; welcomed the Digital Solidarity Fund
- Internet Governance section: working definition; multistakeholder roles; cybersecurity; Internet connectivity costs; enhanced cooperation process; created the IGF
- Implementation section: national, regional, and international mechanisms; ECOSOC oversight; CEB UN group on the Information Society; [[itu]]/[[unesco]]/[[undp]] as lead agencies
- Annex: official list of UN agency facilitators/moderators for all Action Lines (C1–C11)
- Para. 121: called for 17 May as World Information Society Day

### Internet Governance: The Key Compromise

The central achievement of the Tunis phase was threading the needle on Internet governance. The outcome:

1. **Did not** transfer control of the Internet's critical infrastructure (root zone, IP addressing, domain names) away from existing bodies, including [[icann]] under US government supervision
2. **Did** create the [[internet-governance-forum]] — a new multistakeholder forum for discussing Internet policy issues, with no oversight or binding functions
3. **Did** call for an "enhanced cooperation" process to enable governments to play equal roles in international Internet public policy — a concession to developing countries
4. **Did** address Internet connectivity costs and called on [[itu]] to study the issue urgently

The IGF was carefully designed to satisfy competing interests: it gave developing countries and civil society a seat at the table in meaningful Internet policy discussions, while preventing any formal shift of control from existing technical bodies. Critics called it a "talking shop"; supporters argued it created a unique and valuable space for cross-sectoral dialogue.

### Tunisia as Host: Human Rights Context

The choice of Tunisia as the host for a summit focused on freedom of information and the free flow of information was itself controversial. Tunisia under President Zine El Abidine Ben Ali had one of the most restrictive press environments in the world. Civil society groups and journalists raised objections throughout the preparatory process and at the summit itself. The irony of affirming freedom of expression (Tunis Commitment, para. 4) in a country that systematically suppressed it was noted widely. This tension was never fully reconciled and remains a criticism of the WSIS process.

### Implementation Framework Established

The Tunis phase set up:
- **National level:** Governments to establish national e-strategy implementation mechanisms; 100 countries to have e-strategies by 2010
- **Regional level:** UN Regional Commissions to hold regional WSIS follow-up activities
- **International level:** ECOSOC to oversee system-wide follow-up; CEB to create a UN group on the Information Society; [[itu]], [[unesco]], and [[undp]] as the three lead facilitating agencies; UNGA to review WSIS outcomes in 2015

### Legacy

The Tunis phase's primary legacy is the [[internet-governance-forum]], which has met annually since Athens 2006 and become a central venue for multi-stakeholder Internet policy discussions. WSIS outcomes also fed directly into the annual WSIS Forum (held at [[itu]] Geneva), the WSIS+10 review (2015), and subsequent processes such as the [[global-digital-compact]] negotiations.

The "enhanced cooperation" process proved less successful — it generated reports and consultations but no fundamental reform of Internet governance arrangements. The ITU, through its Connect the World initiative and continuing role as Action Line facilitator, remained the dominant institutional home for WSIS implementation.

## Open Questions
- How did civil society groups assess the Tunis outcomes — genuine progress or captured process?
- Has the IGF fulfilled the mandate given to it in para. 72 of the Tunis Agenda, particularly on capacity building for developing country participation?
- The enhanced cooperation process stalled — what are the prospects for reviving intergovernmental coordination on Internet public policy?
- How has the relationship between WSIS and the Sustainable Development Goals been operationalised since 2015?
- What was the significance of Tunisia's political context on the summit's discussions of freedom of expression?

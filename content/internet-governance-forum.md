# Internet Governance Forum (IGF)

## Overview
The Internet Governance Forum (IGF) is the UN's annual multistakeholder forum for open policy dialogue on Internet governance issues. It was created by paragraph 72 of the [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] (18 November 2005), which mandated the UN Secretary-General to convene the first meeting by mid-2006. The inaugural IGF was held in **Athens, Greece, in October–November 2006** — Greece having offered to host it in paragraph 82 of the Tunis Agenda.

The IGF is unique in the UN system: it is deliberately **non-binding, non-decisional**, and has no oversight functions over the Internet or existing Internet governance bodies. It convenes governments, private sector entities, civil society organisations, academia, and technical organisations on equal terms to discuss public policy issues related to the Internet. It has met annually since 2006 and has become one of the most significant venues for Internet policy discussion globally.

The IGF was designed as the central compromise of the most contested issue at WSIS Phase II: developing countries wanted greater intergovernmental control over the Internet, while the US, EU, private sector, and technical community resisted any shift away from existing arrangements centred on ICANN. The IGF satisfied both sides by creating a forum for discussion without creating a new regulatory body.

On **17 December 2025**, the UNGA made the IGF a **permanent forum of the United Nations** (A/RES/80/173, para. 99) — ending nearly 20 years of periodic mandate renewal uncertainty.

## Key Facts
- **Created by:** [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]], paragraph 72, 18 November 2005
- **First meeting:** Athens, Greece, 30 October – 2 November 2006
- **Meeting frequency:** Annual
- **Character:** Non-binding, non-decisional, neutral, non-duplicative, open
- **Secretariat:** UN Office in Geneva (UN DESA); Chengetai Masango, Head of Secretariat
- **MAG:** Multistakeholder Advisory Group; 56 members (governments, private sector, civil society, technical/academic); appointed by UN Secretary-General; meets 3× yearly in Geneva; one-third renewed annually
- **NRI ecosystem:** 170+ national, regional, and youth IGFs as of 2025 (A/RES/80/173, para. 97)
- **Permanent status:** Granted by [[unga-res-80-173]] para. 99 (17 December 2025) — secretariat hosted by UN DESA on stable, sustainable basis
- **Working definition of Internet governance** (Tunis Agenda para. 34): "the development and application by governments, the private sector and civil society, in their respective roles, of shared principles, norms, rules, decision-making procedures, and programmes that shape the evolution and use of the Internet" — reaffirmed verbatim in A/RES/80/173 para. 88
- **Reports to:** [[cstd]] annually (A/RES/80/173, para. 100); outcomes also fed to [[ungis]], action line facilitators, and [[wsis-forum]]
- **GDC contribution:** IGF to contribute to GDC review at UNGA 82nd session (2027) (A/RES/80/173, para. 125)

## Detail

### Creation Context

When the Tunis Agenda was adopted in November 2005, Internet governance was the most contested issue of the entire WSIS process. The core tension:

- **Developing countries and some governments** (including China, Brazil, and the G77 generally) wanted greater intergovernmental control over the Internet's critical infrastructure — particularly ICANN's management of the root zone and IP addressing, which they saw as unduly controlled by the United States.
- **The US** issued a strong statement in June 2005 that it would maintain its role in Internet governance and would not support any proposal that would transfer control of core Internet functions to other entities.
- **The EU** proposed a compromise focused on enhanced international cooperation without a new oversight body.
- **Civil society and the technical community** (ISOC, IETF, ICANN, etc.) strongly opposed any move toward intergovernmental control.

The IGF was the compromise: a forum for open, multistakeholder discussion on Internet policy issues, but explicitly without oversight functions and without the ability to replace or override existing bodies. The "enhanced cooperation" process (paras. 69–71) was a separate, parallel concession that acknowledged governments' legitimate role in Internet public policy.

### IGF Mandate (Tunis Agenda para. 72)

The IGF is mandated to:

a) Discuss public policy issues related to key elements of Internet governance to foster sustainability, robustness, security, stability, and development of the Internet  
b) Facilitate discourse between bodies dealing with cross-cutting international public policies regarding the Internet  
c) Interface with appropriate intergovernmental organisations and other institutions on matters under their purview  
d) Facilitate exchange of information and best practices; make full use of expertise from academic, scientific, and technical communities  
e) Advise stakeholders on accelerating Internet availability and affordability in the developing world  
f) Strengthen and enhance engagement of stakeholders from developing countries in existing/future Internet governance mechanisms  
g) Identify emerging issues, bring them to attention of relevant bodies, make recommendations where appropriate  
h) Contribute to capacity building for Internet governance in developing countries  
i) Promote and assess embodiment of WSIS principles in Internet governance processes  
j) Discuss issues relating to critical Internet resources  
k) Help find solutions to issues arising from use and misuse of the Internet  
l) Publish its proceedings

### Key Limitations (para. 77)

The IGF explicitly:
- Has **no oversight function**
- Does **not replace** existing arrangements, mechanisms, institutions, or organisations
- Is **neutral, non-duplicative, and non-binding**
- Has **no involvement** in day-to-day or technical operations of the Internet

These limitations were essential to prevent the IGF from becoming a vehicle for transferring Internet control to an intergovernmental body.

### Annual Forum Structure

#### Governance: The MAG

The **Multistakeholder Advisory Group (MAG)** is the IGF's governing programme committee:
- **Composition:** 56 members representing governments, private sector, civil society, technical and academic communities from all world regions
- **Appointed by:** UN Secretary-General
- **Tenure:** Two-year terms; approximately one-third renewed annually to ensure continuity and fresh perspectives
- **Meets:** Three times per year in Geneva (plus intersessional work online)
- **Function:** Develops the programme for each annual IGF meeting; oversees thematic work streams; manages call for workshop proposals

The MAG model is constitutionally significant: unlike standard UN processes where governments have formal primacy and non-state actors are observers, all MAG members participate on an essentially equal footing — a structural expression of the multistakeholder model.

#### Session Format

Each annual IGF meeting runs for approximately five days and includes:
- **Main sessions:** High-level panels and plenaries on major themes; Chair's Summary issued at close
- **Open forums:** Space for international organisations, governments, and civil society coalitions to present positions
- **Workshops:** Submitted by any stakeholder and selected by competitive review; open to all
- **Dynamic coalitions:** Sustained working groups on specific issues (e.g., Internet and Human Rights, Net Neutrality, Internet Standards, Accessibility)
- **Best Practice Forums (BPFs):** Multi-year intersessional work producing substantive best practice guides
- **Policy Networks:** Structured intersessional work on emerging issues (e.g., AI, meaningful access, environment)
- **High-Level Leaders Track:** Ministerial-level engagement and political statements

#### Mandate Renewals

- **Initial mandate:** Five years (2006–2010), subject to review (Tunis Agenda para. 76)
- **2011 renewal:** Extended for five additional years (2011–2015); UNGA-established working group recommended improvements to working modalities
- **2015 renewal:** Extended for ten years (2016–2025) by UN General Assembly resolution
- **2025:** Made **permanent** by [[unga-res-80-173]] para. 99

### Key Themes Over the Years

The IGF's agenda has tracked the changing Internet landscape across six broad periods:

#### 2006–2008: Foundational Issues
- **Critical Internet resources** (IP addresses, root zone, DNS governance): The most contested topic — directly reflecting unresolved WSIS tensions
- **Internet access and connectivity:** Broadband rollout; developing country access gaps; affordable access
- **Multilingualism and internationalised domain names:** Access for non-Latin script communities
- **Spam and cybercrime:** Early security discussions
- **Openness and diversity:** Free expression and early net neutrality debates

#### 2009–2012: Maturing Agenda
- **Human rights online:** Free expression, privacy, censorship; galvanised by Arab Spring (2011)
- **Child online protection:** Safe environments for minors; harmful content
- **Cloud computing:** Data localisation; jurisdictional questions; sovereignty of cloud data
- **Internet exchange points:** Infrastructure sovereignty; IXPs for developing countries
- **WCIT-12 context:** Rising government pressure for expanded ITU Internet mandate (erupting at WCIT-12, December 2012)

#### 2013–2015: Post-Snowden and WSIS+10
- **Mass surveillance:** Snowden revelations (2013) made mass surveillance the dominant theme for multiple years; trust in the Internet shaken
- **Internet governance ecosystem reform:** NETmundial multistakeholder meeting (São Paulo, April 2014) produced the NETmundial Statement, feeding into IGF 2015
- **WSIS+10 alignment:** IGF mandate renewal as part of WSIS+10 review; relationship to WSIS outcome
- **Internet universality:** UNESCO's ROAM framework (Rights, Openness, Accessibility, Multistakeholder)
- **SDG alignment:** ICT for development; connecting the 4 billion unconnected

#### 2016–2019: Platform Era
- **Platform regulation:** Fake news, disinformation, content moderation; accountability of intermediaries
- **AI and emerging technologies:** First substantive AI governance sessions (2016 onward); algorithmic accountability
- **Data governance:** GDPR context; privacy as fundamental right; data flows and digital sovereignty
- **Cybersecurity:** State-sponsored attacks; norms for responsible state behaviour in cyberspace
- **Digital economy:** Taxation of digital platforms; gig economy; digital trade
- **Internet fragmentation:** Splinternet concerns; national firewalls; technical and policy fragmentation

#### 2020–2022: COVID-19 and Digital Reset
- **Pandemic response:** ICTs for health systems, education continuity, remote work, economic resilience
- **Universal access:** COVID-19 dramatically exposed the cost of the connectivity divide
- **Digital rights:** Contact-tracing apps; surveillance creep; vaccine certificate systems
- **Meaningful connectivity:** Beyond bare access to quality, reliability, and affordability
- **AI governance:** Accelerating AI deployment raises urgency of governance frameworks

#### 2023–2025: AI Dominance and GDC
- **AI governance:** Dominant theme across all IGF work streams — generative AI, AI safety, AI for development, AI standards
- **Global Digital Compact:** IGF discussions feeding into GDC negotiations; IGF as model for multistakeholder digital governance
- **WSIS+20 preparatory:** IGF outcome feeding into the WSIS+20 High-Level Review (December 2025)
- **Internet fragmentation:** Technical and policy fragmentation as central long-term challenge
- **Cybersecurity norms:** Towards internationally agreed standards of behaviour
- **IGF's own future:** Permanent status question resolved at WSIS+20 (December 2025)

### Relationship to WSIS Action Lines

The IGF contributes most directly to:
- **Action Line C6** (Enabling Environment): Internet governance discussions are the core C6 activity
- **Action Line C5** (Confidence and Security): Cybersecurity, spam, and online trust discussions
- **Action Line C4** (Capacity Building): IGF capacity-building activities for developing country participants
- **Action Line C8** (Cultural and Linguistic Diversity): Multilingualism and content diversity work

The IGF and the [[wsis-forum]] work in synergy — many stakeholders participate in both, and IGF discussions inform WSIS Forum deliberations. The WSIS+20 outcome (A/RES/80/173, para. 100) formalises this: the IGF is to report its outcomes to [[ungis]], action line facilitators, [[cstd]], and [[wsis-forum]].

### Relationship to WSIS Forum, UNGIS, CSTD, and GDC

#### IGF ↔ WSIS Forum
The IGF and [[wsis-forum]] are complementary, not competing:
- **WSIS Forum** (annual, [[itu]]-hosted, Geneva): Reviews implementation of [[wsis-action-lines]]; more institutional; UN-centric; outputs feed formal review processes
- **IGF** (annual, DESA-hosted, rotating cities): Open policy dialogue; more bottom-up; civil society and technical community on equal terms with governments; no formal output

#### IGF → UNGIS
IGF outcomes feed into [[ungis]] annually (A/RES/80/173, para. 100). UNGIS incorporates IGF findings into the WSIS-GDC implementation roadmap and cross-agency coordination. The IGF is not a UNGIS subsidiary and operates independently.

#### IGF → CSTD
The IGF reports annually to [[cstd]] — the ECOSOC subsidiary body whose secretariat is provided by [[unctad]]. The IGF Chair's Summary and MAG Chair report are standing items at each annual CSTD session. This is the formal intergovernmental accountability channel: the IGF discusses; the CSTD receives; ECOSOC transmits to UNGA. It allows the multistakeholder IGF to inform intergovernmental decisions without itself becoming intergovernmental.

#### IGF → GDC (2024 onward)
The [[global-digital-compact]] (September 2024) reaffirmed the multistakeholder model and the importance of the IGF. IGF discussions on AI governance, data, and digital rights fed directly into GDC negotiations. Post-adoption, A/RES/80/173 para. 125 directs the IGF to **contribute to the GDC review at UNGA 82nd session (2027)**.

### Enhanced Cooperation (Tunis Agenda paras. 69–71)

Parallel to the IGF, the Tunis Agenda called for an "enhanced cooperation" process to enable governments to carry out their roles in international public policy for the Internet — but not in day-to-day technical/operational matters. It was to begin by Q1 2006.

Enhanced cooperation proved far less impactful than the IGF. It generated reports and consultations through ITU's Council Working Group on Internet-related Public Policy Issues (CWG-Internet, established 2010) and other mechanisms, but produced no fundamental reform of Internet governance arrangements. The WSIS+20 resolution (A/RES/80/173, para. 91) still references enhanced cooperation, suggesting the question remains formally unresolved even as the IGF has effectively superseded it as the primary multilateral Internet dialogue space.

### The NRI Ecosystem

The IGF's most significant structural development over 20 years is the **National, Regional, and Youth IGF (NRI) ecosystem**:

- **Number:** 170+ NRIs as of 2025 (A/RES/80/173, para. 97)
- **Types:**
  - *National IGFs:* Country-level forums replicating the multistakeholder model (e.g., UK IGF, Kenya IGF, India IGF)
  - *Regional IGFs:* EuroDIG (Europe), APrIGF (Asia-Pacific), AfIGF (Africa), MENA-IGF (Middle East and North Africa), LACIGF (Latin America and Caribbean)
  - *Youth IGFs:* Dedicated spaces for young Internet governance practitioners
- **Function:** Bring Internet governance dialogue to local contexts; surface emerging issues and regional perspectives for the global forum
- **Relationship to global IGF:** Autonomous but affiliated; NRI coordinators participate in global IGF programme; NRI outputs are officially documented and feed the global agenda

The NRI ecosystem is arguably the IGF's most durable institutional legacy — creating communities of Internet governance practitioners in countries and regions that would otherwise have no structured domestic forum for such discussions.

A/RES/80/173 (para. 97) explicitly welcomes the evolution of the IGF into "a broader ecosystem encompassing intersessional and other activities, including dynamic coalitions, best practice forums and policy networks" alongside the 170+ NRIs.

### Twenty Years and Permanent Status (WSIS+20, 2025)

The IGF was initially designed for five years with a review mechanism. By the WSIS+20 process, it had run for nearly 20 years and established itself as an irreplaceable venue for multistakeholder Internet policy discussion.

**On 17 December 2025, the UNGA adopted [[unga-res-80-173]], which made the IGF a permanent forum of the United Nations** (para. 99):

> *"We decide that the Internet Governance Forum shall be made a permanent forum of the United Nations, with a continued secretariat hosted by the Department of Economic and Social Affairs of the Secretariat, on a stable and sustainable basis with appropriate staffing and resources."*

Additional WSIS+20 provisions:
- **Para. 97:** Welcomes evolution into the broader NRI/intersessional ecosystem; welcomes 170+ NRIs
- **Para. 98:** Calls for continued improvement in working modalities, broader developing-country participation, stronger relationships with other digital discussion forums; requests annual reporting to CSTD
- **Para. 100:** IGF to report outcomes to UNGIS, action line facilitators, CSTD, and WSIS Forum
- **Para. 101:** Enhance working modalities; establish and facilitate dialogue among governments with all stakeholders
- **Para. 103:** Calls for strengthening of IGF secretariat; Secretary-General invited to submit proposal for sustainable funding to the 80th UNGA session
- **Para. 125:** IGF to contribute to GDC review at UNGA 82nd session (2027)

## Connections
- Created by [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] (para. 72) as part of [[wsis-phase-ii-tunis-2005]]
- Made a **permanent forum of the United Nations** by [[unga-res-80-173]] (para. 99, December 2025)
- Reports annually to [[cstd]] (formal accountability channel to UNGA); also to [[ungis]], action line facilitators, [[wsis-forum]]
- Works in synergy with [[wsis-forum]] — shared stakeholder community, complementary agendas; IGF is bottom-up and multistakeholder, WSIS Forum is more institutional
- [[itu]] provides expertise and support; UN DESA hosts secretariat
- Mandate reviewed at [[wsis-review-2015]] (ten-year renewal) and [[wsis-review-2025]] (permanent status)
- Aligned with [[global-digital-compact]] (2024); IGF to contribute to GDC review at UNGA 82nd session (2027)
- Working definition of Internet governance (Tunis Agenda para. 34) reaffirmed verbatim in A/RES/80/173 para. 88 — unchanged after 20 years
- Institutional tension with [[itu]] over Internet governance model: ITU is intergovernmental and treaty-based; IGF is multistakeholder and non-binding
- 170+ NRIs form a global ecosystem replicating the multistakeholder model at national and regional levels

## Open Questions
- Permanent status granted, but sustainable funding was still a request to the 80th UNGA session (para. 103) — has the Secretary-General's proposal produced a stable long-term budget?
- The IGF has no formal output — now that it is permanent, will there be pressure to add decisional or quasi-binding functions?
- AI governance is now the dominant theme — how will the IGF coordinate with the Global Dialogue on AI Governance (2026) and the Independent International Scientific Panel on AI?
- The enhanced cooperation process (Tunis Agenda paras. 69–71) has largely stalled — A/RES/80/173 para. 91 still references it — is it functionally obsolete?
- With 170+ NRIs, how does the global IGF manage quality, avoid fragmentation, and ensure coherent upward feedback?
- The IGF is now permanent but the [[global-digital-compact]] also establishes new coordination mechanisms — does the GDC review process (2027) risk duplicating IGF functions or will the IGF become the GDC's primary implementation forum?

# United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP)

## Overview
The United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP) — also known as UN Environment — is the leading global authority on the environment within the UN system. Founded on **5 June 1972** at the UN Conference on the Human Environment (Stockholm), it is headquartered in **Nairobi, Kenya**. UNEP's Executive Director is **Inger Andersen** (appointed February 2019 by UN Secretary-General António Guterres).

Within the WSIS framework, UNEP's primary role is as co-facilitator of the **C7: E-Environment** sub-theme — using ICTs for environmental protection, sustainable resource management, and climate action. This makes UNEP a significant actor in linking the WSIS implementation machinery to the environmental dimension of the [[sdgs]] (particularly SDGs 12–15).

## Key Facts
- **Full name:** United Nations Environment Programme
- **Founded:** 5 June 1972 (Stockholm Conference)
- **Headquarters:** Nairobi, Kenya
- **First Executive Director:** Maurice Strong
- **Current Executive Director:** Inger Andersen (February 2019–present)
- **Governing Body:** UN Environment Assembly (UNEA), created 2012; 193 member states; meets biennially
- **Operating Divisions (8):** Science; Policy and Programme; Ecosystems; Economy; Governance Affairs; Law; Communication; Corporate Services
- **Funding:** Voluntary contributions via the Environmental Fund
- **WSIS role:** Co-facilitates **C7 E-Environment** alongside WHO, WMO, UN-Habitat, [[itu]], ICAO
  - Focal point: Magda Biesiada (UNEP)
- **Key environmental treaties hosted:** Convention on Biological Diversity; Montreal Protocol; Minamata Convention; CITES; Basel/Rotterdam/Stockholm Conventions; Regional Seas Programme (1974)

## Detail

### History and Mandate

UNEP was created in 1972 to provide leadership and encourage partnerships for environmental care by inspiring, informing, and enabling nations and peoples to improve their quality of life without compromising that of future generations. It is unique in the UN family: it is a Programme (not a specialised agency), headquartered in a developing country (Nairobi), and serves as the UN's primary environmental conscience.

Key functions:
- Setting the global environmental agenda
- Promoting coherent implementation of environmental dimensions of sustainable development
- Serving as an authoritative advocate for the global environment
- Developing international environmental law and agreements
- Providing scientific assessments (Global Environment Outlook)

### WSIS C7: E-Environment

UNEP co-facilitates the **E-Environment** sub-theme of [[wsis-action-lines]] C7, which covers:
- Using ICTs for environmental protection and monitoring
- ICT-enabled early warning systems for natural disasters
- E-waste and sustainable ICT production and disposal
- Climate modelling and earth observation via ICTs
- Digital tools for biodiversity monitoring
- ICT-supported sustainable consumption and production (SDG 12)

The E-Environment sub-theme was added renewed urgency by the 2015 Paris Agreement (climate action) and subsequent IPCC reports on climate emergency. ICT tools — from satellite earth observation to AI-based climate modelling — are central to environmental monitoring and response.

Co-facilitators of C7 E-Environment include: WHO, WMO (weather and climate data), UN-Habitat (urban sustainability), ITU (spectrum and standards), and ICAO (aviation emissions monitoring).

### UNEP and E-Waste

One of the most significant WSIS-UNEP intersections is the **e-waste** dimension. The WSIS+10 Statement (2014) identified e-waste as a major emerging challenge, noting that ICT devices contain toxic materials and their disposal in developing countries creates environmental and health hazards. UNEP, ITU, and other bodies have developed the Global E-waste Statistics Partnership to track volumes and inform policy.

### UNEP and UNGIS

UNEP is a member of [[ungis]], the UN Group on the Information Society, connecting its environmental mandate to the broader WSIS coordination structure.

### SDG Connections

UNEP's work is most directly relevant to:
- **SDG 12** (Responsible Consumption and Production) — sustainable ICT, e-waste
- **SDG 13** (Climate Action) — ICT for climate monitoring, early warning
- **SDG 14** (Life Below Water) — ocean monitoring via ICT
- **SDG 15** (Life on Land) — biodiversity monitoring, remote sensing

ICT is increasingly essential to all environmental SDGs as the data infrastructure for measurement, monitoring, and reporting.

# Digital Divide

## Overview
The digital divide refers to "unequal access to and use of digital technology," encompassing multiple interlocking dimensions: motivational access (the will and relevance), material access (devices, connections), skills access (digital literacy), and usage access (ability to benefit). First articulated as a policy concern in U.S. domestic debates in the 1990s, the concept went global through the WSIS process (2003–2005), which formalised an international shift from treating connectivity as a domestic infrastructure challenge to recognising it as a multi-dimensional global issue requiring multilateral action.

Closing the digital divide is the central goal of the WSIS process. The [[geneva-plan-of-action]] (2003) set ten connectivity targets for 2015; the WSIS Action Lines (especially C2 on infrastructure and C4 on capacity building) represent the operational framework for addressing it; and the WSIS-SDG Matrix links WSIS work to SDG 9.c (universal affordable internet) and numerous other goals. Yet twenty years after WSIS Geneva, approximately 2.6 billion people remain offline — predominantly in Least Developed Countries (LDCs), rural areas, and among women and girls.

## Key Facts
- **Global internet users (2023–24):** 67% of world population — approximately 5.4 billion people
- **Still offline:** ~2.6 billion people (concentrated in LDCs, rural areas, Africa)
- **Regional disparity:** Europe 89% penetration vs. Africa 37% penetration
- **Income disparity:** Low-income countries 26% penetration vs. high-income countries 92%
- **Gender gap globally:** Parity score 0.92 (target: 0.98–1.02); Africa's gender gap worst at 0.75
- **Speed disparity:** Downloading 5GB takes 8 minutes in Taiwan but ~30 hours in Yemen
- **SDG target:** SDG 9.c — universal and affordable internet access, especially in LDCs
- **WSIS response:** Action Lines C2 (infrastructure), C4 (capacity), C7 E-Employment, C11 (cooperation)
- **Key WSIS mechanisms:** WSIS Stocktaking, Partner2Connect, Giga initiative, Broadband Commission

## Detail

### Dimensions of the Digital Divide

**Three-Level Framework:**
Scholars (notably Jan van Dijk) identify three successive levels of digital divide:

1. **First-level divide — Physical/Material Access:** Does the individual have access to a device and internet connection? This is the original conception of the digital divide and remains the dominant policy focus (universal service, broadband deployment, spectrum policy).

2. **Second-level divide — Digital Literacy and Skills:** Even with physical access, users need skills to use digital tools effectively. This includes basic operational skills (using a browser), information literacy (evaluating online content), safety skills (protecting personal data), and creative/productive skills (creating content, writing code).

3. **Third-level divide — Conversion to Capital:** Can the individual translate their digital access and skills into socio-economic benefits — better jobs, better health outcomes, political participation, economic productivity? This most complex level recognises that access and skills are insufficient if social structures prevent conversion into real-world advantage.

**Four Dimensions (motivational, material, skills, usage):**
More granular frameworks add a motivational dimension: some individuals with physical access to devices choose not to use them — because of perceived irrelevance, safety concerns, language barriers, or disability. This suggests purely supply-side infrastructure investments are insufficient without demand-side measures (relevant content, local language, digital literacy campaigns).

### Gender Digital Divide

Gender is a persistent cross-cutting dimension of the digital divide:

- Women are less likely to use internet even when nominally available — the "usage gap" persists beyond the access gap
- In rural India (documented case): women with mobile phones often could only answer calls, lacking digital literacy to use other features
- Female representation in computing fell from 36% (1990) to 25% (2016), suggesting structural barriers persist even as overall computing grows
- OECD recommendations: targeted digital skills training; policies to promote female ICT careers; designing relevant online content that addresses women's interests and needs; safety measures to counter online gender-based violence

WSIS [[wsis-action-lines]] C4 (Capacity Building), C7 E-Employment, and C11 (International Cooperation) all address gender dimensions of the digital divide. WSIS+20 outcome [[unga-res-80-173]] (2025) includes gender equality as a crosscutting theme for all action lines (para. 112).

### Persistent Challenges

**Technology Waves and Inequality:**
Each successive technological innovation — broadband DSL, 3G, 4G, 5G, fiber — initially increases inequality before diffusion eventually narrows it. Countries and communities that cannot adopt each new technology face compound disadvantages as the frontier shifts.

**Adverse Digital Incorporation:**
One of the most important critiques of digital inclusion efforts is the concept of "adverse digital incorporation" — where inclusion in digital systems occurs under exploitative conditions that actually deepen existing inequality rather than reduce it. Examples include: gig economy platforms that extract value from workers with minimal benefits; digital lending apps with predatory interest rates targeting low-income borrowers; social media platforms monetising users' attention and data without commensurate benefit.

**Infrastructure Gaps in LDCs:**
Despite 20+ years of WSIS, the most underconnected populations remain in LDCs and fragile states, where physical infrastructure deployment faces challenges of geography, political instability, and financing gaps. The Giga initiative (ITU/UNICEF) specifically targets school connectivity; Partner2Connect (ITU) mobilises financing commitments.

### WSIS Response (2003–Present)

The World Summit on the Information Society directly responded to the digital divide as its core mandate:

**Geneva Phase (2003):**
The [[geneva-plan-of-action]] defined 10 connectivity targets for 2015: village connectivity, school connectivity, research centre and library connectivity, health facility connectivity, government connectivity, curriculum adaptation, traditional media (TV/radio) access, multilingual internet, and half the world online. These were aspirational — most were not achieved by 2015.

**Key WSIS Mechanisms:**
- **[[wsis-action-lines]] C2 (Infrastructure):** Focuses on universal access, broadband deployment, IXPs, spectrum, satellite, and rural connectivity
- **[[wsis-action-lines]] C4 (Capacity Building):** Digital literacy, teacher training, gender equity in education, ICT professional training
- **[[wsis-stocktaking]]:** ITU database of 19,000+ WSIS-related projects; WSIS Prizes recognise excellence
- **Broadband Commission:** ITU/UNESCO joint initiative; sets broadband connectivity targets
- **Partner2Connect:** ITU-led pledge platform for connectivity financing; mobilised billions in commitments
- **Giga initiative:** ITU/UNICEF programme to connect every school to the internet
- **[[digital-solidarity-fund]]:** Voluntary fund proposed at WSIS Geneva (2003) to finance digital development; its implementation has been weak

**WSIS+20 Assessment (2025):**
The [[wsis-review-2025]] outcome ([[unga-res-80-173]]) noted progress but recorded ~1/3 of the world still offline, with 74% global internet usage (up from ~15% in 2005). "Universal connectivity" was identified as the first of six priority areas for the review.

### Connection to SDGs and GDC

**SDGs:**
- **SDG 9.c:** "Significantly increase access to information and communications technology and strive to provide universal and affordable access to the internet in least developed countries by 2020" — the most directly WSIS-aligned SDG target
- **SDG 4** (Education), **SDG 5** (Gender equality), **SDG 8** (Economic growth), **SDG 10** (Reduced inequalities), **SDG 17** (Partnerships) all have strong digital divide dimensions
- The WSIS-SDG Matrix (2015) maps each Action Line to specific SDG targets; see [[wsis-sdg-matrix]]

**Global Digital Compact (2024):**
- [[global-digital-compact]] Objective 1: "Close digital divides and accelerate progress on the SDGs through safe, secure, inclusive and rights-respecting digitalisation" — directly addresses the digital divide as GDC's first objective
- GDC distinguishes between infrastructure access gaps and meaningful connectivity (quality, affordability, digital skills)
- The [[ungis-gdc-matrix]] maps GDC Objective 1 commitments to WSIS Action Lines C2 and C4

## Open Questions
- With 2.6 billion still offline in 2025, is the WSIS approach to the digital divide — primarily through voluntary coordination and non-binding targets — adequate?
- The concept of "adverse digital incorporation" challenges the basic premise that more digital access is always better — how should WSIS and SDG frameworks integrate this critique?
- AI is creating a new layer of digital divide (AI-skill divide, computational resource divide, data divide). Will existing WSIS structures adequately address this?
- Gender digital divide has barely improved in Africa despite two decades of WSIS commitments — what structural interventions have been tried and what is the evidence on what works?

# UNGA Resolution A/RES/80/173 — WSIS+20 Outcome Document

## Overview
Resolution A/RES/80/173 is the outcome document of the high-level meeting of the UN General Assembly on the overall review of the implementation of the outcomes of the World Summit on the Information Society (WSIS+20), adopted at the 68th plenary meeting of the General Assembly's 80th session on **17 December 2025** in New York. It is the most authoritative WSIS outcome document since UNGA Resolution A/RES/70/125 (2015) and the first comprehensive intergovernmental review of WSIS implementation after 20 years.

The resolution reaffirms all four foundational WSIS documents (Geneva Declaration, [[geneva-plan-of-action]], Tunis Commitment, [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]]) and asserts the continuing centrality of the Geneva Plan of Action Action Lines as the main implementation framework. It produces several landmark decisions: the [[internet-governance-forum]] is made a **permanent forum of the United Nations**; a next WSIS review is called for in **2035**; action line facilitators are requested to develop implementation roadmaps by 2027; and strong coherence provisions are established between WSIS and the [[global-digital-compact]].

The resolution is the culminating output of the [[wsis-review-2025]] preparatory process, which involved extensive consultations through the Commission on Science and Technology for Development (CSTD), the WSIS+20 Forum High-Level Events (2024 and 2025), the IGF (held in Norway, June 2025), and inputs from governments, UN agencies, civil society, and private sector.

## Key Facts
- **Resolution number:** A/RES/80/173
- **Full title:** Outcome document of the high-level meeting of the General Assembly on the overall review of the implementation of the outcomes of the World Summit on the Information Society
- **Adopted:** 17 December 2025 (68th plenary meeting, 80th UNGA session)
- **Distribution:** General, 19 December 2025
- **Adopted:** Without reference to a Main Committee (A/80/L.41)
- **Agenda item:** 15 — Information and communications technologies for sustainable development
- **Length:** 127 numbered paragraphs
- **Predecessor:** A/RES/70/125 (WSIS+10 outcome, December 2015)
- **Key landmark decision 1:** IGF declared a permanent forum of the United Nations (para. 99)
- **Key landmark decision 2:** WSIS+30 review requested for 2035 (para. 127)
- **Key landmark decision 3:** Action line facilitators to develop WSIS-SDG-GDC implementation roadmaps by CSTD 2027 (para. 113)
- **Key landmark decision 4:** UNGIS to prepare joint WSIS-GDC implementation roadmap for CSTD 2026 (para. 122)
- **Key landmark decision 5:** ITU to establish task force on financial mechanisms for digital development; report to CSTD 2027 (para. 67)
- **Biennial reviews:** Secretary-General requested to submit biennial WSIS progress reports (para. 124)

## 2025 Digital Statistics (as cited in the resolution)
- **Internet use:** 74% globally (2025), up from just over 15% (2005) — para. 20
- **4G coverage:** 93% of world population now lives in areas covered by 4G networks — para. 20
- **Mobile phone ownership:** 82% of people aged 10+ own a mobile phone — para. 20
- **High-income vs low-income countries:** 94% vs 23% Internet use — para. 21
- **Urban vs rural:** ~85% vs 58% Internet use — para. 21
- **Gender digital divide:** 71% of women vs ~77% of men use the Internet; 78% of women/girls 10+ vs 87% of men own a mobile phone — para. 22
- **Still offline:** Approximately one-third of the world's population — para. 16

## Detail

### Structure of the Resolution

The 127-paragraph resolution is structured in sixteen thematic sections, preceded by a two-paragraph formal preamble. The document is unusual for a UNGA resolution in that it was drafted as an intergovernmental "We" document — reflecting the high-level meeting format — rather than in the standard operative paragraph style.

### Introduction (paras. 1–14): Core Reaffirmations

**Foundational reaffirmations:**
- Para. 2: Reaffirms all four WSIS documents — Geneva Declaration of Principles, Geneva Plan of Action, Tunis Commitment, and Tunis Agenda for the Information Society
- Para. 3: Reaffirms multistakeholder cooperation as characterising the WSIS process since its inception
- Para. 4: Reaffirms sovereign equality of States; equitable and meaningful participation of all governments is crucial
- Para. 8: "The outcomes of the World Summit are anchored in international law, including the Charter of the United Nations and international human rights law"
- Para. 9: Universal connectivity and meaningful, affordable access to ICTs and the Internet are "fundamental to achieving the World Summit vision"

**New commitments in introduction:**
- Para. 11: Gender equality and women's empowerment — countering technology-facilitated violence; women's economic agency; digital skills and STEM
- Para. 13: "The pace and power of emerging technologies are creating new possibilities but also new risks for humanity, some of which are not yet fully known" — human oversight of technology required; human-centric approach throughout the life cycle of digital technologies
- Para. 14: Enabling environment must encompass policy, legal and regulatory frameworks that "protect consumer rights, nurture digital talent and skills, promote fair competition and digital entrepreneurship"

### ICTs for Development (paras. 15–18)

- Para. 17: **Digital public goods** defined as "open-source software, open data, open artificial intelligence models, open standards and open content that adhere to privacy and other applicable international laws, standards and best practices" — first formal WSIS-process definition
- Para. 18: **"We reaffirm the centrality of the World Summit action lines, as defined in the Geneva Plan of Action, as the main framework for translating the World Summit vision into concrete outcomes"** — the most important para. for continuity of the WSIS Action Lines framework

### Closing All Digital Divides (paras. 19–28)

- Para. 20–21: Detailed digital connectivity statistics (see Key Facts above)
- Para. 22: Gender digital divide statistics and commitment to address
- Para. 23: Persons with disabilities — "less likely to access the Internet"; calls for accessibility-by-design principles
- Para. 25: Digital divides are "multidimensional and widening" — extend beyond access to include affordability, language, ability, and technological capacity
- Para. 27: Commitment to achieving **affordable entry-level broadband subscription costs**; specific mention of universal service funds and community networks; reaffirms public access facilities (schools, post offices, libraries, hospitals)

### Digital Economy (paras. 29–34)

- Para. 29–31: Open, fair, non-discriminatory environment for digital development; equitable inclusion requires tackling concentrations of technological capacity and market power
- Para. 32: Digital financial services — Internet banking, mobile money, financial inclusion for previously excluded populations
- Para. 34: AI and automation expected to reshape labour market — "determined to ensure that these developments serve to complement and augment human labour"

### Social and Economic Development (paras. 35–41)

- Para. 37: **"We reaffirm our commitment to connecting every school to the Internet by 2030"** — strongest connectivity target in the resolution
- Para. 38: E-health commitments; "reaffirm our commitment to building on opportunities of ICTs to improve access to health, especially in developing countries"
- Para. 39: Cultural heritage preservation and access to cultural resources
- Para. 40: ICTs for disaster risk reduction, early warning systems

### Environmental Impacts (paras. 42–47)

A substantially new thematic section compared to WSIS 2003 outcomes:
- Para. 43: Growing energy and water consumption from digitalization; impacts on climate change from manufacture and use of digital devices, data centres, AI growth
- Para. 44: Sustainable use of critical minerals including scarce minerals required for digital equipment
- Para. 45: **E-waste** — "growing rapidly, particularly in developing countries, leading to pollution and risks to human health; rates of collection, recycling and recovery are low" — calls for improved collaboration on waste management
- Para. 47: Urges alignment of digital and environmental policies; promotes sustainability across the life cycle of digital technologies

### Enabling Environment (paras. 48–53)

- Para. 51: Technology transfer on mutually agreed terms; calls for ICT transfer policies for developing countries
- Para. 52: States strongly urged to **refrain from unilateral economic measures** not in accordance with international law — important sovereignty provision

### Building Confidence and Security (paras. 54–58)

- Para. 54: Cybersecurity building "should be consistent with international human rights law"
- Para. 56: Acknowledges the final report of the open-ended working group on ICT security 2021–2025 and the establishment of the **Global Mechanism on developments in ICTs in the context of international security**
- Para. 57: Counter all forms of violence "through or amplified by the use of technology"; cyberbullying; child sexual exploitation and abuse

### Capacity Development (paras. 59–61)

- Para. 59: Lack of capacity is a "major barrier to closing digital divides"; scale up financing for digital capacity development in developing countries
- Para. 61: Digital skills and lifelong learning; ability to identify reliable information; data protection and online security

### Financial Mechanisms (paras. 62–67)

- Para. 63: Promote enabling policy environment for investment; public-private cooperation for infrastructure, applications, services, content, and skills
- Para. 65: References Sevilla Commitment (Fourth International Conference on Financing for Development, 2025) — coordination of investment in digital public infrastructure
- **Para. 67:** ITU (as UNGIS secretariat), working with action line facilitators, to **establish an internal task force** to assess gaps and submit recommendations on strengthening financial mechanisms for digital development for developing countries; report to CSTD at its 30th session (2027); task force to also monitor financial commitments

### Human Rights and Ethical Dimensions (paras. 68–83)

- Para. 70: "The same rights that people have offline must also be protected online" — reaffirms UNGA resolution 78/213 (2023) on HR and digital technologies
- Para. 71: Private sector must ensure human rights throughout conception, design, development, deployment, operation, use, evaluation, and regulation of all digital technologies; Guiding Principles on Business and Human Rights apply
- Para. 73: Article 19 UDHR (freedom of expression) reaffirmed for digital context
- **Para. 74:** Privacy — "no one shall be subjected to arbitrary or unlawful interference" (Article 17 ICCPR); surveillance must comply with international human rights law (legality, necessity, proportionality); surveillance technology companies must respect human rights
- Para. 76: **Internet shutdowns** — "refraining from Internet shutdowns and measures that target Internet access" — strongest statement against Internet shutdowns in any WSIS outcome document
- Para. 77: Independence and diversity of media (including digital media); safeguarding journalists; conditions for journalism to operate freely
- Para. 78: Information integrity; misinformation and disinformation; hate speech online; democratic processes

**Data governance (paras. 81–83):** Notes approaches to data governance adopted in the GDC; notes establishment of CSTD working group on data governance for development; responsible and interoperable data governance essential

### Artificial Intelligence (paras. 84–87)

- Para. 84: Notes AI governance approaches adopted in the GDC — effectively cross-referencing GDC as the primary AI governance document
- Para. 85: Encourages AI capacity-building partnerships; access to open AI models, open training data, compute; participation of SMEs in digital economy
- **Para. 86:** Calls on Inter-Agency Working Group on AI to **map UN AI capacity-building initiatives** and establish an **AI capacity-building fellowship** for government officials with focus on developing countries; report to inaugural Global Dialogue on AI Governance (2026)
- **Para. 87:** Notes establishment of **Independent International Scientific Panel on AI** (multidisciplinary); welcomes **Global Dialogue on AI Governance** (involving governments and all stakeholders, on margins of relevant UN conferences)

### Internet Governance (paras. 88–103)

- Para. 88: Reaffirms working definition of Internet governance from [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] para. 34 — unchanged after 20 years
- Para. 90: Internet must be "open, global, interoperable, stable and secure"; reaffirm need to prevent fragmentation of the Internet
- Para. 91: Internet governance must continue to be "global and multi-stakeholder in nature"; should continue to follow WSIS Geneva and Tunis provisions "including in relation to enhanced cooperation"
- Para. 94: Notes **NETmundial+10 guidelines** (April 2024) for multistakeholder collaboration and consensus-building
- **Para. 99: "We decide that the Internet Governance Forum shall be made a permanent forum of the United Nations, with a continued secretariat hosted by the Department of Economic and Social Affairs of the Secretariat, on a stable and sustainable basis with appropriate staffing and resources, in accordance with United Nations budgetary procedures"** — LANDMARK DECISION ending the IGF's periodic mandate renewal cycle
- Para. 100: IGF to report outcomes to UN entities including UNGIS, action line facilitators, CSTD, and WSIS Forum
- Para. 103: Calls for strengthening IGF secretariat; invites Secretary-General to submit proposal to ensure **sustainable funding** during 80th UNGA session

### WSIS Framework Development (paras. 104–113)

- Para. 104: Tunis Agenda arrangements provide "a sound foundation for continued implementation" — with need to ensure continued relevance and coherence with GDC
- Para. 106: Calls for coherence between WSIS, 2030 Agenda, **Pact for the Future**, and **GDC** — "building synergies between them to ensure effective implementation and avoiding wasteful duplication"
- Para. 107: Commends [[itu]] on WSIS Stocktaking platform and WSIS Prizes; encourages all stakeholders to contribute to stocktaking database
- Para. 109: Notes establishment of **Office for Digital and Emerging Technologies (ODET)** to facilitate UN system-wide coordination on digital cooperation and GDC follow-up
- Para. 110: Acknowledges annual WSIS Forum; calls for it to **continue to be held on an annual basis**
- Para. 111: Reaffirms framework of action lines; action line facilitators to ensure close alignment with 2030 Agenda
- Para. 112: Human rights and gender equality as core themes within all action lines; Office of UNHCHR to contribute; UN-Women to be involved
- **Para. 113:** Requests action line facilitators (coordinating with UNGIS) to **develop targeted, results-oriented implementation roadmaps** for each action line, linking WSIS Action Lines with SDG targets and GDC commitments, including "potential targets, indicators and metrics to facilitate monitoring and measurement"; report to CSTD at its 30th session (2027)

### Monitoring and Measurement (paras. 114–118)

- Para. 115: Welcomes Partnership on Measuring ICT for Development
- Para. 117: Requests Partnership to conduct systematic review of existing indicators and methodologies; report to CSTD 2027
- Para. 116: Commitment to further development of internationally agreed targets, indicators, and metrics for universal, meaningful, affordable connectivity

### Follow-up and Review (paras. 119–127)

- Para. 119: "Biennial review of progress on the full set of Summit action lines" — institutionalising regular accountability
- Para. 120: "Ensure coherence between the implementation of World Summit outcomes and the Global Digital Compact to avoid duplication and enhance synergies"
- **Para. 122:** Requests UNGIS to prepare a **joint WSIS-GDC implementation road map** for CSTD 29th session (2026); road map should "strengthen coherence between WSIS and GDC, ensuring a unified, resource-efficient approach to digital cooperation"
- Para. 123: Recognises role of ECOSOC in overseeing WSIS follow-up; CSTD in reviewing progress
- Para. 124: Requests Secretary-General to submit **biennial report** on WSIS implementation through CSTD
- Para. 125: GDC review at UNGA 82nd session (2027)
- Para. 126: Present resolution to be submitted as input to the high-level meeting to review the 2030 Agenda (2030)
- **Para. 127: "We request the General Assembly to hold a high-level meeting on the overall review of the implementation of the outcomes of the World Summit on the Information Society in 2035"** — WSIS+30 formally called for

## Open Questions
- The IGF's permanent status is decided but sustainable funding is requested from the 80th UNGA session — how will this be resourced, and does "permanent" mean anything without a stable budget?
- The WSIS-GDC joint implementation roadmap was due to CSTD 2026 — has it been prepared on time, and does it resolve the duplication/coherence question?
- Action line facilitator roadmaps were due to CSTD 2027 — will these be genuinely "results-oriented" with measurable indicators, or aspirational documents?
- Para. 76's call to refrain from Internet shutdowns is the strongest WSIS statement on the issue — does it generate any state accountability?
- The ITU financial mechanisms task force has a 2027 deadline — what concrete recommendations has it produced?

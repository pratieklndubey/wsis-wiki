# C5: Building Confidence and Security in the Use of ICTs

## Overview
Action Line C5 addresses the trust and security dimensions of the Information Society — the conditions under which individuals, organisations, and governments can rely on digital systems without fear of attack, fraud, privacy violation, or manipulation. It is the Action Line most dramatically reshaped by events since 2003: cybersecurity threats that were nascent in the WSIS era have grown into a primary geopolitical and economic risk, transforming C5 from a secondary concern into one of the most operationally active WSIS Action Lines.

C5 is facilitated solely by [[itu]], reflecting ITU's central role in international cybersecurity standards, the Global Cybersecurity Index (GCI), and the ITU's development sector work on national cybersecurity strategies and CIRT (Computer Incident Response Team) deployment. No other UN agency co-facilitates C5, giving it the most focused facilitation structure after [[c2]].

The scope of C5 has expanded enormously from 2003 to 2025: from spam, cybercrime, and basic authentication in the original framework to AI-enabled threats, critical infrastructure protection, child online safety, state-sponsored cyber attacks, and the international regulatory debates around encryption and surveillance. C5 sits at the intersection of technical, legal, and human rights concerns — privacy protection, for example, is both a security issue and a fundamental right.

## Key Facts
- **Full name:** Building Confidence and Security in the Use of ICTs
- **Code:** C5 (Action Line 5)
- **Origin:** [[geneva-plan-of-action]], Section C, para. C5 (adopted 12 December 2003)
- **Facilitating organisation:** [[itu]] (sole UN facilitator)

- **Facilitation meetings:** 16 (2006–2021), held annually at [[wsis-forum]]

## Detail

### Scope and Purpose

C5 addresses eight dimensions of digital confidence and security:

**1. Cybersecurity cooperation:** International coordination to address cyber threats that cross borders; information sharing between national CIRTs; ITU's role as coordinator. The Budapest Convention on Cybercrime and UN GGE processes operate alongside but not inside the WSIS framework.

**2. Privacy and data protection:** Rights of individuals over their personal data; data protection legislation; accountability for data handlers. This dimension has grown massively in scope since the GDPR (2018) and subsequent data protection laws globally.

**3. Anti-spam:** Coordinating international responses to unsolicited commercial email; harmonising spam laws; technical measures (DKIM, SPF). Largely addressed technically by 2015, spam remains but is better managed.

**4. Cybercrime prevention:** National legislation on cybercrime; capacity building for law enforcement; international mutual legal assistance. ITU's cybercrime toolkit and model laws support developing-country legislation.

**5. Authentication and secure applications:** Digital signatures, PKI infrastructure, secure e-government systems, secure financial transactions — the technical security layer for e-commerce and e-government.

**6. Network integrity:** Security of telecommunications networks; protection of critical infrastructure (power, water, finance, health); resilience against attacks.

**7. Child online protection:** Safeguarding children from online harms — exploitation, grooming, cyberbullying. ITU's Child Online Protection (COP) initiative (launched 2008) is a major implementation vehicle.

**8. Incident response:** Real-time coordination for cyber incidents; national CIRT development; ITU-IMPACT partnership for incident response support.

### Key Geneva Plan of Action Provisions

The C5 section called for:
- Building confidence and trust in the use of ICTs through cybersecurity cooperation
- Preventing cybercrime through appropriate measures and law enforcement capacity
- Protecting privacy through appropriate national legislation and technical measures
- Countering spam through technical, legislative, and self-regulatory approaches
- Ensuring network integrity through certification and authentication systems
- Exchanging best practices for cybersecurity and incident response

### Evolution Since 2003: A Transformed Action Line

C5's scope has been transformed by the cyber threat landscape:

**2003–2010:** Basic cybersecurity, spam, and cybercrime focus. ITU developed its cybersecurity agenda; Budapest Convention entered force.

**2011–2015:** APT (Advanced Persistent Threat) attacks; state-sponsored cyber operations; Snowden revelations (2013) and the surveillance debate; critical infrastructure protection becomes prominent.

**2016–2020:** Ransomware (WannaCry, NotPetya); influence operations and disinformation; misinformation vs. cybersecurity convergence; 5G security; cybersecurity of IoT devices; GDPR and the global data protection wave.

**2021–2025:** AI-enabled threats (deepfakes, AI-powered phishing); quantum computing and cryptographic vulnerability; supply chain attacks (SolarWinds); satellite and space infrastructure security; geopolitical fragmentation of cyberspace.

### SDG Connections

C5's security focus connects to SDGs through trust-enabling development:
- **SDG 16.2** (Protection of children from violence): Child online safety
- **SDG 9.1, 9.c** (Reliable infrastructure; ICT access): Secure networks as prerequisite for trusted digital services
- **SDG 17.8** (Full operationalization of technology bank and STI capacity): Cybersecurity capacity for developing countries
- **SDG 8.1** (Economic growth): Cyber attacks impose significant economic costs; security enables digital economy
- **SDG 5.b** (Women's technology empowerment): Online harassment and gendered cyber threats as barriers

### WSIS+20 Mandate (2025)

[[unga-res-80-173]] (December 2025) addressed cybersecurity prominently:
- **Para. 18:** Reaffirmed C5 and all Action Lines as "the main framework"
- **Para. 68–75** (Cybersecurity section): Called for enhanced international cybersecurity cooperation; welcomed ITU's Global Cybersecurity Index; called for national cybersecurity strategies with human rights safeguards; recognised AI-enabled cyber threats as a growing concern; called for capacity building for developing-country CIRTs
- **Para. 76** (Internet shutdowns): Called on all stakeholders to refrain from "Internet shutdowns and measures that target Internet access" — the strongest anti-shutdown language in any WSIS document; relevant to C5 as shutdowns are often justified on security grounds
- **Para. 113:** C5 facilitators to develop implementation roadmap by CSTD 2027

### Facilitation Meeting History

16 facilitation meetings were held under C5 (2006–2021), all facilitated by [[itu]]. The meetings have been among the most technically substantive of the WSIS facilitation process, attracting participants from national CIRTs, telecommunications regulators, law enforcement, and the private sector. ITU's Global Cybersecurity Index (published periodically) serves as the primary indicator framework for C5 implementation tracking.

## Connections
- Defined in [[geneva-plan-of-action]] Section C (2003); facilitator confirmed in [[tunis-agenda-for-the-information-society]] Annex (2005)
- [[itu]] as sole UN facilitator — unified cybersecurity technical authority
- Linked to [[c6]] (Enabling Environment) — legal and regulatory frameworks for cybersecurity overlap with C6's enabling environment work
- Linked to [[c10]] (Ethical Dimensions) — privacy and data protection are both security and ethics issues
- Linked to [[c7]] applications — each e-application sub-theme has security requirements (e-health data privacy, e-government authentication, e-business transaction security)
- ITU's Child Online Protection (COP) initiative directly implements C5 child safety provisions
- ITU Global Cybersecurity Index (GCI) is the principal measurement tool
- Facilitation meetings held annually at [[wsis-forum]]; coordination through [[ungis]]

## Open Questions
- C5's WSIS framing predates both the Snowden revelations and the emergence of AI-enabled threats — is the Action Line framework flexible enough to address state surveillance, mass data collection, and disinformation?
- The para. 76 internet shutdown provision is unprecedented in WSIS outcomes — how will it be implemented given that many UN member states conduct shutdowns for stated security reasons?
- Should C5 be formally connected to the UN GGE/OEWG processes on cybersecurity norms, or do those processes have a different institutional logic?
- How does C5 address the tension between cybersecurity (which can justify encryption restrictions) and privacy (which depends on strong encryption)?
